<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 1 · Premise</p>
## Where MLA leaves room

[MLA](/llm-arch-kb/attention/mla/) cut the KV cache 5–7× below MHA. At very long contexts —
DeepSeek targets 128K tokens at deployment — even the latent cache is large enough that
attention compute, not memory, becomes the bottleneck. Every query token at position $t$ still
attends to all $t$ historical keys, even though only a small fraction of them carry
information relevant to the current decoding step.

The natural next move is **token-level sparse attention** — restrict each query's $O(L)$ key
budget to a learned $O(K)$ subset, with $K \ll L$. The hard part is choosing the subset
without paying $O(L)$ to score the keys.

DSA's framing: spend a small budget on a *cheap* indexer that approximates the relevance
score, then spend the full attention budget only on the indexer's top-K picks.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 2 · The two stages</p>
## Lightning Indexer + selected attention

A DSA layer runs two attention-shaped operations per query token:

**Stage 1 — Lightning Indexer.** A separate, much smaller attention-like score:

$$s_t(k) = \mathrm{LightningIndexer}(\mathbf{q}_t, \mathbf{k}_k)\quad \text{for all } k \le t.$$

The indexer uses its own small $\mathbf{Q}_I, \mathbf{K}_I$ projections with a much narrower
hidden dim than the main attention. Compute is $O(L)$ per query, but with a small constant —
the paper reports this stage at a fraction of full-MLA-attention cost.

**Stage 2 — Selected attention over top-K keys.** Take the indexer's top-K scores; gather the
corresponding latent KV slots; run full MLA attention over only those K slots:

$$\mathrm{TopK}_t = \operatorname{argtopk}_k\, s_t(k),\quad \text{output}_t = \mathrm{MLA}\bigl(\mathbf{q}_t,\, \{\mathbf{c}^{KV}_k\}_{k \in \mathrm{TopK}_t}\bigr).$$

The expensive operations (full-rank $W_Q W_{UK}$ inner products, the softmax over selected
keys) run only over $K$ entries. End-to-end, the per-token attention cost shifts from
$O(L \cdot d)$ to $O(L \cdot d_I + K \cdot d)$ where $d_I \ll d$ is the indexer dim and
$K \ll L$ is the top-K budget.

```python
# Per query token at position t
scores  = lightning_indexer(Q_I[t], K_I[:t])   # O(t * d_I)
top_idx = topk(scores, K)                       # K << t
out     = mla_attention(q[t], c_kv[top_idx])    # O(K * d)
```

The latent KV cache itself is unchanged — DSA piggybacks on MLA's compression. What's new is
the **selection** layer that decides which cached entries each query gets to see.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 3 · How the indexer is trained</p>
## A distillation target

The indexer needs to score keys in the same order the full attention would prefer — otherwise
the top-K subset misses the truly relevant tokens and quality collapses. DSA trains the
indexer with a distillation loss against the full-attention scores from a frozen MLA teacher:

$$\mathcal{L}_{I} = \sum_t \mathrm{KL}\bigl(\mathrm{softmax}(s_t),\, \mathrm{softmax}(\mathrm{MLA\text{-}scores}_t)\bigr).$$

The teacher attention pattern is computed on a sample of training examples; the indexer learns
to match it. The combined model trains end-to-end with the indexer loss as an auxiliary term
alongside the main language modeling loss.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 4 · Why it builds on MLA, not GQA</p>
## Latent compression matters

DSA's selection happens in the cached representation: the indexer scores against
$\mathbf{c}^{KV}_k$ slots, and the gathered K, V are reconstructed from the same latents. With
GQA's per-group K, V cache, gather operations would have to load full per-head vectors per
selected token — losing much of the bandwidth advantage. MLA's narrow latent makes the gather
cheap, which is what makes DSA practical.

The combination — MLA for cache compression, DSA for compute compression on top — is the
DeepSeek-V3.2 long-context efficiency story. The same V3 base model with DSA enabled at
inference time reportedly trades a small quality drop for a substantial speedup at 128K
context (V3.2-Exp tech report, §4).

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 5 · Status</p>
## Experimental, but production-grade engineering

V3.2 is labeled "Exp" because the team treats it as an experimental release rather than the
mainline V4. The DSA approach is novel enough that long-term behavior (especially at extreme
contexts beyond 128K, and on retrieval-heavy tasks where the indexer's top-K must include
sparse needle-in-haystack tokens) is still being studied.

For the architecture DSA depends on, see [MLA](/llm-arch-kb/attention/mla/) and the
[decoupled RoPE](/llm-arch-kb/positional/decoupled-rope/) head.

<Summary>
**DSA in one breath.** A cheap Lightning Indexer scores all historical keys against the
current query; full MLA attention runs only over the top-K. Trains the indexer to match the
full-attention pattern via distillation. Built on top of MLA's compressed latent cache —
DSA brings the *compute* down where MLA brought the *cache* down. First production: DeepSeek
V3.2-Exp.
</Summary>
