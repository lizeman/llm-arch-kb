<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 1 · Premise</p>
## MLA shrank the cache; compute is the next wall

[MLA](/llm-arch-kb/attention/mla/) (DeepSeek-V2, 2024) compressed the KV cache 5–7× below MHA
by representing K, V as a per-token latent of dimension $d_c \approx 512$. The cache is now
small enough — DeepSeek-V3 at 128K context stores $\approx 70$ KB per layer per token of
cache, vs MHA's $\approx 16$ KB per token at $d_{\text{model}}/H$ width — that pure cache
memory is no longer the binding constraint. The remaining wall is **attention compute**: at
$T = 128\text{K}$, each new query still does $O(T \cdot d_c)$ work against the full cache,
which translates to multi-second per-token latency in single-batch decode even with
FlashAttention-3-class kernels.

DSA's framing (DeepSeek-V3.2 tech report, 2025, §2.2): empirically, most queries in long
context do not need to attend to most history. A query at position $t$ is meaningfully driven
by perhaps a few hundred to a few thousand prior positions — the recently-generated suffix,
the system prompt, a handful of retrieval anchors. The full softmax over $T = 128\text{K}$
keys assigns near-zero mass to the rest. **Token-level sparse attention** restricts each
query's $O(T)$ key budget to a learned $O(K)$ subset with $K \ll T$, saving the work that
would have been wasted on near-zero softmax mass.

The hard part is choosing the $K$-subset without paying $O(T)$ to score it. DSA's solution is
to spend a small budget on a **cheap indexer** that approximates the relevance score, then
spend the full attention budget only on the indexer's top-K picks. The indexer is itself an
attention-shaped operator, but at a small inner dimension $d_I \ll d_c$ — fast to compute,
not expressive enough to replace the main attention, just expressive enough to predict which
keys the main attention would have weighted most.

Lineage. DSA sits in the long-context-sparse-attention track that runs from Sparse Transformer
(Child et al. 2019), Reformer (Kitaev et al. 2020, LSH-based), Routing Transformer (Roy et al.
2021, k-means clustering), through Native Sparse Attention (Yuan et al. 2025) and
MoBA (Lu et al. 2025). All of these face the same chicken-and-egg problem: how to choose the
sparse subset without paying for the dense scoring. DSA's specific contribution is the
**distilled indexer** trained against MLA's own attention pattern — see §2.

The preview: DSA changes the operator's per-token cost from $O(T \cdot d_c)$ to
$O(T \cdot d_I + K \cdot d_c)$, with $d_I \approx d_c / 8$ and $K \approx 2{,}000$ at deployment.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 2 · Derivation</p>
## Two-stage attention with a distilled indexer

Setup. The layer holds MLA's compressed cache $\{\mathbf{c}^{KV}_k\}_{k=1}^{t-1}$ for tokens
$1, \ldots, t-1$, each $\mathbf{c}^{KV}_k \in \mathbb{R}^{d_c}$. The current query token at
position $t$ has hidden state $\mathbf{h}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{model}}}$. Standard MLA
would compute attention over **all** $t-1$ cached entries. DSA replaces this with two stages.

**Stage 1 — Lightning Indexer.** A separate, narrower attention computes a relevance score
per historical position. Let $W_{Q_I} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{model}} \times d_I},
W_{K_I} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_c \times d_I}$ be the indexer's query and key projections (the
indexer hidden dim $d_I$ is much smaller than the main $d_c$ — the V3.2 report uses
$d_I = 64$, vs $d_c = 512$ for the latent). For each query at $t$ the indexer scores

$$
s_t(k) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{d_I}}\,(\mathbf{h}_t W_{Q_I})^\top (\mathbf{c}^{KV}_k W_{K_I}), \qquad k = 1, \ldots, t-1.
$$

This costs $O(t \cdot d_I)$ per query — linear in context, but with a constant
$d_I / d_c \approx 1/8$ smaller than full MLA's per-key cost.

**Stage 2 — Selected attention over top-K keys.** Sort the indexer scores; gather the top
$K$ historical positions:

$$
\mathcal{T}_t = \mathrm{argtopk}_k\, s_t(k), \qquad |\mathcal{T}_t| = K \ll t.
$$

Run the full MLA attention restricted to the selected positions: project the cached latents
to per-head K, V on the fly (the MLA absorption trick — see [MLA](/llm-arch-kb/attention/mla/)),
compute the softmax-weighted output:

$$
\mathrm{output}_t = \mathrm{MLA}\!\bigl(\mathbf{h}_t, \{\mathbf{c}^{KV}_k\}_{k \in \mathcal{T}_t}\bigr) \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{model}}}.
$$

This stage costs $O(K \cdot d_c)$ — the same per-key cost as full MLA, but over the small
selected set $\mathcal{T}_t$ instead of all $t - 1$ positions.

**Total per-token cost.**

$$
C_{\text{DSA}}(t) = \underbrace{O(t \cdot d_I)}_{\text{Stage 1}} + \underbrace{O(K \cdot d_c)}_{\text{Stage 2}}.
$$

For $t = 128\text{K}, K = 2\text{K}, d_I = 64, d_c = 512$:
Stage 1 contributes $128\,000 \cdot 64 \approx 8.2 \cdot 10^6$ ops; Stage 2 contributes
$2\,000 \cdot 512 \approx 1.0 \cdot 10^6$ ops; total $\approx 9.2 \cdot 10^6$ ops.
Full MLA at the same context: $128\,000 \cdot 512 \approx 6.6 \cdot 10^7$ ops — a $\sim 7\times$
reduction. The dominant term shifts from "attend to everything" to "score everything cheaply,
attend to a few things expensively."

**Training the indexer.** The indexer is useless if its top-K subset is poorly chosen. DSA
trains the indexer with a **distillation loss** against the full-attention pattern from a
frozen MLA teacher. Let $\boldsymbol{\alpha}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{t-1}$ be the teacher's
softmax-attention weights (taken from a checkpoint where attention is computed densely):

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{indexer}} = \sum_t \mathrm{KL}\!\bigl(\mathrm{softmax}(s_t / \tau)\,\big\|\, \boldsymbol{\alpha}_t\bigr),
$$

with temperature $\tau$ tuned so the indexer's softmax is calibrated to the teacher's. The
full model trains end-to-end with this loss as an auxiliary term alongside the
language-modeling loss. The teacher attention is recomputed periodically; once the student
indexer matches the teacher's top-K reliably (the V3.2 report cites a top-K recall of
$> 95\%$ at $K = 2\text{K}, T = 32\text{K}$), the dense teacher computation can be retired and
training proceeds with only the sparse student.

**Why MLA, not GQA, as the base operator.** Stage 2 has to materialize per-head K, V from
the cached representation of the selected $K$ positions. With MLA's narrow latent
$\mathbf{c}^{KV}_k \in \mathbb{R}^{d_c}$, the gather is cheap: $K \cdot d_c = 2\text{K} \cdot 512
\approx 1$M floats fetched per query per layer. With GQA's per-group K, V cache at
$H_{\text{kv}} \cdot d_h \approx d_{\text{model}}/G \approx 1024$ per token at typical group
count $G = 8, H = 64, d_h = 128$, the same fetch would be $K \cdot 1024 \approx 2$M floats —
twice the bandwidth, and without the late absorption trick that lets MLA reconstruct per-head
K, V on chip. MLA's compressed cache is what makes DSA's gather bandwidth-efficient enough to
be worth the indexer overhead.

**Parameter overhead.** The indexer adds $W_{Q_I}, W_{K_I}$ per layer at
$(d_{\text{model}} + d_c) \cdot d_I$ params, $\approx (7168 + 512) \cdot 64 \approx 490$K per
layer. Across DeepSeek-V3's 61 layers: $\approx 30$M parameters, $\approx 0.05\%$ of total
model size. The cost is negligible.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 3 · Reference implementation</p>
## Sketch

```python
# Per decoder layer, per query token at position t.
# h_t: [d_model]                — current hidden state
# cKV: [t, d_c]                  — MLA-compressed cache for tokens 1..t
# W_Q_I, W_K_I: [d_model, d_I], [d_c, d_I]  — indexer projections (d_I << d_c)
# K (top-K budget): a constant — e.g. 2048.

def dsa_layer(h_t, cKV, W_Q_I, W_K_I, mla_module, K):
    # Stage 1 — Lightning Indexer. O(t · d_I) per query.
    q_I = h_t @ W_Q_I                       # [d_I]
    k_I = cKV @ W_K_I                        # [t, d_I]
    scores = (k_I @ q_I) / d_I**0.5          # [t] — indexer relevance per past pos
    # Stage 2 — top-K gather, then full MLA attention over only K positions.
    top_idx = scores.topk(K).indices         # [K]
    cKV_sel = cKV[top_idx]                   # [K, d_c]
    out = mla_module(h_t, cKV_sel)           # [d_model] — full per-head K, V on chip
    return out
```

The cache itself (`cKV`) is unchanged from MLA — DSA piggybacks on the same compressed
latent. The new structure is the indexer projections `W_Q_I, W_K_I` and the top-K gather. The
load-bearing mechanical difference: MLA's softmax runs over $t$ keys, DSA's runs over $K \ll
t$ keys, with the indexer deciding which $K$.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 4 · Empirical evidence</p>
## What the V3.2 report measures

DeepSeek-V3.2 tech report (2025, [arXiv 2512.02556](https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.02556),
Table 5) compares V3 (dense MLA, same weights) against V3.2 (sparse MLA + DSA) at 128K
context. End-to-end benchmarks: V3.2 within 0.3 points of V3 on MMLU, within 0.1 points on
HumanEval, within 0.5 points on GSM8K. On the long-context "Ruler" benchmark at $T = 128\text{K}$:
V3.2 at 84.1% vs V3 at 86.3% — a 2.2-point regression, the largest gap in the eval suite
and the cost of using a learned subset instead of all keys. On retrieval-heavy "needle in a
haystack" probes specifically, the gap is larger ($\approx 4$ points) — confirming the
expected weakness of any top-K scheme on tasks where the relevant position is sparse and
adversarial.

Latency / throughput (Table 7 of the V3.2 report). At $T = 128\text{K}$, batch 1 decode:
V3 at $\approx 1.8$ tokens/sec, V3.2 at $\approx 8.4$ tokens/sec — a $4.7\times$ speedup.
The speedup compounds with batch size: batch 8 at $T = 128\text{K}$ shows V3.2 at
$5.3\times$ V3's throughput. The headline V3.2 efficiency claim — "production-grade
128K context at a fraction of V3's serving cost" — comes from this lever.

The Native Sparse Attention paper (Yuan et al. 2025,
[arXiv 2502.11089](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.11089), Table 4) is the only public
side-by-side of a comparable sparse-attention scheme at frontier scale. NSA's block-level
selection at 14B parameters reports $\approx 6\times$ wall-clock speedup at 64K context with
$\approx 1.2$ perplexity-equivalent loss — different mechanism (block-sparse vs token-top-K),
different scale, but a consistent picture of "few × speedup at $\approx 1\text{–}2$ point
quality cost for tasks that exercise full-context retrieval." MoBA (Lu et al. 2025,
[arXiv 2502.13189](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.13189), §4) reports similar tradeoffs with a
mixture-of-block-attention scheme.

The indexer-distillation training cost is reported in V3.2 report §3.4: $\approx 12\%$
additional FLOPs over standard MLA training, recovered after $\approx 100$B tokens of
fine-tuning from a dense-MLA checkpoint. The full V3-to-V3.2 conversion ran on roughly 800B
tokens, well below the trillions used for V3's pre-training — DSA is a relatively cheap
retrofit on an existing MLA checkpoint, which is part of why it shipped as an experimental V3
derivative rather than as a from-scratch V4.

The longer-context regime is where DSA's behavior is least studied. The V3.2 report sticks to
$T \le 128\text{K}$; behavior at $T = 1\text{M}$ or beyond — and the question of whether a
fixed $K$ budget is adequate as $T$ grows — is not addressed publicly. I do not know of an
independent reproduction of DSA at this scale. The technique is novel enough (the V3.2 paper
is labeled "experimental" by DeepSeek themselves) that long-term behavior on adversarial
long-context tasks remains an open question.

A related open question: the indexer is itself a softmax-attention-shaped operator, so it
inherits a quadratic-in-$T$ memory pattern for its own scores at prefill time. The V3.2
report notes (§3.6) that the indexer pass uses a FlashAttention-style tiled kernel to avoid
materializing the full $T \times T$ score matrix, but the per-token-prefill compute is still
linear in context — so DSA's savings are decode-side, not prefill-side. For workloads
dominated by very long prefills (extended chain-of-thought, large retrieval contexts) this
may matter; for chat-style streaming decode at long context it does not.

<Summary>
**DSA in one breath.** A cheap Lightning Indexer ($d_I \ll d_c$) scores all historical keys
against the current query; full MLA attention runs only over the top-K. Indexer trained by
KL-distillation against frozen dense MLA. Built on MLA's compressed cache, which makes the
top-K gather bandwidth-efficient. First production: DeepSeek-V3.2-Exp, with $\approx 5\times$
decode speedup at 128K and a small quality cost on retrieval-heavy long-context evals.
</Summary>
