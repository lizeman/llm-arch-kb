<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 1 · Premise</p>
## Attention is bottlenecked on HBM bandwidth, not FLOPs

A standard attention forward pass at sequence length $N$ and head dimension $d$ computes
$S = Q K^\top / \sqrt{d}$, then $P = \mathrm{softmax}(S)$, then $O = P V$. Both $S$ and $P$
are $N \times N$ matrices. On an A100 GPU, the two main memory tiers are HBM (40–80 GB,
$\approx 1.5$ TB/s bandwidth) and per-SM SRAM ($\approx 192$ KB on A100, $\approx 19$ TB/s).
The standard implementation materializes $S$ and $P$ to HBM, then reads them back to compute
$O$ — three round trips of $N^2$ floats each.

Dao et al. (2022, §1) measure the arithmetic intensity for typical 2022 transformer attention
at $N \in \{1\text{K}, 4\text{K}, 16\text{K}\}, d = 64$ and find it firmly in the
memory-bound regime: the GPU spends 60–80% of wall-clock on HBM reads of $S, P$, and only
20–40% on the actual matmul FLOPs that compute them. The PyTorch reference implementation in
2022 ran at roughly 5% of peak compute throughput on long-sequence attention.

The conventional optimization target for slow attention had been the $O(N^2)$ FLOP count: the
sparse-attention lineage (Sparse Transformer, Longformer, BigBird) and the linear-attention
lineage (Performer, Linformer, [Linear Attention](/llm-arch-kb/attention/linear-attention/))
both replace the dense softmax with something cheaper. Dao et al.'s reframing: the FLOPs are
fine, the bytes are the problem. Quadratic memory traffic, not quadratic compute, is the wall
attention training hit in practice.

The technique below is exact — it returns bit-identical outputs to standard attention up to
floating-point reduction order — and the resulting kernel underlies essentially every
production transformer trainer in 2024–26. It is the rare optimization that frontier models
adopt without any quality trade. The lineage from FlashAttention runs through FlashAttention-2
(Dao 2023, sequence-axis parallelism) to FlashAttention-3 (Shah, Dao et al. 2024,
H100-specific async tensor-memory), each of which extracts another constant factor.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 2 · Derivation</p>
## Tiling + online softmax + recomputation

Setup. Let $Q, K, V \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d}$ live in HBM. Choose block sizes
$B_r$ (rows of $Q$ per tile) and $B_c$ (columns of $K, V$ per tile) such that
$4 \cdot B_r \cdot d + B_r \cdot B_c$ floats fit in SRAM — the per-tile Q-block,
K-block, V-block, output accumulator, and score scratch must all be resident on-chip
simultaneously. On A100 with $d = 64$, the paper picks $B_r = B_c = 128$ for $\approx 80$ KB
of SRAM, leaving room for register pressure and double-buffering.

The naive softmax — compute $S$, subtract max, exponentiate, divide by sum — cannot run
tile-by-tile because the row-wise softmax denominator depends on every column of $S$.
FlashAttention solves this with the **online softmax** of Milakov & Gimelshein (2018,
[arXiv 1805.02867](https://arxiv.org/abs/1805.02867)). For a row of $S$ split into two
partial blocks with maxima $m_1, m_2$ and denominators $\ell_1 = \sum_j e^{S_{1,j} - m_1},
\ell_2 = \sum_j e^{S_{2,j} - m_2}$, the merged statistics are

$$m = \max(m_1, m_2),\qquad \ell = e^{m_1 - m}\,\ell_1 + e^{m_2 - m}\,\ell_2.$$

Why this and not "compute the row max separately, then softmax"? Because that requires
two passes over $K$ — one for the max, one for the exponentiation — doubling the HBM
read traffic. The online form folds both into a single pass that updates running statistics
as new $K, V$ tiles stream in.

Output accumulation uses a corresponding rescale. With running output $O_1$ accumulated under
local max $m_1$, the merged output after seeing block 2 is

$$O = e^{m_1 - m}\,\frac{\ell_1}{\ell}\,O_1 + e^{m_2 - m}\,\frac{\ell_2}{\ell}\,O_2.$$

The factor $e^{m_1 - m}$ corrects the earlier output for the new global max; the $\ell_1/\ell$
re-weights it by the new denominator. The algebra reduces to a one-line update: each time a
new K-tile arrives, the accumulator gets rescaled in place before the new tile's softmax-times-V
gets added.

The final per-row attention output written to HBM is the standard softmax-weighted average,
read out of the running statistics after the full inner-loop pass:

$$O_i = \frac{1}{\ell_i}\,\sum_{j=1}^N e^{S_{ij} - m_i}\,V_j,$$

which equals $\mathrm{softmax}(S_i)\,V$ exactly — the online merge accumulates this sum
without ever holding the full row $S_i$ in memory simultaneously.

**Forward algorithm** (Algorithm 1 of Dao et al. 2022). Loop over Q-tiles in the outer loop
(parallelizable across CUDA blocks), K/V tiles in the inner loop. For each Q-tile of shape
$(B_r, d)$: zero an output accumulator $O \in \mathbb{R}^{B_r \times d}$, a max
$m \in \mathbb{R}^{B_r}$, and a denominator $\ell \in \mathbb{R}^{B_r}$. For each inner K/V
tile of shape $(B_c, d)$, compute the local $S_{\text{tile}} = Q_{\text{tile}} K_{\text{tile}}^\top /\sqrt{d}$,
take its row-max, update $m$, exponentiate, update $\ell$ and $O$ with the merge equations
above. After the inner loop, divide $O$ by $\ell$ once. Write $O$ to HBM. The output is
bit-equivalent to the dense softmax up to floating-point summation order.

**Backward algorithm** (Algorithm 2 of Dao et al. 2022). Storing the $N \times N$ matrix $P$
for the backward pass would defeat the purpose. Instead, FlashAttention stores only the
softmax statistics $m, \ell \in \mathbb{R}^N$ from the forward pass and **recomputes** the
relevant tiles of $S$ and $P$ inside SRAM during the backward — the same idea as gradient
checkpointing applied at the kernel level. The extra FLOPs are recouped many times over by
the avoided HBM traffic; the paper reports the backward at $\approx 1.5\times$ the forward
cost, vs $\approx 3\times$ for the standard implementation.

**Memory complexity.** Peak activation memory drops from $O(N^2)$ (standard, materialized
$S, P$) to $O(N)$ (FlashAttention, only $m, \ell$ stored across the iteration). The $O(Nd)$
output is unchanged. This is the lever that unlocked $N \ge 64\text{K}$ training on a single
GPU.

**FLOP complexity.** Unchanged at $O(N^2 d)$ — the matmul work is the same. The savings are
in the constant in front of the *bytes* term, not the FLOPs. Dao et al. (§3.3) prove a
lower bound: any IO-aware attention algorithm requires
$\Omega(N^2 d^2 / M)$ HBM accesses where $M$ is SRAM size, and FlashAttention is within a
constant of this lower bound. Standard attention has $\Theta(N d + N^2)$ HBM accesses — the
$N^2$ term dominates at long $N$ and is what FlashAttention removes.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 3 · Reference implementation</p>
## Sketch

```python
# Forward sketch — illustrative, not a CUDA kernel.
# Q, K, V: [N, d] in HBM.
# Output: O, plus log-sum-exp statistics for the backward.
def flash_attention_forward(Q, K, V, B_r, B_c):
    N, d = Q.shape
    O = zeros((N, d))                      # accumulator in HBM, written once.
    m = full((N,), -inf)                   # running row max.
    l = zeros((N,))                        # running row denominator.
    for q_start in range(0, N, B_r):       # outer loop — Q tiles.
        q = Q[q_start:q_start+B_r]         # [B_r, d], resident in SRAM.
        o_local = zeros((B_r, d))          # in SRAM.
        m_local = full((B_r,), -inf)
        l_local = zeros((B_r,))
        for k_start in range(0, N, B_c):   # inner loop — K, V tiles stream in.
            k = K[k_start:k_start+B_c]     # [B_c, d], resident in SRAM.
            v = V[k_start:k_start+B_c]
            s = q @ k.T / d**0.5           # [B_r, B_c] — never written to HBM.
            m_new = maximum(m_local, s.max(axis=-1))
            p = exp(s - m_new[:, None])
            l_local = exp(m_local - m_new) * l_local + p.sum(axis=-1)
            o_local = exp(m_local - m_new)[:, None] * o_local + p @ v
            m_local = m_new
        O[q_start:q_start+B_r] = o_local / l_local[:, None]
        m[q_start:q_start+B_r] = m_local
        l[q_start:q_start+B_r] = l_local
    return O, m, l  # m, l are reused on the backward via recomputation.
```

The load-bearing mechanical difference vs naive attention: the $N \times N$ score matrix $s$
is never written to HBM. It lives in SRAM for the duration of each $(B_r, B_c)$ tile pass,
gets consumed by the online softmax, and is discarded. HBM traffic per Q-tile is
$O(B_r d + (N / B_c) \cdot B_c d) = O(N d)$, vs the standard implementation's $O(N^2)$ for
the materialized score matrix.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 4 · Empirical evidence</p>
## What FlashAttention buys, in numbers

Dao et al. (2022, Table 1) on GPT-2 small (124M params, $N = 1\text{K}$) end-to-end training:
FlashAttention 3.5× faster than the PyTorch eager implementation, 1.6× faster than Megatron-LM's
fused attention. On GPT-2 medium (350M, $N = 1\text{K}$): 3.0× over PyTorch eager. At BERT-large
($N = 512$): 1.15× — the speedup is smaller at short sequences because the constant overhead of
the tile loop matters more relative to the savings. The headline 2–4× number is for
long-sequence training where the HBM-traffic saving compounds.

Peak memory measurements (Table 2 of Dao et al. 2022): GPT-2 medium at $N = 16\text{K}$
crashes on an A100 with standard attention ($\approx 80$ GB peak) but trains under
$\approx 20$ GB peak with FlashAttention — the $5\times$–$20\times$ memory reduction headline.
This is the result that unlocked the $\ge 32\text{K}$ context training that the 2023 long-context
wave (LongLLaMA, Claude 100K, GPT-4-32K) leaned on.

**FlashAttention-2** (Dao 2023, [arXiv 2307.08691](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.08691),
§3.1, Figure 5) reorganizes the inner loop and parallelizes across the sequence axis (FA-1
parallelized only over batch and head axes). On A100 at $N = 16\text{K}, d = 128$: FA-2 hits
$\approx 230$ TFLOPS/s vs FA-1's $\approx 140$ TFLOPS/s — 1.7× over v1. End-to-end GPT-3-style
training at $N = 8\text{K}$ on 64 A100s: $1.4\times$ wall-clock speedup over FA-1.

**FlashAttention-3** (Shah, Dao et al. 2024, [arXiv 2407.08608](https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.08608),
§4) targets H100's asynchronous tensor-memory accelerator (TMA) and warp-specialized
scheduling. On H100 at $d = 128, N = 16\text{K}$, fp16: 740 TFLOPS/s — $\approx 75\%$ of H100's
peak fp16 throughput, $1.5\text{–}2\times$ over FA-2. The same paper reports an fp8 variant at
1.2 PFLOPS/s on H100, $\approx 1.5\times$ further over fp16.

Independent reproduction in PyTorch's `torch.nn.functional.scaled_dot_product_attention`
(merged via PyTorch 2.0, March 2023) wraps the FlashAttention-2 kernel as one of three
default backends; the same operator under JAX
([dot_product_attention](https://github.com/google/jax/pull/15294)) ships an equivalent
kernel. Triton implementations of FlashAttention-2
([OpenAI tutorial](https://triton-lang.org/main/getting-started/tutorials/06-fused-attention.html))
are within 5% of the CUDA kernel on A100 and have become the reference reimplementation for
custom backends.

The kernel is so universal that **adopting it is invisible at the model-spec level** — none of
the 50+ models in this knowledge base list "FlashAttention" as an architectural choice, the
way they list GQA or RoPE. It is in the system stack, not the model card. Llama-3
(Meta 2024, [arXiv 2407.21783](https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.21783), §3) cites FlashAttention-2 by
name as the training kernel; DeepSeek-V3
([arXiv 2412.19437](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.19437), §3.5) uses a custom FlashAttention-3-style
kernel; Gemma-2 (Gemma team 2024, [arXiv 2408.00118](https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.00118)) cites
FA-2 directly. The pattern across all frontier-decoder tech reports is the same: cite, then
move on. No public study finds a workload where vanilla attention is competitive once the
sequence length crosses $\approx 2\text{K}$.

For numerical-precision sensitivity, Golden et al. (2024,
["Is Flash Attention Stable?"](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.02803), §4) compared FA-2 against
the reference attention on the same model and observe that bf16 FlashAttention can produce
training-loss differences of $\approx 10^{-3}$ per step from accumulated reduction-order
effects in the online-softmax merge — small enough that no production trainer has reported it
as a quality issue, but non-zero and worth flagging. The fp32 variant is bit-equivalent up to
reduction order.

<Summary>
**FlashAttention in one breath.** Tile attention so $QK^\top$ and softmax stay in SRAM; use
online softmax across tile boundaries and recomputation across the backward. Exact (not
approximate), 2–4× faster than naive on A100, 5–20× less peak memory. FA-2 reorganizes the
loop for sequence-axis parallelism; FA-3 specializes for H100 async TMA. Universal in modern
training; invisible at the model-spec level.
</Summary>
