<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 1 · Premise</p>
## MHA vs MQA, both flawed

Multi-Head Attention (Vaswani et al. 2017) gives every query head its own K and V projection.
For $H$ heads at $d_h$ head-dim, the KV cache per token per layer is

$$\text{cache} = 2 \cdot H \cdot d_h \quad (\text{fp16 bytes} = 4 \cdot H \cdot d_h).$$

For a 64-head model at $d_h = 128$, that is 16 KB per token per layer. At 128K context and 80
layers, the KV cache pushes 160 GB — most of a frontier inference budget.

Multi-Query Attention (Shazeer 2019) is the opposite extreme: one K, V pair shared across all
heads. The cache becomes $2 \cdot d_h$ — an $H\times$ reduction. The quality cost is real but
hard to pin down precisely; PaLM and others reported subtle degradation at scale.

GQA picks the middle.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 2 · The grouping</p>
## Share K, V across groups of size H/G

Pick a group count $G$ dividing $H$. Each group of $H/G$ query heads shares one K, V pair.

$$\mathbf{k}^{(g)}_t = W_K^{(g)}\,\mathbf{h}_t,\quad \mathbf{v}^{(g)}_t = W_V^{(g)}\,\mathbf{h}_t,\quad g \in \{1, \ldots, G\}.$$

At attention time, query head $i$ uses $K, V$ from group $\lfloor i / (H/G) \rfloor$:

$$\mathrm{Attn}_i(\mathbf{Q}, K^{(g)}, V^{(g)}) = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{\mathbf{Q}^{(i)}\,K^{(g)\top}}{\sqrt{d_h}}\right)V^{(g)}.$$

KV cache per token per layer becomes $2 \cdot G \cdot d_h$. The KV-cache ratio vs MHA is exactly $H/G$.

```python
def gqa(q, k, v, num_heads, num_kv_heads):
    # q: [B, T, H, d_h]; k, v: [B, T, G, d_h]
    repeat = num_heads // num_kv_heads
    k = k.repeat_interleave(repeat, dim=2)  # [B, T, H, d_h]
    v = v.repeat_interleave(repeat, dim=2)
    return scaled_dot_product_attention(q, k, v)
```

In practice the repeat is implemented as a virtual stride in optimized kernels rather than a
materialized copy — Flash-Attn and friends know how to broadcast across the group axis.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 3 · Uptraining from MHA</p>
## The clever bit of the paper

Ainslie et al.'s actual contribution was less the architecture (the idea is mechanical) and
more the recipe: they showed you can *uptrain* an existing MHA checkpoint to GQA with only
~5% of the original training compute. Initialize each group's $W_K^{(g)}, W_V^{(g)}$ by averaging
the original $W_K^{(i)}, W_V^{(i)}$ across the heads that will share them, then fine-tune.

This was the unlock that let Llama 2 70B ship with GQA without retraining from scratch, and
established GQA as the default thereafter.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 4 · Results</p>
## Empirical evidence

The original paper (Table 1) reports T5-XXL with GQA at $G = 8$ matching the MHA baseline's
quality within noise on translation and summarization tasks, with $8\times$ smaller KV cache.
MQA on the same setup loses 1–2 quality points.

Llama 2 (Touvron et al. 2023) confirmed the same at decoder-LM scale: GQA-8 matched MHA on
HumanEval, MMLU, and reasoning suites within run-to-run noise. Llama 3 and 3.1 kept GQA
unchanged.

For models that need even smaller cache than GQA-8 allows, the natural next step is MLA — see
the [MLA entry](/llm-arch-kb/attention/mla/) for the latent-compression alternative.

<Summary>
**GQA in one breath.** Share K, V across small groups of query heads. $H/G\times$ smaller KV cache
with quality indistinguishable from MHA; uptrainable from an MHA checkpoint in 5% of original
compute. The dominant 2023–25 attention layout for dense decoders.
</Summary>
