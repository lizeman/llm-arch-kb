<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 1 · Premise</p>
## Attention is approximately low-rank — bake that in

Standard self-attention over a sequence of length $N$ with head dimension $d_h$ computes an
$N \times N$ probability matrix $P = \mathrm{softmax}(QK^\top/\sqrt{d_h})$ and then applies it
to $V \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d_h}$. Time and memory are $\Theta(N^2 d_h)$, and the dominant
cost at $N \gtrsim d_h$ is the matrix $P$ itself, which carries $N^2$ logits regardless of
content. At $N = 4{,}096$, a single attention head materializes 67 MB of fp32 logits per batch
element before softmax — and the model has $H$ of these per layer.

Wang et al. (§ 2 and Figure 2 of [arXiv 2006.04768](https://arxiv.org/abs/2006.04768)) ran a
singular-value analysis of $P$ in pretrained RoBERTa and observed that its singular spectrum
decays rapidly: ~90% of the spectral energy is captured by the top-128 singular vectors at
$N = 512$. They proved (their Theorem 1, via Johnson–Lindenstrauss) that for any softmax
attention matrix and any $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists a rank-$k$ approximation with
$k = \Theta(d_h \log(d_h) / \varepsilon^2)$ that achieves $\varepsilon$ error in the resulting
output — and crucially, $k$ does not grow with $N$.

The architectural question: can the low rank be enforced at training time rather than
discovered post hoc on a trained model? Linformer's bet: yes, by projecting $K$ and $V$ along
the sequence axis to a fixed dimension $k$ via two learned matrices. The resulting attention
has $\Theta(N k d_h)$ cost — linear in $N$ — and trains end-to-end.

The predecessor lineage is the broader "fast transformer" line of 2019–2020 — Sparse
Transformer ([entry](/llm-arch-kb/attention/sparse-transformer/)) commits to a structured
sparsity pattern, Reformer ([entry](/llm-arch-kb/attention/reformer/)) hashes to learned
buckets. Linformer differs by attacking the low-rank structure of the dense probability matrix
itself, not the sparsity of its support.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 2 · Derivation</p>
## Project the sequence axis, not the feature axis

Let $Q \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d_h}$, $K \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d_h}$, $V \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d_h}$
be the queries, keys, and values for one head ($H$ heads in parallel). Standard scaled
dot-product attention is

$$\mathrm{Attn}(Q, K, V) \;=\; \mathrm{softmax}\!\Bigl(\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{d_h}} Q K^\top\Bigr) V \;\in\; \mathbb{R}^{N \times d_h}.$$

**Step 1: insert a sequence-axis projection.** Introduce two learnable matrices
$E \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times N}$ and $F \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times N}$, where $k$ is a chosen rank
(Wang et al. use $k = 128$ or $256$ regardless of $N$). Apply them to $K$ and $V$ along the
sequence axis:

$$\tilde{K} \;=\; E K \;\in\; \mathbb{R}^{k \times d_h}, \qquad \tilde{V} \;=\; F V \;\in\; \mathbb{R}^{k \times d_h}.$$

Note that $E$ and $F$ act on the *sequence* dimension, not the feature dimension. The features
$d_h$ are untouched. This is the load-bearing design choice: a feature-axis projection
(reducing $d_h$ to $d' < d_h$) loses the model's representational dimensionality; a
sequence-axis projection only loses the number of distinct "memory slots" the queries can
attend to, which the low-rank result says is safe.

**Step 2: attend against the projected sequence.** Replace $K, V$ with $\tilde{K}, \tilde{V}$:

$$\mathrm{LinAttn}(Q, K, V) \;=\; \mathrm{softmax}\!\Bigl(\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{d_h}} Q \tilde{K}^\top\Bigr) \tilde{V} \;\in\; \mathbb{R}^{N \times d_h}.$$

The logit matrix $Q \tilde{K}^\top$ is now $N \times k$, not $N \times N$. The softmax is over
$k$ entries per row. The final matmul against $\tilde{V}$ is $N \times k \cdot k \times d_h$.

**Step 3: count the FLOPs.** Each query computes $k$ inner products against the $k$ projected
keys, then weights $k$ projected values:

$$\mathrm{FLOPs}_{\text{Linformer}} \;=\; \underbrace{\Theta(N k d_h)}_{Q \tilde{K}^\top} + \underbrace{\Theta(N k d_h)}_{\text{attn} \cdot \tilde{V}} + \underbrace{\Theta(N k d_h)}_{EK \text{ and } FV} \;=\; \Theta(N k d_h).$$

Linear in $N$ at fixed $k$. The $EK$ and $FV$ projections themselves are $k \times N \cdot N \times d_h = \Theta(N k d_h)$ matmuls, so they cost the same as the attention step rather than dominating.

**Step 4: why is the approximation tight?** Wang et al. (Theorem 1 of [arXiv 2006.04768](https://arxiv.org/abs/2006.04768),
restated):

> For any $Q, K \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d_h}$ and any $\varepsilon, \delta \in (0, 1)$, there
> exists a matrix $E \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times N}$ with $k = \Theta(d_h \log d_h / \varepsilon^2)$
> such that, with probability $\ge 1 - \delta$,
> $$\bigl\lVert \mathrm{softmax}(QK^\top) v - \mathrm{softmax}(Q (EK)^\top) (E v) \bigr\rVert \;\le\; \varepsilon \lVert \mathrm{softmax}(QK^\top) v \rVert$$
> simultaneously for every column $v$ of $V$.

The proof reduces softmax-attention error to Johnson–Lindenstrauss inner-product preservation
on the unnormalized logits, then exploits the Lipschitz constant of softmax. The key takeaway
is that the required $k$ depends on $d_h$ (head dimension) and the target error, *not on
$N$* — the projection rank can be a fixed hyperparameter and quality does not degrade as $N$
grows. Empirically (Figure 4 of the paper), $k = 128$ is sufficient up to $N = 4{,}096$.

**Step 5: parameter sharing.** A naive instantiation has $H$ pairs of $(E_h, F_h)$ matrices per
layer, $2 H k N$ parameters total. Wang et al. sweep three sharing modes (§ 4.2):

1. **Headwise**: separate $(E_h, F_h)$ per head — most parameters, best quality.
2. **Key-Value shared**: $E_h = F_h$ within a head — half the params, ~0.1 GLUE points worse.
3. **Layerwise shared**: one $(E, F)$ pair shared across all heads in a layer — fewest params,
   best Pareto point.

The recommended default is layerwise sharing, which adds $2 k N$ parameters per layer — roughly
$0.5\%$ of the FFN's parameter count at typical $k, N$ scales.

**Why this works only for encoders.** $E$ has shape $k \times N$, hard-coded at training time
to the maximum sequence length $N_{\text{max}}$. Two consequences:

- At inference time, the model cannot handle sequences longer than $N_{\text{max}}$ — the
  projection has no defined behavior on out-of-range positions.
- Causal masking is incompatible with $E K$. The projection mixes keys across all positions,
  so a query at position $t$ ends up attending to $\tilde{K}_j = \sum_{s=1}^N E_{js} \mathbf{k}_s$
  — a key that has already been contaminated by future positions $s > t$.

Wang et al. address only the bidirectional (encoder) case. Subsequent work — Linear Attention
(Katharopoulos et al. 2020), Performer ([entry](/llm-arch-kb/attention/performer/)) — solves
the decoder case via a feature-map kernel rather than a sequence-axis projection.

**Parameter count and complexity summary.** Per layer at head dim $d_h$, $H$ heads, sequence
length $N$, rank $k$, with layerwise-shared projection:

$$|\theta_{\text{Linformer}}| \;=\; \underbrace{4 H d_h^2}_{\text{QKVO projections}} + \underbrace{2 k N}_{\text{Linformer projections}},$$

$$\mathrm{FLOPs}_{\text{Linformer}}/\text{layer} \;=\; \Theta(N k d_h H).$$

The FLOP saving vs. standard attention is the factor $k / N$.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 3 · Reference implementation</p>
## Sketch

```python
def linformer_attention(Q, K, V, E, F):
    # Q, K, V: [B, T, d_h]            T <= T_max, fixed at training time
    # E, F:    [k, T_max]              shared across heads in this layer
    K_proj = einsum("kt,btd->bkd", E, K)            # [B, k, d_h]
    V_proj = einsum("kt,btd->bkd", F, V)            # [B, k, d_h]
    logits = einsum("btd,bkd->btk", Q, K_proj)      # [B, T, k]
    logits = logits / d_h**0.5
    attn   = logits.softmax(-1)                     # softmax over k, not over T
    return einsum("btk,bkd->btd", attn, V_proj)      # [B, T, d_h]
```

The load-bearing change vs. MHA is the two `einsum` projections that compress the sequence
axis from $T$ to $k$ before any $Q$-dependent computation. The remaining attention is
identical in shape to standard MHA with a "key sequence length" of $k$. The implementation
fits in a single Hugging Face transformers patch (see `linformer-pytorch` by Lucidrains for a
production-style version: [github.com/lucidrains/linformer](https://github.com/lucidrains/linformer)).

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 4 · Empirical evidence</p>
## Results

**Pretraining quality at fixed $N$ (Wang et al. 2020, Table 3).** A 12-layer Linformer with
$k = 128$ trained on the same RoBERTa pretraining recipe at $N = 512$ matches the dense
RoBERTa baseline within 0.5 GLUE points on average and matches on SQuAD-1.1. At $k = 256$ the
gap closes to within 0.1 GLUE points. The paper reports a 1.5× wall-clock speedup at $N = 512$
that grows to 5.5× at $N = 4{,}096$ (their Figure 3).

**Quality at long sequences (Table 3 again).** At $N = 1024$ and $N = 2048$, Linformer at
$k = 256$ remains within ~0.3 GLUE points of the dense baseline that was *not* trained at these
lengths — Linformer is the first transformer in their suite to support these sequence lengths
on the same hardware. The paper does not have a dense-attention comparison at $N \ge 4{,}096$
because it does not fit on the V100 hardware they used.

**Long Range Arena (Tay et al. 2020, [arXiv 2011.04006](https://arxiv.org/abs/2011.04006),
Table 1).** Independent reproduction by the LRA benchmark suite scores Linformer at average
51.36 across the five LRA tasks (ListOps 35.7, Text 53.9, Retrieval 52.3, Image 38.6, Pathfinder
76.3), vs. 54.39 for the dense softmax-attention baseline — a ~3-point gap. Linformer is
mid-pack among the LRA variants: better than Reformer (50.67) and Performer (51.41), worse than
BigBird (55.0) and Longformer (53.5). LRA uses a fixed $N = 4{,}096$ across tasks, which is
exactly the regime Linformer was designed for.

**Sensitivity to $k$ (Wang et al. Figure 4).** Quality is monotone-increasing in $k$ with a
clear knee around $k = 128$ for $N \le 1{,}024$ and around $k = 256$ for $N \le 4{,}096$.
Past $k = 512$ there is no measurable improvement and the speedup over dense attention shrinks
toward 1×.

**Sharing scheme (Wang et al. Table 5).** Layerwise sharing loses ~0.1–0.2 GLUE points vs.
headwise sharing but uses $1/H$ as many projection parameters. Key-value sharing within a head
($E = F$) is essentially free at $\le 0.1$ point cost. Their recommended default ships
layerwise-shared projections.

**Why no decoder LLM ships it.** Beyond the causality and variable-length issues laid out in
§ 2, Wang et al. did not run a decoder-side ablation. Subsequent surveys (Tay et al. 2022,
"Efficient Transformers: A Survey", [arXiv 2009.06732](https://arxiv.org/abs/2009.06732), §3.4)
classify Linformer as a "fixed-pattern + low-rank" hybrid suitable only for the encoder
setting. The lineage descendant that *does* work autoregressively is Linear Attention
(Katharopoulos et al. 2020), which uses a feature-map kernel
$\phi(\mathbf{q})^\top \phi(\mathbf{k})$ instead of a sequence-axis projection.

**Independent reproduction.** The HuggingFace `nielsr/linformer` implementation reproduces
the GLUE numbers within 0.3 points using $k = 128$; the FAIR `fairseq` reference shipped a
Linformer variant in 2021 that reproduces the speedup curves on TPU v3 hardware.

**No public production-LLM adoption.** I do not know of any frontier decoder-only LLM that
ships Linformer. The technique persists in long-document encoder applications and as a baseline
in efficient-attention research, but not in production generative models.

<Summary>
**Linformer in one breath.** Learn N→k projection matrices for K and V; attention becomes O(N·k)
via the resulting low-rank factorization. Matches softmax on encoder tasks. The sequence-length-
dependent projection makes it awkward for autoregressive decoders, so no current frontier
decoder ships it.
</Summary>
