<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 1 · Premise</p>
## Attention is low-rank in practice

Wang et al. observed that the attention matrix $P = \mathrm{softmax}(QK^\top / \sqrt{d_h})$ in
a trained transformer has approximately low rank — a small number of singular values capture
most of its mass. If the matrix is rank-$k$ with $k \ll N$, then there exists a rank-$k$
factorization that approximates $P$ accurately.

The architectural question: can you bake this low-rank structure into the model from training
time, rather than discovering it post hoc? Linformer's bet: yes, via learned projections of
K and V along the sequence dimension.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 2 · The projection</p>
## Squash N down to k

Define two learnable matrices $E, F \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times N}$ that project along the
sequence axis. Apply them to K and V:

$$\tilde{K} = E K \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times d_h},\quad \tilde{V} = F V \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times d_h}.$$

Now compute attention against the projected keys and values:

$$\mathrm{Attn}(Q, K, V) = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{Q \tilde{K}^\top}{\sqrt{d_h}}\right)\tilde{V}.$$

The intermediate $Q \tilde{K}^\top$ matrix is $N \times k$, not $N \times N$. Total compute is
$O(N \cdot k \cdot d_h)$ — linear in $N$.

```python
def linformer_attention(Q, K, V, E, F):
    # Q, K, V: [B, T, d_h]
    # E, F: [k, T_max]   (T_max is the maximum supported sequence length)
    K_proj = einsum("kt,btd->bkd", E, K)        # [B, k, d_h]
    V_proj = einsum("kt,btd->bkd", F, V)
    logits = einsum("btd,bkd->btk", Q, K_proj) / d_h**0.5
    return einsum("btk,bkd->btd", logits.softmax(-1), V_proj)
```

The projections $E, F$ are learned, shared across heads (in the original paper's recommended
configuration), and sized $k \approx 256$ regardless of $N$.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 3 · Why no production decoder uses Linformer</p>
## The autoregressive problem

The projection matrices $E, F$ have shape $k \times N$, which is fixed at training time. For
encoder applications with bounded sequence length, this is fine. For autoregressive decoders,
two problems arise:

1. **Variable-length inference**. At generation time the sequence grows token by token; an
   $N$-fixed projection is incompatible with extending past training length.
2. **Causal masking gets weird**. The projection mixes past and future keys before attention
   ever runs; recovering a causal structure requires careful constraints on $E, F$ that the
   original paper does not formalize.

Several follow-ups (Performer's random-feature kernel, Linear Attention's prefix-sum recurrence)
address the autoregressive case via mathematically distinct mechanisms. Linformer itself
remains an encoder technique.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 4 · Results</p>
## Empirical evidence

On GLUE and IMDB (encoder tasks), Linformer at $k = 256$ matches the standard transformer
within 0.5 quality points (Wang et al. Table 1), with $4$–$10$× lower compute at $N = 512$
to $N = 4096$. At very long sequences ($N \geq 16K$) the gap widens to $\sim 1$ quality point
but compute stays linear.

The technique never crossed into frontier decoder-only LLMs but its low-rank framing — the
attention matrix is approximately rank-$k$ for small $k$ — informs the subsequent linear-attention
research lineage. See the [Linear Attention entry](/llm-arch-kb/attention/linear-attention/)
for the lineage descendant that does support autoregressive decoding.

<Summary>
**Linformer in one breath.** Learn N→k projection matrices for K and V; attention becomes O(N·k)
via the resulting low-rank factorization. Matches softmax on encoder tasks. The sequence-length-
dependent projection makes it awkward for autoregressive decoders, so no current frontier
decoder ships it.
</Summary>
