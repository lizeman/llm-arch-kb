<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 1 · Premise</p>
## One attention head cannot carry every relation type

Vaswani et al. (2017) opened the transformer paper with a deliberately weak baseline: a single
scaled dot-product attention over the full model width $d_{\text{model}} = 512$. With queries,
keys and values living in one inner-product space, every relation a token might want to express
— subject-verb agreement, coreference, positional adjacency, topical similarity — collapses
onto the same set of $W_Q, W_K, W_V$ weights. The bet of "Attention Is All You Need" §3.2.2 is
that splitting the projection into $H$ smaller heads, each with its own $d_h = d_{\text{model}}/H$
subspace, gives the optimizer enough independent capacity to specialize.

Concretely: at $d_{\text{model}} = 512, H = 8$ the per-head $d_h = 64$ and each head holds
$3 \cdot 512 \cdot 64 = 98{,}304$ parameters. Across 8 heads plus the output projection
$W_O \in \mathbb{R}^{512 \times 512}$, the attention sub-layer carries $4 \cdot 512^2 \approx 1.05$M
parameters — exactly matching the cost of a single full-rank attention while distributing
expressivity across heads. The paper's Table 3 ablation (row B, $H = 1$) shows base-model
perplexity rising from 4.92 to 5.29 and BLEU dropping by 0.9 points on WMT En-De when $H$
is forced to 1 — the empirical case for multiple heads.

The lineage runs from the Bahdanau (2014) additive attention used in seq2seq translators,
through Luong (2015) multiplicative attention, to Cheng et al. (2016) intra-attention which
first applied attention inside a single sequence. Vaswani's contribution is twofold: dropping
recurrence entirely (the entire sequence is consumed in parallel), and breaking attention into
parallel subspaces. The first move is what made the architecture trainable at scale; the
second is what made it expressive enough to compete with LSTM seq2seq at translation quality.

What this entry sets up: the layer's exact math, its parameter and compute footprint, the
mechanical reason its KV cache eventually became intolerable at long context, and what survives
in every descendant — GQA, MQA, MLA, linear-attention variants — that has supplanted it.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 2 · Derivation</p>
## From dot-product attention to H parallel subspaces

The starting point is the scaled dot-product attention of Vaswani et al. (2017) §3.2.1. For a
sequence of $T$ tokens at width $d_{\text{model}}$, with $X \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times d_{\text{model}}}$
the input, the operator is

$$\mathrm{Attn}(Q, K, V) = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{Q K^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) V,$$

where $Q, K \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times d_k}$ are queries and keys, $V \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times d_v}$
are values, and $d_k$ is the key dimension. The $\sqrt{d_k}$ scaling is justified in §3.2.1: for
random $\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{k}$ with i.i.d. zero-mean unit-variance components, $\mathbf{q}^\top\mathbf{k}$
has variance $d_k$, so dividing by $\sqrt{d_k}$ keeps the softmax temperature constant as the
head dimension changes — without it, large $d_k$ pushes the softmax into saturated regions
where gradients vanish.

Multi-head attention replaces the single projection $W_Q, W_K, W_V$ with $H$ per-head
projections. For head $i \in \{1, \ldots, H\}$ at head dim $d_h = d_{\text{model}}/H$:

$$\mathbf{Q}_i = X W_Q^{(i)},\quad \mathbf{K}_i = X W_K^{(i)},\quad \mathbf{V}_i = X W_V^{(i)},$$

with $W_Q^{(i)}, W_K^{(i)}, W_V^{(i)} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{model}} \times d_h}$. Each head
computes its own attention

$$\mathrm{head}_i = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{\mathbf{Q}_i \mathbf{K}_i^\top}{\sqrt{d_h}}\right) \mathbf{V}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times d_h},$$

and the outputs concatenate before a final linear projection $W_O \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{model}} \times d_{\text{model}}}$:

$$\mathrm{MHA}(X) = \bigl[\mathrm{head}_1 \,\Vert\, \cdots \,\Vert\, \mathrm{head}_H\bigr]\, W_O.$$

Why split the projection rather than run $H$ full-width attentions? Two reasons in §3.2.2.
First, parameter parity: the concatenated per-head projection matrices stack to a single matrix
$W_Q = [W_Q^{(1)} \,\Vert\, \cdots \,\Vert\, W_Q^{(H)}] \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{model}} \times d_{\text{model}}}$,
so multi-head attention with $H$ heads at $d_h = d_{\text{model}}/H$ costs the same number of
parameters as single-head attention at $d_k = d_{\text{model}}$. The split is free. Second,
the lower per-head $d_k$ keeps each head's softmax inputs bounded, which interacts well with the
$\sqrt{d_k}$ scaling.

Why concatenate instead of average? Concatenation preserves all $H \cdot d_h = d_{\text{model}}$
output dimensions and lets the downstream $W_O$ choose how to mix them. Averaging would force
heads to agree on a single output and erase the specialization the architecture is designed to
encourage.

The causal-decoder version applies a mask $M \in \{0, -\infty\}^{T \times T}$ inside the softmax
to forbid attending to future positions: $\mathrm{head}_i = \mathrm{softmax}((\mathbf{Q}_i \mathbf{K}_i^\top + M)/\sqrt{d_h})\, \mathbf{V}_i$
with $M_{tt'} = -\infty$ for $t' > t$. This is what the language-model variants of the
transformer use.

**Parameter count.** The attention sub-layer carries
$P_{\text{attn}} = 4 \cdot d_{\text{model}}^2$
parameters: three $d_{\text{model}} \times d_{\text{model}}$ projection matrices (stacked from
per-head pieces) plus one output projection. For Llama-1-65B at $d_{\text{model}} = 8192$, this
is $4 \cdot 8192^2 \approx 268$M parameters per layer.

**FLOP cost.** Per layer, the projections cost $4 \cdot T \cdot d_{\text{model}}^2$ FLOPs and the
attention-score computation costs $2 \cdot H \cdot T^2 \cdot d_h = 2 \cdot T^2 \cdot d_{\text{model}}$
FLOPs. The score matrix is the quadratic term that dominates at long $T$.

**KV cache size.** At inference, each generated token writes $K, V \in \mathbb{R}^{H \cdot d_h}$
to the cache, so cumulative cache size is

$$M_{\text{KV}} = 2 \cdot L \cdot H \cdot d_h \cdot T \cdot \text{bytes/scalar},$$

where $L$ is layer count and $T$ is context length. At $L = 80, H = 64, d_h = 128$, fp16
($2$ bytes), $T = 128\text{K}$ — the headline DeepSeek-grade long-context setting — the cache is
$2 \cdot 80 \cdot 64 \cdot 128 \cdot 128\,000 \cdot 2 \approx 335$ GB. This is the cost that
killed pure MHA for long-context production inference and forced the MQA/GQA/MLA lineage.

The asymmetry between train and inference matters: at training time, the per-head K and V are
recomputed from $X$ every forward pass and never cached, so MHA's compute per layer is the
quadratic-in-$T$ score matrix plus $O(T \cdot d_{\text{model}}^2)$ projections. At inference,
the projections of the new token cost $O(d_{\text{model}}^2)$ but every prior K, V must remain
resident in HBM to answer the next softmax. The architecture that is cheap to train is the same
architecture that is expensive to decode — and decode is what production serves.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 3 · Reference implementation</p>
## Sketch

The implementation below shows the structural pieces — projection, head split, per-head
softmax, concatenation, output projection. It is illustrative, not optimized: the in-place
softmax and matmul fusion that production paths use (FlashAttention's tile loop) hide the
per-head shape inside a kernel, but the operator definition is the one shown.

```python
def mha(x, W_Q, W_K, W_V, W_O, H, mask=None):
    # x: [B, T, d_model]
    B, T, D = x.shape
    d_h = D // H
    # Project and split into heads.
    q = (x @ W_Q).view(B, T, H, d_h).transpose(1, 2)  # [B, H, T, d_h]
    k = (x @ W_K).view(B, T, H, d_h).transpose(1, 2)  # [B, H, T, d_h]
    v = (x @ W_V).view(B, T, H, d_h).transpose(1, 2)  # [B, H, T, d_h]
    # Per-head scores: each head sees its own d_h-dim subspace.
    logits = (q @ k.transpose(-2, -1)) / d_h**0.5     # [B, H, T, T]
    if mask is not None:
        logits = logits.masked_fill(mask, float("-inf"))
    # Softmax over the key axis, then mix V.
    out = logits.softmax(-1) @ v                       # [B, H, T, d_h]
    # Concatenate heads (transpose then reshape) and project.
    return out.transpose(1, 2).reshape(B, T, D) @ W_O.T  # [B, T, d_model]
```

The load-bearing structure: K and V are written per-head, with a separate $(T, d_h)$ tile
per head per layer. Every variant in the lineage modifies exactly that step. MQA collapses to
one shared K, V; GQA partitions heads into groups; MLA replaces per-head K, V with a single
low-rank latent. Everything else — the softmax, the $\sqrt{d_h}$ scaling, the per-head split,
the concatenation — survives.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 4 · Empirical evidence</p>
## What the original paper reported, what later work confirmed

The base Transformer at $d_{\text{model}} = 512, H = 8, L = 6$ scored 27.3 BLEU on WMT
2014 En-De and 38.1 BLEU on En-Fr, beating every prior published result while training in 12
hours on 8 P100s (Vaswani et al. 2017, Table 2). The transformer-big variant at
$d_{\text{model}} = 1024, H = 16, L = 6$ pushed En-De to 28.4 and En-Fr to 41.8 (Table 2 rows
4–5). The head-count ablation in Table 3 sweeps $H \in \{1, 4, 8, 16, 32\}$ at fixed parameter
budget: $H = 1$ loses 0.9 BLEU vs $H = 8$; $H = 4, 8, 16$ are within 0.2 BLEU of each other;
$H = 32$ regresses by 0.4 BLEU because per-head $d_h$ shrinks to 16 and individual heads lose
representational capacity. This is the empirical justification for the canonical
"$d_h$ between 64 and 128" range that every descendant inherits.

Independent reproductions of head specialization came quickly. Voita et al. (2019, "Analyzing
Multi-Head Self-Attention", [arXiv 1905.09418](https://arxiv.org/abs/1905.09418)) showed by
attention-head pruning that a subset of heads in a trained transformer specialize on
interpretable patterns — positional (attending to the previous token), syntactic (attending to
the dependency parent), and rare-token attention — while a large fraction (up to 48 of 48 heads
in some layers) can be pruned with $<$1 BLEU loss. This is the strongest evidence that the
multi-head split actually delivers specialization, not just nominal parallelism. Michel et
al. (2019, "Are Sixteen Heads Really Better Than One?",
[arXiv 1905.10650](https://arxiv.org/abs/1905.10650)) reached a similar conclusion: many heads
are redundant after training, but the redundancy emerges from training dynamics that benefit
from over-parameterization — pruning at initialization fails.

For the KV-cache scaling story, the GQA paper of Ainslie et al. (2023,
[arXiv 2305.13245](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.13245), Figure 6) measured T5-XXL decode
throughput as a function of cache size. MHA hits a memory wall at sequence 8K on a single TPU
chip; collapsing to MQA gives a 12× decode speedup at modest quality cost; GQA-8 recovers most
of the quality gap at 11.6× speedup. The MLA paper (DeepSeek-V2, 2024,
[arXiv 2405.04434](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.04434), Table 1) records that MHA at the same
parameter budget would consume $\approx 7\times$ the KV cache of MLA at 128K context — the
quantitative reason no frontier 2024+ decoder ships plain MHA.

The 2024–25 open-decoder census is telling: the original Llama-1 (65B) ran MHA across
all 80 layers at $H = 64$; Llama-2 and every subsequent Llama variant switched to GQA; OLMo-1
shipped MHA at OLMo's choice to mirror Llama-1's recipe, then OLMo-2 moved to GQA. Gemma-1 ran
MHA at an unusually wide $d_h = 256$ (Gemma team 2024, [arXiv 2403.08295](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.08295),
§2), which is the last public production deployment of plain MHA at scale; Gemma-2 also
switched. The transition is essentially complete in dense decoders; the only contexts where
MHA still appears unmodified are short-context encoders (BERT-class) where the cache cost does
not bind and full per-head expressivity is preferred.

No public sensitivity study exists that disentangles the per-head $d_h$ choice from the
total $d_{\text{model}}$ at modern scale — most ablations vary $H$ at fixed
$d_{\text{model}}$, which conflates the two. I don't know of a study at $d_{\text{model}} \ge 8\text{K}$
that varies $d_h$ independently.

One under-discussed empirical detail: Vaswani et al. (2017) §5.3 trained with Adam at the
warmup schedule $\eta = d_{\text{model}}^{-0.5} \cdot \min(s^{-0.5}, s \cdot w^{-1.5})$ with
$w = 4000$ warmup steps, dropout $p_{\text{drop}} = 0.1$ on the attention output and the
sub-layer residual, and label smoothing $\varepsilon_{ls} = 0.1$. Every subsequent
MHA-using decoder we document picked up this recipe (or a close variant) before substituting
GQA/MLA — the head-split was rarely the lever that mattered for fine-tuning behavior; the
$d_h$ choice, the normalization placement, and the optimizer schedule were.

<Summary>
**MHA in one breath.** H independent attention heads in $d_h$-dim subspaces, concatenated and
projected. The KV cache scaling at long context is the cost that retired it for production
inference — every modern decoder shares K and V more aggressively (GQA, MLA). The per-head
subspace decomposition itself survives in every descendant.
</Summary>
