<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 1 · Premise</p>
## Full attention is quadratic

Standard causal attention computes $\mathbf{q}_t \cdot \mathbf{k}_s$ for every $s \leq t$. The
work per layer is $O(T^2 \cdot d_h)$, and the KV cache holds $T \cdot 2 d_h$ floats. At long
context this becomes the dominant cost — quadratic compute, linear memory in $T$ but with a
large constant.

The observation behind sliding-window attention: in practice, attention weights are dominated
by a small local window of recent tokens for most heads, most of the time. The long-range
signal is real but sparse. A local window approximation should sacrifice little at greatly
reduced cost.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 2 · The mask</p>
## Attend to the last W keys

For a window size $W$, replace the causal mask with a causal-and-windowed mask:

$$\mathrm{Attn}_t(\mathbf{q}_t, K, V) = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{\mathbf{q}_t \cdot K_{t-W+1\,:\,t+1}^\top}{\sqrt{d_h}}\right) V_{t-W+1\,:\,t+1}.$$

Query $t$ sees keys in the half-open interval $[t - W + 1,\ t]$. Compute per layer drops to
$O(T \cdot W \cdot d_h)$. KV cache per token is unchanged in shape but can be pruned in inference
because keys older than $W$ are dead — see the [streaming-llm entry](/llm-arch-kb/long-context/streaming-llm/)
for the subtle case where the first few tokens must stay pinned.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 3 · Receptive field grows with depth</p>
## Why local layers still see the full sequence

A token at layer $\ell$ depends on tokens in the previous layer within a window of size $W$
behind it. Stacked, layer $\ell$ at position $t$ depends on layer $0$ tokens in a band of width
$\ell \cdot W$. For a 32-layer model at $W = 4096$, every token's layer-32 representation
depends on roughly 131K predecessors — well past the model's training context.

This is the central trick: local-only attention does not mean local-only information flow.
The receptive field grows multiplicatively across depth, much like a stack of small-kernel
convolutions in vision.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 4 · Interleaved local + global</p>
## What Gemma 3 actually does

Pure SWA gives up the cheap-to-reach long-range signal — for tokens past $L \cdot W$ to the left,
the model has to route information through many intermediate hops. Several modern designs
interleave occasional *global* attention layers (full causal attention) between SWA layers to
restore direct long-range access while keeping most layers cheap.

Gemma 3's published configuration uses a 5:1 SWA-to-global ratio, with the SWA layers running
at RoPE base 10K and a 4096-token window, and the global layers at RoPE base 1M for the full
context. The compute split is roughly 80% local : 20% global at long context — far cheaper than
a pure-global stack.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 5 · Results</p>
## Empirical evidence

Longformer (Beltagy et al. 2020) reported SWA matching full attention on WikiHop and TriviaQA
while reducing peak memory by ~6× at 4K context (Table 1). For decoder-only LLMs, Mistral 7B
(Jiang et al. 2023, arXiv 2310.06825) was the high-profile re-introduction — its `W = 4096`
window plus 32 layers gives an effective receptive field of 131K tokens at a per-layer cost
that scales as $O(T)$.

Gemma 3 reports the interleaved variant matching or exceeding pure-global ablations at long
context on RULER and Needle-in-a-Haystack benchmarks while training ~30% faster at 128K context.
The technique is now standard for context-extension recipes that want to avoid the full
quadratic cost.

<Summary>
**SWA in one breath.** Each query sees only the last W keys; per-layer cost becomes $O(T \cdot W)$;
the effective receptive field grows linearly with depth so the full context is still reachable.
Interleaving an occasional global layer (Gemma 3's 5:1 recipe) restores direct long-range routing
nearly for free.
</Summary>
