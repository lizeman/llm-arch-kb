<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 1 · Premise</p>
## Most attention is wasted

In a trained transformer, the attention matrix is highly sparse in practice: most queries
attend strongly to a small subset of keys. Yet computing the full $T \times T$ logit matrix
requires evaluating every (query, key) pair regardless.

Child et al.'s observation: if you commit to a *structured* sparsity pattern at training time
rather than discovering it post hoc, you can train models with much lower attention cost. The
trick is choosing a pattern dense enough for the model to route any token-to-token signal in
a small number of hops.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 2 · The two patterns</p>
## Strided + Fixed

For sequence length $T$ with stride $\ell \approx \sqrt{T}$, the paper defines two sparse
patterns that interleave across attention heads (or across layers):

- **Strided attention**: each token attends to the previous $\ell$ tokens (a local window) and
  to every $\ell$-th token going back (a strided long-range view). The local window captures
  short-range dependence; the strided keys give every token periodic access to the far past.
- **Fixed attention**: each token attends to its local block of $\ell$ tokens, plus to a small
  set of "summary" tokens that are positioned at fixed locations and that every other token
  also attends to. The summary tokens act as long-range hubs.

Either pattern alone reaches $O(T \sqrt{T})$ keys per query. The paper demonstrates that two
hops of either pattern are sufficient to connect any pair of tokens — meaning the model
retains the expressivity of full attention for sequences past two of its layers.

```python
def strided_mask(T, stride):
    # Each position t attends to: [t-stride, t]  plus  {t, t-stride, t-2*stride, ...}
    mask = torch.zeros(T, T, dtype=bool)
    for t in range(T):
        # Local window
        for s in range(max(0, t - stride + 1), t + 1):
            mask[t, s] = True
        # Strided long-range
        for s in range(0, t + 1, stride):
            mask[t, s] = True
    return mask
```

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 3 · Results and successors</p>
## What it enabled

The original Sparse Transformer trained character-level language models at sequence length
12288 on a single GPU — well past what dense attention could fit. Generative quality on
enwik8, ImageNet at the pixel level, and classical music MIDI all matched or exceeded the
dense baselines of the era.

The two-pattern design did not survive directly into production decoders. Several reasons:

- **Strided + fixed is rigid.** The patterns are hard-coded; the model cannot learn that a
  specific head needs a slightly different sparsity layout.
- **Pure local windows (SWA) plus occasional global attention** (Mistral 7B, Gemma 3) achieve
  similar receptive-field properties with simpler implementation, since "strided" became
  unnecessary once stacked-depth gave the same coverage.
- **Trainable sparsity** (Reformer's LSH attention, Routing Transformer's clustering) tries to
  let the model discover patterns rather than commit to one — though most of these have not
  reached frontier scale either.

What stuck from Child et al.: the framing that O(T√T) is enough, the proof-by-construction that
two hops connect everything, and the demonstration that sparse attention can train stably from
scratch. See the [SWA entry](/llm-arch-kb/attention/sliding-window/) for the descendant pattern
that actually shipped at frontier scale.
