<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 1 · Premise</p>
## The auxiliary-loss tax on MoE routing

A sparse top-$K$ MoE layer with $E$ experts faces a coordination problem the dense FFN does
not have: the router has to decide which experts process which tokens, and the dispatch
infrastructure runs at the speed of the *most-loaded* expert in each step. If the gate
collapses onto a handful of "popular" experts, the rest of the experts sit idle, GPU memory
is wasted on parameters that never see a gradient, and any token that exceeds the popular
experts' capacity gets dropped — its residual passes through unchanged
([Switch Transformer §2.2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2101.03961)).

The standard fix, established by [GShard §2.2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2006.16668) and
canonicalized by Switch, is an **auxiliary load-balancing loss** added to the language-modeling
objective with a small coefficient $\alpha$:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{LM}} + \alpha \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{aux}}, \qquad
\mathcal{L}_{\text{aux}} = E \cdot \sum_{i=1}^{E} f_i \cdot P_i,
$$

where $f_i$ is the fraction of tokens in the batch routed to expert $i$ (a count, post-argmax)
and $P_i$ is the mean softmax probability assigned to expert $i$ by the gate
([Switch Transformer eq. 4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2101.03961)). Switch uses $\alpha = 10^{-2}$;
[DeepSeekMoE §2.3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.06066) uses the same form with a small
$\alpha_1$ for expert-level balance and a separate $\alpha_2$ for device-level balance.

The auxiliary loss works — experts stay balanced — but it has a cost
[Wang et al. 2024 §2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.15664) names directly: the gradient
$\nabla_{\theta} \mathcal{L}_{\text{aux}}$ pulls the gate parameters in a direction that has
nothing to do with predicting the next token. The two gradients can disagree on the same
parameter, and the optimizer averages them. Picking $\alpha$ too small leaves load imbalanced
(experts get dropped, throughput collapses); picking $\alpha$ too large overwhelms the LM
signal and hurts perplexity. Wang et al. quantify the trade-off on a 1B-parameter MoE in their
Figure 2: at $\alpha$ small enough to recover the LM-only perplexity, the maximum-violation
metric on expert load is $\geq 3\times$ the target; at $\alpha$ large enough to push
maximum-violation near zero, perplexity degrades by ~0.04 nats.

**The contribution preview.** Replace the $\alpha \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{aux}}$ term with a
per-expert scalar bias $b_i$ that is added to the routing score at top-$K$ time only, then
updated after each step by a closed-form rule based on observed load — no gradient flows
through $b_i$. The LM gradient stays unmodified; load balance is enforced by book-keeping
instead of by a competing loss.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 2 · Derivation</p>
## From standard top-K routing to a non-differentiable bias

**Standard top-K routing.** Let $\mathbf{x}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$ be the token's residual-stream
input. A standard sparse MoE layer with $E$ experts and top-$K$ gating computes a score for
each expert from a learned gate $W_g \in \mathbb{R}^{E \times d}$ and a non-linearity $\sigma$
(softmax in Switch and GShard; sigmoid in DeepSeekMoE V3):

$$
s_{i,t} = \sigma\bigl( (W_g \mathbf{x}_t)_i \bigr), \qquad
\mathcal{T}_t = \mathrm{TopK}\bigl(\{s_{i,t}\}_{i=1}^{E},\, K\bigr),
$$

where $\mathcal{T}_t \subseteq \{1, \dots, E\}$ is the set of $K$ expert indices chosen for
token $t$. The layer's output is the gate-weighted combination of the selected experts:

$$
\mathrm{MoE}(\mathbf{x}_t) = \sum_{i \in \mathcal{T}_t} g_{i,t} \cdot f_i(\mathbf{x}_t), \qquad
g_{i,t} = \frac{s_{i,t}}{\sum_{j \in \mathcal{T}_t} s_{j,t}},
$$

where $f_i$ is the $i$-th expert FFN and $g_{i,t}$ is the renormalized gate weight
([DeepSeek-V3 §2.1.2, eq. 12–14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.19437)). The gradient of
$\mathcal{L}_{\text{LM}}$ flows back through $g_{i,t}$ to $s_{i,t}$ for the selected experts;
unselected experts receive no gradient through this token.

**Load-imbalance measurement.** Let $f_i \in [0, 1]$ denote the fraction of tokens in a batch
of size $N$ routed to expert $i$, i.e., $f_i = \tfrac{1}{N} \sum_t \mathbb{1}[i \in \mathcal{T}_t]$.
The mean utilization is $\bar{f} = K / E$ under top-$K$ routing (each token contributes $K$
selections distributed across $E$ experts). Wang et al. measure imbalance with the maximum
violation,

$$
\mathrm{MaxVio} = \frac{\max_i f_i - \bar{f}}{\bar{f}},
$$

([Wang et al. 2024, Definition 1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.15664)). A perfectly balanced
routing has $\mathrm{MaxVio} = 0$; the worst case is $\mathrm{MaxVio} = E/K - 1$. The
auxiliary-loss objective minimizes a smooth proxy of this quantity; the aux-loss-free scheme
will attack $\mathrm{MaxVio}$ directly via the bias.

**The bias at top-K time.** Introduce a per-expert scalar $b_i \in \mathbb{R}$, initialized to
zero. Modify the top-$K$ selection — and *only* the top-$K$ selection — to use the biased
score $s_{i,t} + b_i$:

$$
\mathcal{T}_t = \mathrm{TopK}\bigl(\{s_{i,t} + b_i\}_{i=1}^{E},\, K\bigr).
$$

The gate weights $g_{i,t}$ in the output combination are still computed from the *unbiased*
scores $s_{i,t}$, normalized over $\mathcal{T}_t$
([Wang et al. 2024 eq. 4–5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.15664)). The bias changes which set of
$K$ experts processes the token; once the set is chosen, the model's expressive output is the
same as it would have been without the bias, given that selection.

**Why the LM gradient is preserved.** The TopK operator is already non-differentiable — gradient
implementations of MoE treat the selection $\mathcal{T}_t$ as a stop-gradient categorical and
backpropagate only through the $g_{i,t}$ weights of the selected experts
([Shazeer et al. 2017 §3](https://arxiv.org/abs/1701.06538)). Adding $b_i$ inside the
non-differentiable TopK therefore does not introduce a new backward path: there is no
$\partial \mathcal{L}_{\text{LM}} / \partial b_i$. Because $b_i$ does not appear in the gate
weights $g_{i,t}$ either, the partial derivative of every other quantity in
$\mathrm{MoE}(\mathbf{x}_t)$ with respect to $b_i$ is exactly zero. The LM gradient
$\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}_{\text{LM}}$ for the gate parameters $W_g$ is identical to what an
unbiased gate with the same realized selection would have produced — selection effects are
absorbed into the data-distribution side, not into the gradient signal.

**The closed-form update rule.** Because the LM loss carries no gradient information about
$b_i$, the bias is updated by an external rule. Define the load gap $e_i = \bar{f} - f_i$
measured per training step. Wang et al. propose the discrete-sign rule

$$
b_i \leftarrow b_i + u \cdot \mathrm{sign}(e_i),
$$

with step size $u > 0$ (Wang et al. report $u = 10^{-3}$ at 1B scale; DeepSeek-V3 §3.4 uses
$\gamma = 10^{-3}$ at 671B scale with the same form
[DeepSeek-V3 §3.4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.19437)). The sign-only variant is preferred over
the proportional $b_i \leftarrow b_i + u \cdot e_i$ because the magnitude of $e_i$ varies
wildly with batch composition, while the sign is a stable per-step signal of "which direction
to nudge."

**Stability and convergence argument.** Treat $b_i$ as a control input acting on a load
process $f_i(b)$. Two structural facts ensure the loop is stable.

1. **Monotonicity.** For fixed gate scores $s_{i,t}$, increasing $b_i$ weakly increases
   $f_i$: the expert moves higher in more tokens' TopK rankings, never lower. So
   $\partial f_i / \partial b_i \geq 0$.
2. **Bounded step.** Because the update is $\pm u$, the bias trajectory $\{b_i^{(s)}\}_s$ has
   bounded one-step variation, so the load $f_i$ cannot oscillate by more than a
   per-step-bounded amount even under adversarial gate drift.

Together, monotonicity plus a small fixed step size make the update a contraction toward the
fixed point $f_i = \bar{f}$ in the deterministic limit. Wang et al. §3.3 verify empirically
that $\mathrm{MaxVio}$ decays roughly exponentially over the first ~5K steps and stays
within 5% of zero for the rest of training. In practice the loop is robust to a $10\times$
range of $u$ ([Wang et al. §3.3, Figure 4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.15664)): too small and
balancing is slow at startup; too large and the bias oscillates around zero without harming
final quality.

**One subtlety: the bias is not absorbed into $W_g$.** A reader might ask why the per-expert
bias $b_i$ is not just folded into a bias row of $W_g$ and learned jointly with the rest of
the gate. The answer is the gradient-conflict argument from § 1: if $b_i$ were learned via
$\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}_{\text{LM}}$, it would drift toward whatever value maximizes
next-token likelihood, which is generally *not* balanced load. The point of the closed-form
rule is precisely to decouple $b_i$ from the LM gradient.

**Complementary sequence-level penalty (V3).** DeepSeek-V3 §3.4 reports that the bias alone
prevents per-batch maximum violations but can still permit within-sequence imbalance — long
sequences where the same expert is chosen repeatedly within a single example. V3 adds a
*sequence-wise* balance loss with a very small coefficient ($\alpha = 10^{-4}$) as a backstop:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{bal}}^{\text{seq}} = \alpha \cdot \sum_{i=1}^{E} f_i^{(\text{seq})} \cdot P_i^{(\text{seq})},
$$

evaluated per sequence rather than per batch
([DeepSeek-V3 §3.4, eq. 17–19](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.19437)). The coefficient is small
enough that V3's authors describe it as "complementary to the bias-update mechanism" rather
than a primary balancing signal.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 3 · Reference implementation</p>
## Sketch

```python
# Shapes: B batch, T tokens, E experts, K top-K, d model dim.
# State: b is a per-expert bias buffer (not a Parameter — no autograd).

def aux_loss_free_moe(x, W_g, experts, b, K=2, u=1e-3):
    # 1. Routing scores from the gate.
    logits   = x @ W_g.T                                # [B, T, E]
    s        = logits.sigmoid()                         # [B, T, E]   <- DeepSeek-V3 uses sigmoid

    # 2. Bias enters at top-K selection only. detach() makes the
    #    addition explicit-non-differentiable in case b ever becomes a Tensor.
    biased   = s + b.detach()[None, None, :]            # [B, T, E]
    topk_idx = biased.topk(K, dim=-1).indices           # [B, T, K]

    # 3. Gate weights come from the UNBIASED scores, renormalized over the K chosen experts.
    s_sel    = s.gather(-1, topk_idx)                   # [B, T, K]
    g        = s_sel / s_sel.sum(-1, keepdim=True)      # [B, T, K]   <- output combination weights

    # 4. Standard dispatch & combine. The model's expressive output is independent of b
    #    given a fixed selection, because b never appears past the topk call.
    y = dispatch_combine(x, experts, topk_idx, g)       # [B, T, d]

    # 5. Closed-form bias update — runs outside the autograd graph.
    #    f_i = fraction of token-slots that landed on expert i in this batch.
    with torch.no_grad():
        counts = scatter_count(topk_idx, num_experts=b.shape[0])   # [E]
        f      = counts / counts.sum()                              # [E]   load distribution
        f_bar  = K / b.shape[0]                                     # target = K/E
        b.add_(u * (f_bar - f).sign())                              # b_i += u * sign(e_i)
    return y
```

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 4 · Empirical evidence</p>
## Ablations, perplexity, and the unanswered downstream question

**Introducing-paper ablations (Wang et al. 2024).** The paper's main experiments train a 1B
total / ~150M activated MoE on 100B tokens and a 3B total / ~500M activated MoE on the same
budget, comparing three regimes: no balancing (LM loss only), standard auxiliary loss with the
Switch coefficient $\alpha$, and the bias-update scheme
([Wang et al. 2024, Table 2 and Table 3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.15664)). On the 1B model
the aux-loss-free run reports validation perplexity 7.86 vs. 7.95 for the tuned aux-loss
baseline and 7.83 for an LM-loss-only run that has $\mathrm{MaxVio}$ in the hundreds (i.e.,
mostly dead experts). The aux-loss-free run reaches $\mathrm{MaxVio} < 0.05$ within the first
~5K steps and stays there; the aux-loss baseline holds $\mathrm{MaxVio} \approx 0.1$ at the
$\alpha$ that minimizes perplexity. The 3B-scale Table 3 reproduces the ordering with similar
gaps.

**Drop-rate.** Wang et al. §4.2 also report token-drop rate when a fixed expert capacity
$C = 1.1$ is enforced (the Switch-style overflow policy). The aux-loss-free scheme drops
0.5–0.8% of tokens at steady state versus 1.4–2.0% for the tuned aux-loss baseline at the
same capacity — consistent with the lower MaxVio.

**DeepSeek-V3 confirmation at frontier scale.** DeepSeek-V3 §3.4 is the only public reproduction
at frontier-scale (671B total, 37B active, 14.8T training tokens, 256 routed experts × 61
layers). The V3 report's Table 5 ablates the bias-update scheme against an aux-loss baseline
on a smaller proxy run and reports the aux-loss-free configuration matching or slightly beating
the baseline on Pile validation perplexity and on a panel of zero-shot evaluations including
BBH, MMLU, and HumanEval ([DeepSeek-V3 Table 5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.19437)). The full
671B training uses $\gamma = 10^{-3}$ for the first 14.3T tokens and then $\gamma = 0$ for the
final 500B tokens (an annealing phase that freezes the bias). The V3 authors describe the
bias-update scheme as one of two routing modifications — the other being a sigmoid replacing
softmax in the gate — that they credit with V3's MoE stability at this scale.

**Carry-over to V3.1 and V3.2.** [DeepSeek-V3.1 model card](https://huggingface.co/deepseek-ai/DeepSeek-V3.1)
and [V3.2-Exp model card](https://huggingface.co/deepseek-ai/DeepSeek-V3.2-Exp) both retain the
V3 routing configuration unchanged; V3.2 only modifies the attention stack (adding the DeepSeek
Sparse Attention layer). No re-ablation against an aux-loss baseline is published at V3.1 or
V3.2 scale; the lineage is "kept because V3 worked."

**Independent reproductions.** Adopters outside DeepSeek are documented but limited as of
2026-05-12. [Hunyuan-Large §3.1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.02265) (Tencent, 389B MoE, 52B
active) adopts the aux-loss-free scheme citing Wang et al. directly and reports comparable
load-balance behavior; no head-to-head ablation against an aux-loss baseline is provided in
the Hunyuan report. Other open MoE families — [Mixtral 8x22B](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.04088),
[OLMoE](https://arxiv.org/abs/2409.02060), [Qwen2-MoE](https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.10671) — use
the standard auxiliary-loss formulation, so no comparable scale-matched aux-loss-free vs.
aux-loss comparison exists outside the DeepSeek line.

**Does perplexity equivalence imply downstream-task equivalence?** This is the open question
the published evidence does not settle. Wang et al. measure validation perplexity and one
benchmark panel at 3B scale; DeepSeek-V3 measures the same at 671B. Both report that the
aux-loss-free scheme is at-worst a wash and modestly better. But the literature on perplexity
as a predictor of downstream behavior is mixed
([Liu et al. 2023, "Same Pre-training Loss, Better Downstream"](https://arxiv.org/abs/2301.10472))
and small absolute perplexity gaps in MoE training do not always cash out in downstream
ability the way they do in dense models. No public study isolates which downstream behaviors
(reasoning chain quality, multilinguality, long-tail recall) are sensitive to the routing
balance method at fixed capacity, and the routing imbalance distribution at long context is
not analyzed in any of the cited reports. The honest answer to "should you switch your
production MoE from aux-loss to aux-loss-free?" is: at DeepSeek's scale the production
evidence is positive; at other scales there is no public ablation to cite, and "I don't know"
is the correct response.

<Summary>
**Aux-loss-free routing in one breath.** Maintain a per-expert scalar bias $b_i$ added at
top-$K$ time only; combine selected expert outputs with the *unbiased* gate weights. Update
$b_i$ by $u \cdot \mathrm{sign}(\bar{f} - f_i)$ outside the autograd graph each step. The LM
gradient is unmodified, $\mathrm{MaxVio}$ collapses within ~5K steps, and validation
perplexity matches or beats the tuned aux-loss baseline at 1B–671B scale. Production-deployed
in DeepSeek V3 / V3.1 / V3.2 and Hunyuan-Large; no independent scale-matched ablation outside
those families.
</Summary>
