<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 1 · Premise</p>
## Parameter scaling without FLOP scaling

A dense transformer with hidden size $d$ and FFN intermediate size $d_{ff}=4d$ spends
$\approx 8\,d^{2}$ FLOPs per token on the FFN. Doubling $d_{ff}$ doubles both the parameter
count and the per-token FLOPs — capacity and compute are welded together. Mixture-of-Experts
(MoE) breaks that coupling. A Switch Transformer layer with $N$ expert FFNs of width $d_{ff}$
and top-$K$ routing has $N$ times the FFN parameters but only $K$ times the per-token FFN
FLOPs (Fedus et al., 2022, [arXiv:2101.03961](https://arxiv.org/abs/2101.03961), §2). Holding
$K=1$ or $K=2$ fixed, one can scale total parameters by an order of magnitude while keeping
the FLOP budget of a much smaller dense model. This is the lever every modern frontier
open-weights release pulls: Mixtral 8×7B at 47B total / 13B active
([arXiv:2401.04088](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.04088) §2.1), DeepSeek-V3 at 671B total / 37B
active ([arXiv:2412.19437](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.19437) §2), Qwen3-Next 80B at 80B total
/ 3B active.

Two failure modes plague the vanilla recipe. First, *coarse granularity*: GShard
([arXiv:2006.16668](https://arxiv.org/abs/2006.16668) §3.2) and Switch use a small expert
count ($N=8$ to $128$) with each expert sized to a full FFN. With $K=2$ of $N=8$, the gate
chooses among only $\binom{8}{2}=28$ expert combinations per token — coarse, and each expert
must absorb a broad slice of input space. A single Mixtral expert is forced to cover Python
syntax, German morphology, and basic arithmetic in the same parameters
([arXiv:2401.04088](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.04088) §3.4 reports per-domain routing
distributions that are nearly uniform rather than peaked — consistent with experts that have
not specialized). Second, *redundant common knowledge*: tokens that need basic linguistic
competence (almost every token) route through different experts depending on minor surface
features, so every expert ends up re-learning the same common weights. The auxiliary
load-balancing loss compounds the problem by actively pushing the gate toward uniform
routing even when uniformity is not what the task wants
([arXiv:2101.03961](https://arxiv.org/abs/2101.03961) §2.2).

The lineage so far: Shazeer et al. ([arXiv:1701.06538](https://arxiv.org/abs/1701.06538))
introduced sparsely-gated MoE for LSTMs; GShard scaled it to transformers with the standard
top-2 routing recipe; Switch Transformer pushed to top-1 to simplify dispatch and balance;
Mixtral demonstrated the recipe at frontier open-weights quality with $N=8$, top-2. All four
share the same coarse granularity, leaving the specialization-vs-redundancy tension
unresolved.

DeepSeekMoE ([arXiv:2401.06066](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.06066)) makes two coupled changes
to the layer — finer experts at higher top-$K$, plus a small number of always-on shared
experts — and shows in 2B / 16B / 145B ablations that the resulting layer reaches the loss
of a vanilla MoE with roughly $1.5\times$ the activated compute.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 2 · Derivation</p>
## From top-K MoE to fine-grained + shared

**Start: vanilla top-$K$ MoE.** Let $\mathbf{u}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$ be the input to the MoE
layer for token $t$ (the post-attention residual stream), $N$ the number of routed experts,
and $K$ the number activated per token. Each expert $\mathrm{FFN}_i$ is a full-width FFN with
intermediate size $d_{ff}$. The gate produces affinity scores $s_{i,t} = \mathrm{Softmax}_i(\mathbf{u}_t^{\top}\mathbf{e}_i)$
over expert centroids $\mathbf{e}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$, and the layer output is

$$
\mathbf{h}_t \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{N} g_{i,t}\,\mathrm{FFN}_i(\mathbf{u}_t) \;+\; \mathbf{u}_t,
\qquad
g_{i,t} \;=\;
\begin{cases}
 s_{i,t}, & s_{i,t} \in \mathrm{Top\text{-}K}\big(\{s_{j,t}\}_{j=1}^{N}\big),\\
 0, & \text{otherwise.}
\end{cases}
\tag{1}
$$

Only $K$ of the $g_{i,t}$ are non-zero per token, so the per-token FFN FLOP cost is
$K \cdot \mathcal{O}(d\,d_{ff})$ regardless of $N$. Total FFN parameters are $N \cdot 2\,d\,d_{ff}$
(two matrices per FFN, ignoring biases and the gate variant; SwiGLU adds a third). Activated
parameters per token are $K \cdot 2\,d\,d_{ff}$. This is the form Switch Transformer ($K=1$),
GShard, and Mixtral ($K=2, N=8$) all instantiate.

**Move 1: fine-grained expert segmentation.** Replace each of the $N$ experts with $m$
slimmer copies whose intermediate size is $d_{ff}/m$. The new expert count is $mN$, and the
top-$K$ is raised to $mK$. The layer becomes (DeepSeekMoE §3.1, Eq. 6–8):

$$
\mathbf{h}_t \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{mN} g_{i,t}\,\mathrm{FFN}_i(\mathbf{u}_t) \;+\; \mathbf{u}_t,
\qquad
g_{i,t} \;=\;
\begin{cases}
 s_{i,t}, & s_{i,t} \in \mathrm{Top\text{-}}mK\big(\{s_{j,t}\}_{j=1}^{mN}\big),\\
 0, & \text{otherwise.}
\end{cases}
\tag{2}
$$

The key invariant: activated parameters per token are unchanged. Each fine expert has
intermediate size $d_{ff}/m$ and so contributes $2\,d\,d_{ff}/m$ parameters; $mK$ of them
fire; total activated $= mK \cdot 2\,d\,d_{ff}/m = K \cdot 2\,d\,d_{ff}$, identical to (1).
Total parameters are likewise unchanged at $N \cdot 2\,d\,d_{ff}$. What changes is the
*combinatorial flexibility* of routing. Switch Transformer / GShard with $K=2$ of $N=16$
offers $\binom{16}{2}=120$ possible expert subsets; raising to $mK=8$ of $mN=64$ (i.e.,
$m=4$) offers $\binom{64}{8} \approx 4.4 \times 10^{9}$ subsets
([arXiv:2401.06066](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.06066) §3.1). The gate now has the addressing
resolution to assign each narrow concept (Python syntax, German noun gender, basic
arithmetic) to its own small expert.

**Why not just raise $K$ without segmentation?** Because that costs FLOPs linearly: top-$8$
of $N=16$ activates $8\times$ the FFN compute of top-$2$. Segmentation buys finer routing at
no FLOP increase.

**Why not just raise $N$ without raising $K$?** Adding more large experts at fixed $K$ shrinks
the chance each expert gets enough tokens to train on, and does not give the gate more *per-token*
addressing resolution (still picks $K$ of $N$). Segmentation gives finer per-token resolution.

**Move 2: shared expert isolation.** Reserve $K_s$ of the fine experts as *shared*: they
bypass the gate and fire on every token. The remaining $mN - K_s$ are routed, and the gate
picks $mK - K_s$ of them. The layer becomes (DeepSeekMoE §3.2, Eq. 9–11):

$$
\mathbf{h}_t \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{K_s}\mathrm{FFN}_i(\mathbf{u}_t)
\;+\; \sum_{i=K_s+1}^{mN} g_{i,t}\,\mathrm{FFN}_i(\mathbf{u}_t) \;+\; \mathbf{u}_t,
\tag{3}
$$

with $g_{i,t}$ as in (2) but with $\mathrm{Top\text{-}}(mK - K_s)$ over the routed pool only.
The activated parameter budget stays $mK \cdot 2\,d\,d_{ff}/m = 2K\,d\,d_{ff}$ — same as
vanilla — because we shifted $K_s$ of the activations from "routed and learned" to "always-on
and shared." Setting $K_s = 1$ or $K_s = 2$ is what production configurations use; the
DeepSeek-V2 layer has $K_s = 2, mN-K_s = 160, mK-K_s = 6$
([arXiv:2405.04434](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.04434) §2.1), DeepSeek-V3 has
$K_s = 1, mN-K_s = 256, mK-K_s = 8$ ([arXiv:2412.19437](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.19437) §2).

**Why a shared expert and not "just train the gate harder"?** The argument is
information-theoretic. Common-knowledge weights are needed by *every* token; any routing
decision that sometimes sends a token away from those weights forces the gate to spend
capacity re-learning them in multiple experts. Reserving $K_s$ experts that always fire
removes the routing decision entirely for that fraction of the layer, freeing the routed
experts to specialize on input-specific features. The probing study in §4 shows this matters:
disabling the single shared expert in DeepSeekMoE 2B and replacing it with one extra routed
slot raises Pile loss from $1.808$ to $2.414$
([arXiv:2401.06066](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.06066) §4.5).

**Load balance.** Without intervention, the gate can collapse to a few favored experts. The
paper adds two auxiliary losses (§3.3): an expert-level balance loss
$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ExpBal}} = \alpha_1 \sum_{i=1}^{N'} f_i P_i$ (Eq. 12) where $f_i$ is the
fraction of tokens routed to expert $i$ and $P_i$ is the mean gate probability assigned to
it, and a device-level balance loss
$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{DevBal}} = \alpha_2 \sum_{i=1}^{D} f'_i P'_i$ (Eq. 15) that balances
load across device groups rather than individual experts. Successor work
([aux-loss-free MoE](https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.15664), DeepSeek-V3 §3.4) replaces both with
a per-expert bias that floats up when an expert is under-loaded and down when over-loaded —
gradient-free balancing at no expressivity cost.

**Final budget.** With $N'_r = mN - K_s$ routed experts and $K'_r = mK - K_s$ activated:
total FFN params $= mN \cdot 2\,d\,d_{ff}/m = 2N\,d\,d_{ff}$; activated FFN params per token
$= (K_s + K'_r) \cdot 2\,d\,d_{ff}/m = 2K\,d\,d_{ff}$. The total/activated ratio is $N/K$ — the
same lever as before — but the routing decision now happens at a finer grain over a larger pool.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 3 · Reference implementation</p>
## A DeepSeekMoE layer in sketch form

```python
def deepseek_moe_layer(u, routed_experts, shared_experts, gate, K_r):
    # u: [B, T, d]
    # routed_experts: list of (mN - K_s) fine-grained FFNs, each d_ff/m wide
    # shared_experts: list of K_s always-on fine-grained FFNs
    # gate: linear d -> (mN - K_s); K_r = mK - K_s

    # --- shared lane: every token sees every shared expert ---
    h_shared = sum(f(u) for f in shared_experts)        # [B, T, d]

    # --- routed lane: top-K_r selection over the routed pool ---
    scores = softmax(gate(u), dim=-1)                   # [B, T, mN - K_s]
    topk_w, topk_idx = scores.topk(K_r, dim=-1)         # [B, T, K_r], [B, T, K_r]
    # the paper keeps raw softmax weights (no re-normalization); some variants renormalize

    # dispatch: gather each token's K_r expert outputs and combine
    h_routed = dispatch_and_combine(u, routed_experts, topk_idx, topk_w)  # [B, T, d]

    return u + h_shared + h_routed                      # residual add
```

`dispatch_and_combine` is the all-to-all permutation that groups tokens by chosen expert,
runs each expert on its assigned slice, and scatters the outputs back, weighted by `topk_w`.
The mechanics of that permutation are the engineering bulk of any MoE implementation but are
orthogonal to the algorithmic claim being illustrated here.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 4 · Empirical evidence</p>
## How much the two changes buy

**2B-scale validation (Table 1).** The introducing paper trains a $2.0$B-total / $0.3$B-active
DeepSeekMoE configuration ($1$ shared expert, $63$ routed at $1/4$ FFN width, top-$7$ routed)
against a same-budget GShard 2B with $16$ standard-width experts at top-$2$. On Pile validation
loss DeepSeekMoE reaches $1.808$ versus GShard's $1.867$, and on downstream zero-shot
benchmarks: HellaSwag $54.8\%$ vs $50.5\%$, PIQA $72.3\%$ vs $70.6\%$, ARC-challenge $34.3\%$
vs $31.6\%$, TriviaQA $16.6\%$ vs $10.2\%$
([arXiv:2401.06066](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.06066) Table 1, §4.1). Table 2 compares against
a "GShard$\times 1.5$" baseline at $2.9$B total / $0.45$B activated — DeepSeekMoE 2B matches it
roughly, meaning the recipe gets the loss of a 1.5$\times$-larger vanilla MoE at the same
activated compute.

**Expert specialization probes (§4.5).** Two ablations isolate where the gain comes from. (a)
*Disable top routed experts at inference.* The Pile loss curve when knocking out the highest-
ranked routed experts rises faster for DeepSeekMoE than for GShard$\times 1.5$, indicating
each routed expert in DeepSeekMoE is less replaceable by its peers — lower parameter
redundancy. (b) *Disable the shared expert.* Setting $K_s = 0$ while activating one extra
routed slot raises Pile loss from $1.808$ to $2.414$, a much larger jump than knocking out a
routed expert. The shared expert holds high-value common-knowledge weights that routed
experts cannot reproduce on demand ([arXiv:2401.06066](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.06066) §4.5).

**16B-scale follow-through (Tables 3–4).** The $16.4$B-total / $2.8$B-active DeepSeekMoE
model, trained on 2T tokens, matches a fully dense DeepSeek 7B on Pile (BPB $0.74$ vs $0.75$)
at $40.5\%$ of training FLOPs, and out-performs LLaMA-2 7B on majority of benchmarks at
$39.6\%$ of LLaMA's training compute, including HumanEval $26.8\%$ vs $14.6\%$ and GSM8K
$18.8\%$ vs $15.5\%$ ([arXiv:2401.06066](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.06066) §5, Tables 3–4).

**145B-scale extension (Table 6).** A $144.6$B-total / $22.2$B-active DeepSeekMoE 145B
trained on only $245$B tokens reaches Pile loss $1.876$ versus DeepSeek 67B dense at $1.905$,
at $28.5\%$ of the dense FLOP budget; a half-activated variant at $12.2$B active matches
DeepSeek 67B at $18.2\%$ of compute ([arXiv:2401.06066](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.06066) §7,
Table 6). MMLU lags ($39.4\%$ vs $45.1\%$) — the paper attributes this to the smaller
attention parameter count rather than the MoE recipe.

**Production adoption.** DeepSeek-V2 (236B total / 21B active) keeps the recipe with
$N_r = 162$ routed, $K_s = 2$ shared, top-$6$ routing
([arXiv:2405.04434](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.04434) §2.1). DeepSeek-V3 (671B total / 37B
active) scales to $N_r = 256$ routed, $K_s = 1$ shared, top-$8$ routing, and switches the
balance mechanism to the aux-loss-free bias scheme; it reports a near-monotone improvement on
downstream loss versus a same-FLOP V2 baseline
([arXiv:2412.19437](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.19437) §2, §3.4). DeepSeek-V2 §2.1 also runs an
internal Mixtral-style baseline (8 large experts, top-2, same total parameters and same
training tokens) and reports the DeepSeekMoE variant winning on validation loss by a
non-trivial margin — the cleanest like-for-like Mixtral-vs-DeepSeekMoE comparison currently
in the public literature. The recipe has since been adopted by GLM-4.5
([arXiv:2508.06471](https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.06471)), MiniMax-01
([arXiv:2501.08313](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.08313)), Hunyuan-Large
([arXiv:2411.02265](https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.02265) §3) and Qwen3-Next — independent
adoptions but not independent ablations. No external peer-reviewed reproduction of the
2B-scale ablation has been published; the strongest external evidence remains the production
scaling results above.

**DeepSeekMoE in one breath.** Split each big expert into $m$ slim experts at top-$mK$ — same
activated FLOPs, $10^{7}\times$ more routing combinations, sharper specialization. Reserve
$K_s$ always-on shared experts for common-knowledge work so routed experts stop re-learning

$40\%$ of training compute; production V2/V3 keep the recipe.
