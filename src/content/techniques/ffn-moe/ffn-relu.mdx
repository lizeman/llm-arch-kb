<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 1 · Premise</p>
## The original FFN

The 2017 transformer's feed-forward sublayer is a two-layer MLP applied independently at each
token position. The architecture is:

$$\mathrm{FFN}_{\text{ReLU}}(\mathbf{x}) = \max(0, \mathbf{x}W_1 + \mathbf{b}_1)\, W_2 + \mathbf{b}_2.$$

Two linear projections, a ReLU between them, and an additive bias on each. The hidden
dimension $d_{\text{ff}}$ is chosen as $4 \cdot d_{\text{model}}$ — large enough that the ReLU
gate creates a useful sparse representation, small enough to keep the FFN's parameter count
balanced against the attention block.

```python
def ffn_relu(x, W1, b1, W2, b2):
    # x: [..., d_model]
    # W1: [d_model, d_ff],  W2: [d_ff, d_model]
    return torch.relu(x @ W1 + b1) @ W2 + b2
```

This is the FFN of the original transformer (Vaswani et al. 2017, §3.3). Every transformer
variant that came after — encoder-decoder translation, BERT, GPT-1 through 3, T5 — used a
close cousin, varying the activation but keeping the two-layer-with-nonlinearity shape.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 2 · What ReLU got right</p>
## Sparsity, simplicity, throughput

ReLU's appeal at the time:

- **Computationally trivial.** A single comparison plus a select. No exponentials, no
  divisions. Faster on every hardware target than tanh, sigmoid, or GELU.
- **Sparse activations.** Roughly half of FFN hidden units are zero on any given input,
  matching empirical observations about sparse representations being learnable and useful.
- **Gradient simplicity.** Gradient is exactly 1 for active units, exactly 0 for inactive — no
  gradient saturation issues that plagued tanh-based MLPs.
- **Empirical match to softmax-attention dynamics.** ReLU FFN trains stably with the standard
  Adam-derived optimizers from 2017, no special initialization tricks needed.

The original transformer's success made the ReLU FFN a kind of unstated default for the
2017–2020 generation of language models.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 3 · What displaced it</p>
## GELU and the GLU variants

Two trends pushed FFN-ReLU out of the modern stack:

- **[GELU](/llm-arch-kb/ffn-moe/gelu/)** (Gaussian Error Linear Unit, Hendrycks & Gimpel 2016)
  smooths the ReLU corner, which empirical work showed gives ~0.5 perplexity points improvement
  on language modeling at matched compute. BERT, GPT-2, T5, and GPT-3 all chose GELU.
- **[SwiGLU](/llm-arch-kb/ffn-moe/swiglu/) and [GeGLU](/llm-arch-kb/ffn-moe/geglu/)** (Shazeer
  2020) replace the two-projection MLP with a three-projection bilinear gated form, which gives
  another ~0.5 perplexity points at matched FLOPs. From Llama 1 onward this is the open-stack
  default.

By 2024 the Llama family, DeepSeek, Mistral, OLMo, and others had all moved to a GLU variant.
ReLU FFN persists in older models (GPT-2/3 era encoders), in some specialized fast-inference
deployments where the ReLU savings matter, and as a baseline in ablation studies.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 4 · The hidden-dim convention</p>
## Why d_ff = 4 · d_model

The original paper's $4\times$ ratio was empirically chosen — the FFN at $4\times$ matches the
attention block's parameter count to within a constant factor at standard model widths,
keeping the per-block compute roughly balanced between attention and FFN. Subsequent variants
have adjusted this ratio: SwiGLU uses $\frac{8}{3} \cdot d_{\text{model}}$ (the third
projection compensates), Mixtral's MoE uses larger ratios per expert because only a few
experts activate per token, and so on. The $4\times$ convention itself is deprecated in modern
designs but lingers in some open implementations of older models.

<Summary>
**FFN-ReLU in one breath.** Two linear projections at $4\times$ hidden expansion with ReLU
between them, plus additive biases. The original 2017 transformer FFN. Displaced by GELU
(smoother) and then by SwiGLU/GeGLU (gated bilinear) for ~1 perplexity point of gain in total.
Still seen in older models and as an ablation baseline; not present in the modern open frontier
stack.
</Summary>
