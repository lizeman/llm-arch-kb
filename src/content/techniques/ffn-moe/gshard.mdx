<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 1 · Premise</p>
## Before MoE worked at scale

The Shazeer et al. 2017 Sparse MoE paper had shown sparse top-K routing could work in language
modeling but only at modest scales (~5B parameters). Pushing past that required answering two
hard infrastructure questions:

1. **How do you shard hundreds of experts across hardware?** Each expert lives on a different
   device; tokens have to be routed across the network and the resulting traffic patterns are
   irregular.
2. **How do you handle the dispatch step itself?** Different tokens go to different experts;
   the per-expert input is variable-length; standard tensor ops require fixed shapes.

GShard answered both, and demonstrated 600B-parameter sparse-MoE models on multilingual
translation. The infrastructure plumbing it introduced — `dispatch_tensor`, `combine_tensor`,
`expert_capacity` — is still the conceptual basis for every distributed-MoE training framework.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 2 · Top-2 routing</p>
## The recipe Switch later simplified

For each token, GShard computes gate logits $g(\mathbf{x}) \in \mathbb{R}^E$ over $E$ experts.
The top-2 indices are selected, normalized, and the token is routed to both:

$$\mathrm{out}(\mathbf{x}) = g_{i^*}(\mathbf{x}) \cdot f_{i^*}(\mathbf{x}) + g_{j^*}(\mathbf{x}) \cdot f_{j^*}(\mathbf{x}),$$

where $(i^*, j^*) = \mathrm{top2}(g)$. The two outputs are weighted by their normalized gate
values and summed.

The reasoning behind top-2 rather than top-1 (which Switch later argued was enough): the second
expert gives the gating network gradient signal even when the first choice is dominant. If the
gate routes everything to expert A and never to B, B's weights cannot adjust to be the right
"runner up" for future tokens. Top-2 keeps the gate's choices reversible.

```python
def gshard_layer(x, experts, gate, expert_capacity):
    logits = gate(x)                          # [B, T, E]
    top2_logits, top2_idx = logits.topk(2, dim=-1)
    weights = top2_logits.softmax(-1)         # [B, T, 2]
    # Each token goes to two experts; capacity policy applies per expert
    return dispatch_and_combine(x, experts, top2_idx, weights, expert_capacity)
```

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 3 · Expert capacity and token dropping</p>
## What to do when an expert is full

A natural problem with top-K routing: if expert $i$ is the top choice for too many tokens in a
batch, dispatching all of them to one device creates load imbalance and OOM risk. GShard's fix:
**expert capacity** $C = \alpha \cdot T / E$, where $T$ is batch size, $E$ is expert count, and
$\alpha \approx 1.5$. Each expert handles at most $C$ tokens per batch.

When an expert is at capacity, additional tokens are **dropped** — their MoE-layer output is the
residual stream unchanged. The dropping is a deterministic policy based on the order tokens
arrive at the expert dispatch; later GShard variants randomize the order to prevent positional
bias.

A small auxiliary load-balance loss pushes the gate toward uniform expert utilization (Switch
later formalized this loss, eq. 4 of Switch).

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 4 · Results</p>
## Empirical evidence

GShard's flagship was a 600B-parameter multilingual translation model spanning 100 language
pairs. It trained on 2048 TPU-v3 cores and achieved better quality than dense baselines at
matched compute (Lepikhin et al. Table 4): +0.9 BLEU averaged across 100 language pairs vs a
matched-FLOP dense T5.

The architectural details did not survive directly into modern decoder-only MoEs (which started
with Switch's top-1 then re-introduced top-2 in Mixtral). What survived:

- **Expert capacity as a first-class hyperparameter.**
- **The dispatch / combine tensor abstraction**, now generalized in libraries like
  Megatron-DeepSpeed and Tutel.
- **The per-token load-balance loss** that Switch refined.
- **The top-K routing structure**, with K = 1 (Switch), K = 2 (GShard, Mixtral), or K = 8
  (DeepSeekMoE) all variants of the same dispatch.

GShard is therefore the conceptual root of every entry in this knowledge base's MoE category.
