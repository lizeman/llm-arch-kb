<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 1 · Premise</p>
## Production MoE before DeepSeekMoE

The 2017–2023 MoE literature (Sparse MoE, Switch Transformer, GShard, ST-MoE) had largely
converged on a recipe: 8–32 full-sized FFN experts per layer, top-1 or top-2 routing, an
auxiliary balance loss. Mixtral 8x7B (Jiang et al. 2024) shipped this recipe at production
quality, open-weights, with a clean MIT license — and it became the default mental model for
"how MoE works" for an entire wave of practitioners.

The architecture is intentionally not novel. Its contribution was operational: showing the
8-expert, top-2 design *worked* at the 7B-active scale and could be deployed with off-the-shelf
tooling.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 2 · The layer</p>
## Eight experts, top-2, one balance loss

Each MoE layer replaces the dense FFN with $E = 8$ SwiGLU-style experts of the same size as a
normal FFN. A small gating network produces logits over the 8; the top 2 are activated:

$$\mathrm{MoE}(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{i \in \mathrm{top2}(g(\mathbf{x}))} g_i(\mathbf{x})\,f_i(\mathbf{x}),\quad g_i = \frac{e^{l_i}}{\sum_{j \in \mathrm{top2}} e^{l_j}}.$$

Note the renormalization: the gate softmax is taken *over only the top-K selected experts*, not
over all $E$. This avoids the gradient instability of softmaxing over near-zero entries for the
$E - K$ unused experts.

```python
def mixtral_moe(x, experts, gate, K=2):
    logits = gate(x)                           # [B, T, E]
    topk_logits, topk_idx = logits.topk(K, dim=-1)
    weights = topk_logits.softmax(-1)          # renormalize over K only
    return dispatch_and_combine(x, experts, topk_idx, weights)
```

Auxiliary load-balancing loss (Fedus et al. 2022's Switch Transformer formulation):

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{aux}} = \alpha \cdot E \cdot \sum_i f_i \cdot P_i,$$

where $f_i$ is the fraction of tokens routed to expert $i$ and $P_i$ is the gate's mean
probability for expert $i$. The loss is minimized when both quantities are uniform across
experts.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 3 · The granularity tradeoff</p>
## Coarse, simple, somewhat redundant

Mixtral's design choice — few large experts — has predictable strengths and weaknesses:

**Strengths.** Operationally simple. Eight experts fit cleanly into 8-way expert parallelism on
a single node. Routing decisions are coarse enough that imbalance is rare and the auxiliary
loss is well-conditioned. Implementations exist in vLLM, llama.cpp, HuggingFace Transformers
without bespoke kernels.

**Weaknesses.** Each expert has to cover a large slice of input space, so specialization is
shallow. Common-knowledge work gets redundantly stored across all 8 experts. DeepSeekMoE later
made these costs explicit and proposed the fine-grained-plus-shared-experts fix; see the
[DeepSeekMoE entry](/llm-arch-kb/ffn-moe/deepseek-moe/) for the alternative.

Mixtral 8x7B reports (Table 4 in the paper) matching or beating Llama 2 70B on most benchmarks
at 13B active parameters per token — a ~5× compute reduction at inference. The tradeoff is
total parameter count: 47B in Mixtral vs 70B in dense Llama 2, but the MoE has to be loaded in
full for serving.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 4 · Why no production decoder in v1 uses this exact design</p>
## Where the field went

The Llama series stayed dense. DeepSeek went to fine-grained MoE with shared experts (V2/V3).
Kimi K2 ships a different but similarly fine-grained variant. Gemma 3 is dense. The 8-expert
top-2 design that Mixtral established remains the canonical "intro MoE" recipe but the
production frontier has largely moved past it.

That said, the design's *operational* qualities — simple parallelism, predictable balance,
off-the-shelf tooling — are why it remains a common second-tier choice for open-weights
post-training MoE conversions (where retraining the gating is expensive).

<Summary>
**Mixtral MoE in one breath.** Eight full SwiGLU experts per layer, top-2 routing, standard
auxiliary balance loss. Simple, operationally clean, and the design that taught most
practitioners what production MoE is. DeepSeekMoE's fine-grained-plus-shared variant is
strictly better on quality-per-active-parameter, but Mixtral remains the canonical baseline.
</Summary>
