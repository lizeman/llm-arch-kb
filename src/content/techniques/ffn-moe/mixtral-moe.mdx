<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 1 · Premise</p>
## Production MoE before DeepSeekMoE

A dense Llama-2-style 7B decoder uses ~13 GFLOPs per token at the FFN stage and stores all 7B
parameters in HBM. A GShard / Switch-style MoE replaces the dense FFN with $E$ parallel expert
FFNs and a small gating network that picks the top-$K$ for each token, so activated parameters
scale with $K$ while total parameters scale with $E$ (Shazeer et al. 2017, eq. 2). The
question Mixtral 8x7B (Jiang et al. 2024, [arXiv 2401.04088](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.04088))
takes on is not whether sparse MoE works — Switch (Fedus et al. 2021) had already crossed the
trillion-parameter line and GShard (Lepikhin et al. 2020) had shown encoder-side scaling — but
whether the small-$E$, top-2, full-sized-expert recipe is operationally clean enough to ship
as an open-weights flagship.

The 2017–2023 sparse-MoE literature had converged on a recipe sitting roughly at $E = 8$ to
$E = 32$ FFN-sized experts per layer with top-1 or top-2 routing and an auxiliary balance loss
on the gate (Fedus et al. 2021, §2.2). The variants disagreed on which expert size and which
$K$. Sparse-MoE 2017 used many small LSTM experts; Switch used top-1 over hundreds; GShard
used top-2 over hundreds; ST-MoE (Zoph et al. 2022) used top-2 over 64. Mixtral picks the
coarsest viable point: $E = 8$, $K = 2$, expert size equal to a full Llama 2 FFN. The choice
matters because it determines what hardware layout works and what auxiliary loss schedule
needs tuning.

The Mixtral contribution is not a new primitive. It is an open-weights demonstration that the
$E = 8$, top-2, SwiGLU-expert recipe trains stably at the 13B-active scale, serves on
single-node 8-way expert-parallel hardware without bespoke kernels, and reaches Llama-2-70B
quality at ~5× lower inference compute (Jiang et al. 2024, Table 4). For the basic top-K
gating math and the noisy-top-K trick on which this whole recipe rests, see
[Sparse MoE](/llm-arch-kb/ffn-moe/sparse-moe/); this entry focuses on what is specific to the
Mixtral configuration.

The contribution in one sentence: take the smallest-$E$ top-2 MoE that still gives meaningful
specialization, retrain at production scale, and ship it open-weights under MIT.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 2 · Derivation</p>
## Eight experts, top-2, normalized over selection

Start from the dense Llama-2 FFN at the position-wise layer:

$$\mathrm{FFN}(\mathbf{x}) = W_2\bigl(\mathrm{SiLU}(W_1 \mathbf{x}) \odot W_3 \mathbf{x}\bigr),$$

with $W_1, W_3 \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{ff} \times d_{model}}$ and $W_2 \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{model}
\times d_{ff}}$. Mixtral 8x7B uses $d_{model} = 4096$, $d_{ff} = 14336$ (Jiang et al. 2024,
Table 1). The dense FFN parameter cost per layer is $3 \cdot d_{model} \cdot d_{ff} \approx
176\text{M}$, and the per-token FLOP cost is $\approx 2 \cdot 3 \cdot d_{model} \cdot d_{ff}$.

Mixtral replaces this single FFN with $E = 8$ independent SwiGLU experts $\{f_1, \dots, f_8\}$,
each parameterized exactly like the dense $\mathrm{FFN}$. A linear gate $W_g \in \mathbb{R}^{E
\times d_{model}}$ produces logits $\ell(\mathbf{x}) = W_g \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^E$. The
layer output is

$$\mathrm{MoE}(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{i \in \mathcal{T}(\mathbf{x})} g_i(\mathbf{x}) \cdot f_i(\mathbf{x}),$$

where $\mathcal{T}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathrm{Top}_2\bigl(\ell(\mathbf{x})\bigr)$ is the index set
of the two largest logits and the gate weights $g_i$ are the *renormalized softmax restricted
to the selected pair*:

$$g_i(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{\exp \ell_i(\mathbf{x})}{\sum_{j \in \mathcal{T}(\mathbf{x})} \exp \ell_j(\mathbf{x})},\quad i \in \mathcal{T}(\mathbf{x}).$$

This is the load-bearing difference from Sparse-MoE 2017's gate, which softmaxes over all $E$
logits and then masks the non-top-$K$ entries to zero (Shazeer et al. 2017, eq. 3). The
Mixtral form (Jiang et al. 2024, eq. 4) softmaxes *only over the selected $K$*. The two
choices differ when the gate is poorly calibrated: with $E$-wide softmax, the $K = 2$
post-mask weights can collectively be far below 1, attenuating the expert outputs and forcing
the residual to do the work; with $K$-only softmax, the weights always sum to 1, so the expert
block always contributes its full magnitude to the residual stream. The Mixtral team chose
the second form to keep the residual budget consistent regardless of how spiky the gate is.

Why $E = 8$ and not 64? With $E = 8$ each expert covers a wider slice of input semantics, so
specialization is *coarse*: a single expert ends up handling both code syntax and English
grammar rather than one fine-grained concept each. DeepSeekMoE (Dai et al. 2024,
[arXiv 2401.06066](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.06066), §2.2) measures this directly and shows
the coarse design redundantly stores common-knowledge weights across every expert, since
every token has to hit *some* expert for basic linguistic competence. The Mixtral counter is
operational: $E = 8$ fits cleanly into 8-way expert-parallel hardware, the gate's 8-way
classification is well-conditioned, and the balance loss only needs to push toward a uniform
distribution over 8 buckets — all of which fail more often at $E = 256$.

Why top-2 and not top-1? Top-1 (Switch) gives one bit of routing signal per token and forces
the dispatch to handle a discrete categorical choice with no soft fallback; if expert $i^*$
overflows its capacity, the token is *dropped* (residual passes through unchanged). Top-2
gives a soft second choice that absorbs capacity overflow gracefully — the gate weights $g_1
+ g_2 = 1$ adapt to which of the two experts actually accepts the token. The empirical case
for top-2 over top-1 was made by GShard (Lepikhin et al. 2020, §3) and confirmed at scale by
ST-MoE (Zoph et al. 2022, Table 5), which reports a 0.3–0.6 perplexity point quality gap.

Mixtral inherits the **load-balancing auxiliary loss** from Switch (Fedus et al. 2021, eq. 4),
unchanged in form:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{aux}} = \alpha \cdot E \cdot \sum_{i = 1}^{E} f_i \cdot P_i,$$

where $f_i$ is the fraction of tokens in the batch whose top-2 set contains expert $i$ and
$P_i$ is the gate's mean *softmax probability* (over all $E$, not over the selected pair) for
expert $i$. The product $f_i \cdot P_i$ is jointly minimized when both quantities are uniform
at $1/E$, so the sum is bounded below by $\alpha / E$ and is bounded above by $\alpha$ when
all tokens collapse to one expert. The factor $E$ in front rescales so $\alpha$ has a
consistent meaning across $E$. Mixtral's released code uses $\alpha = 0.02$ (Hugging Face
transformers reference implementation,
[`MixtralSparseMoeBlock`](https://github.com/huggingface/transformers/blob/main/src/transformers/models/mixtral/modeling_mixtral.py)).

The $P_i \cdot f_i$ product, not $P_i^2$ or $f_i^2$ alone, is what makes the loss
gradient-tractable: $f_i$ is a non-differentiable count, but $P_i$ is differentiable in $W_g$,
so the gradient on the gate is $\alpha \cdot E \cdot \sum_i f_i \cdot \nabla_{W_g} P_i$ — a
weighted softmax-cross-entropy-style update that pulls $P_i$ toward $1/E$ proportionally to
how *over*-used expert $i$ currently is. The discrete count $f_i$ acts as a stop-gradient
target. See Fedus et al. 2021, §2.3 for the same derivation.

**Parameter and FLOP accounting.** Per MoE layer Mixtral stores $E \cdot 3 \cdot d_{model}
\cdot d_{ff} + E \cdot d_{model}$ FFN-plus-gate parameters, i.e., $8 \cdot 176\text{M} +
8 \cdot 4096 \approx 1.4\text{B}$. Per token only $K = 2$ experts fire, so the per-token FFN
FLOP cost is $K / E = 1/4$ of the equivalent $E \cdot \mathrm{FFN}$-dense cost, matching the
dense FLOP cost of a 13B-parameter dense FFN. Total parameter footprint: 46.7B across 32
layers (Jiang et al. 2024, Table 1); active per token: 12.9B. The ratio active/total $\approx
0.28$ is the *coarse* end of the MoE spectrum; DeepSeek V3 sits near 0.06 and Kimi K2 near
0.032.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 3 · Reference implementation</p>
## Top-2 with renormalization over the pair

```python
def mixtral_moe(x, experts, gate, K=2):
    # x: [B, T, d_model]   gate.weight: [E, d_model]   experts: list of E SwiGLU FFNs
    logits = gate(x)                                # [B, T, E]
    topk_logits, topk_idx = logits.topk(K, dim=-1)  # [B, T, K]
    # Renormalize softmax over the K selected logits only — not over E
    weights = topk_logits.softmax(-1)               # [B, T, K], sums to 1 along K
    # Dispatch each token to its K experts; combine outputs by weights
    return dispatch_and_combine(x, experts, topk_idx, weights)

def load_balance_loss(logits, topk_idx, alpha=0.02):
    # logits: [B, T, E]    topk_idx: [B, T, K]
    E = logits.shape[-1]
    P = logits.softmax(-1).mean(dim=(0, 1))         # [E] mean gate prob
    one_hot = scatter_to_onehot(topk_idx, E)        # [B, T, E] in {0, 1}
    f = one_hot.float().mean(dim=(0, 1))            # [E] selection fraction
    return alpha * E * (f * P).sum()
```

The dispatch step is the implementation pain point. A naive `for i in range(E): x[mask_i] =
f_i(x[mask_i])` is correct but serializes the experts. Production implementations gather
tokens into a `[E, capacity, d_model]` tensor, run all experts in parallel as a batched
matmul, then scatter back — see the
[Megablocks](https://github.com/databricks/megablocks) kernel for the standard form.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 4 · Empirical evidence</p>
## What Mixtral 8x7B and follow-ups measure

**Quality vs dense Llama 2 70B.** Jiang et al. 2024 Table 2 reports Mixtral 8x7B matching or
exceeding Llama 2 70B on MMLU (70.6 vs 69.9), HellaSwag (86.7 vs 85.4), Arc-C (66.0 vs 64.9),
and TriviaQA (77.6 vs 73.7), with the largest gap on code (HumanEval 40.2 vs 32.3) and math
(MATH 28.4 vs 13.8). The active parameter count is 12.9B vs Llama 2 70B's 70B, so the
quality-per-active-FLOP gap is roughly 5×. Total parameter count is 46.7B vs 70B, so the
quality-per-stored-byte gap is roughly 1.5× — a meaningful but smaller win.

**Expert specialization.** Jiang et al. 2024 §5 measures expert-routing distributions across
domains (ArXiv math, biology, PhilPapers, etc.) and finds *no clean per-domain specialization*
— the routing distribution looks roughly uniform within each domain. The paper's interpretation:
with $E = 8$ each expert is too coarse for domain-level specialization to be visible; the
specialization is happening at a sub-token (syntactic) level that the routing histogram does
not resolve. This is the empirical hook DeepSeekMoE (Dai et al. 2024, §4) uses to argue for
finer $E$.

**Comparison to OLMoE.** Muennighoff et al. 2024
([arXiv 2409.02060](https://arxiv.org/abs/2409.02060), §4) train OLMoE-1B-7B with $E = 64$,
$K = 8$ — Mixtral's recipe scaled to many small experts but without DeepSeekMoE's shared
experts. They report (Table 6) that this fine-grained-coarse variant beats Mixtral 8x7B at
matched active parameters on most benchmarks, with the biggest gap on multitask reasoning.
The OLMoE ablation (§5.1) attributes the gain to the finer granularity — same conclusion as
DeepSeekMoE's, but reached with the Mixtral-style aux-loss design.

**Independent reproduction at scale.** Mixtral 8x22B (Mistral AI, April 2024) keeps the same
recipe with $d_{model} = 6144$, $d_{ff} = 16384$, 56 layers, 141B total, 39B active. The
public configuration confirms the $E = 8$, $K = 2$ choice scales to the next size class
without architectural changes. No formal ablation paper accompanies this release.

**Auxiliary-loss sensitivity.** The Mistral team has not published an $\alpha$ sweep. The
Switch authors (Fedus et al. 2021, §3.2) sweep $\alpha \in [10^{-4}, 10^{-1}]$ and find a wide
plateau around $10^{-2}$; the Mixtral default $\alpha = 0.02$ sits in this plateau. ST-MoE
(Zoph et al. 2022, §3.1) confirms the same plateau at top-2 with $E = 32$. There is no
public Mixtral-specific re-ablation of $\alpha$ to point to.

**Routing instability.** A practitioner-side measurement from the Hugging Face team
([Mixtral fine-tuning notes](https://huggingface.co/blog/mixtral)) reports that long-tail
fine-tuning data can collapse the gate to using only 2–3 of 8 experts unless the auxiliary
loss is kept on. This matches the Switch result (Fedus et al. 2021, §3.1) that the aux loss
is load-bearing during distribution shift, not only during pretraining.

I don't know of a public ablation isolating the renormalize-over-$K$ choice vs softmax-over-
$E$-then-mask. The Sparse-MoE 2017 form and the Mixtral form should differ in the gate's
gradient when the top-$K$ logits are not the dominant entries; whether this matters at the
$E = 8$, $K = 2$ regime has not been measured in any paper I can cite.

<Summary>
**Mixtral MoE in one breath.** Eight full SwiGLU experts per layer, top-2 routing with softmax
renormalized over the selected pair, standard Switch-style $\alpha \cdot E \cdot \sum f_i P_i$
auxiliary balance loss. The coarsest viable production-MoE point: simple expert parallelism,
well-conditioned gate, off-the-shelf tooling. DeepSeekMoE's fine-grained-plus-shared variant
beats it on quality-per-active-parameter, but Mixtral 8x7B remains the canonical baseline and
taught most practitioners what "production MoE" means.
</Summary>
