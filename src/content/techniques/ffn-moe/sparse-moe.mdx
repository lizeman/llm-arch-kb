<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 1 · Premise</p>
## Conditional computation, before it worked

Conditional computation — running different parts of a network on different inputs to scale
capacity without scaling cost — had been studied since Bengio's mid-2010s "blocks of experts"
work, but had not produced clear wins at scale. Two obstacles dominated:

1. **Gate collapse.** Without intervention, the gating network learns to route most tokens to
   a small subset of experts; the remaining experts never get gradient signal and stay
   random-initialized.
2. **Discrete routing breaks gradients.** Choosing the top-K experts is a discrete operation;
   the gate network's gradient signal vanishes if you naively take an argmax.

Shazeer et al. 2017's paper engineered both away and demonstrated 137B-parameter models on
language modeling — outrageously large for 2017 — that beat dense baselines on per-token
quality and converged in fewer wall-clock steps.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 2 · Noisy top-K gating</p>
## The gradient trick

The gate computes logits, adds tunable per-expert Gaussian noise, and selects the top-K:

$$g(\mathbf{x}) = \mathrm{softmax}\bigl(\mathrm{KeepTopK}(\mathbf{x} W_g + \boldsymbol{\epsilon},\ K)\bigr),$$

where $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\ 1) \cdot \mathrm{softplus}(\mathbf{x} W_{\text{noise}})_i$
and $\mathrm{KeepTopK}$ sets the non-top-K entries to $-\infty$ before softmax. The noise
serves two purposes:

- It makes the routing decision a stochastic distribution, so gradients propagate via the
  reparameterization-like trick of including the noise.
- It encourages exploration: a single deterministic ranking would lock in early biases.

The output combines the selected experts' outputs weighted by their post-softmax gate values.

```python
def noisy_topk_gate(x, W_g, W_noise, K):
    clean_logits = x @ W_g.T
    noise_stddev = F.softplus(x @ W_noise.T)
    eps = torch.randn_like(clean_logits) * noise_stddev
    logits = clean_logits + eps
    topk_logits, topk_idx = logits.topk(K, dim=-1)
    # KeepTopK then softmax over those K
    masked = torch.full_like(logits, float("-inf"))
    masked.scatter_(-1, topk_idx, topk_logits)
    return masked.softmax(-1), topk_idx
```

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 3 · Two loss terms</p>
## Importance and load balance

To prevent gate collapse, Shazeer et al. add two auxiliary terms to the training loss:

- **Importance loss.** For each expert $i$, the *importance* $I_i$ is the sum of gate values
  $g_i(\mathbf{x})$ across the batch. The importance loss penalizes the coefficient of variation
  of $I_i$ across experts — pushing all experts toward equal aggregate utilization.
- **Load loss.** Estimates the *probability* that expert $i$ receives nonzero traffic from each
  token, again summing across the batch. Penalizes the coefficient of variation, ensuring the
  expert dispatch is balanced not just in importance-weight but in token count.

Both are scaled by a tiny coefficient ($\sim 10^{-2}$). Together they prevent the failure mode
where the gate routes 90% of traffic to two of eight experts.

Switch Transformer's load-balance loss (eq. 4 in Switch) is a simplified single-term version of
the two losses here; the conceptual structure is the same.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 4 · Results and legacy</p>
## What stuck

Shazeer et al.'s 137B-parameter LSTM-based MoE on the 1-Billion-Words benchmark achieved
significant perplexity reductions over dense baselines at ~4× less compute per token (Table 8).
The architecture predates the transformer era — the experts in the original were LSTM
sub-networks — but the gating recipe transferred unchanged.

Everything in this knowledge base's MoE category descends from this paper:

- **GShard** (2020) scaled it to transformer encoders at 600B parameters via expert sharding.
- **Switch Transformer** (2021) simplified to top-1 routing and replaced the two losses with one.
- **Mixtral** (2024) re-adopted top-2 routing at production-quality scale.
- **DeepSeekMoE** (2024) added fine-grained expert segmentation plus shared experts on top.

The lineage is intact and the foundational tricks — noisy top-K gating, auxiliary balance
losses, sparse forward + sparse backward — all originate here.
