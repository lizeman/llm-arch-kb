<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 1 · Premise</p>
## Before Switch

GShard (Lepikhin et al. 2020) was the canonical sparse MoE at the time, using top-2 routing
across hundreds of experts. The design rationale: top-2 routing is differentiable through both
selected experts, giving the gating network gradient signal for the unselected-but-runner-up
expert too. Top-1 was assumed to be unstable — if the gate consistently routes a token to
expert A, the gradient through expert B never adjusts, and the gate can never learn that B
might have been better.

Fedus, Zoph, and Shazeer's bet: this reasoning is wrong empirically. Top-1 routing works fine
if you size the experts appropriately and use a calibrated auxiliary loss.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 2 · The simplification</p>
## Top-1 routing

A Switch layer routes each token to the single highest-scoring expert:

$$\mathrm{Switch}(\mathbf{x}) = g_{i^*}(\mathbf{x}) \cdot f_{i^*}(\mathbf{x}),\quad i^* = \arg\max_i g(\mathbf{x})_i.$$

The selected gate value $g_{i^*}$ multiplies the expert output — this multiplicative factor is
what gives the gating network a gradient signal even though only one expert was selected. The
gradient with respect to $g_{i^*}$ is $f_{i^*}(\mathbf{x})$, which encodes whether routing to
$i^*$ was a "good" decision.

```python
def switch_layer(x, experts, gate):
    logits = gate(x)                          # [B, T, E]
    top_idx = logits.argmax(dim=-1)           # [B, T]
    top_w = logits.softmax(-1).gather(-1, top_idx.unsqueeze(-1)).squeeze(-1)
    # Dispatch each token to its single expert; multiply output by top_w.
    return dispatch_and_combine(x, experts, top_idx.unsqueeze(-1), top_w.unsqueeze(-1))
```

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 3 · The balance loss everyone copies</p>
## Switch's auxiliary objective

The auxiliary load-balancing loss the paper introduces (eq. 4):

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{aux}} = \alpha \cdot E \cdot \sum_{i=1}^{E} f_i \cdot P_i,$$

where:

- $f_i$ is the fraction of tokens in the batch routed to expert $i$ (a count, post-argmax).
- $P_i$ is the mean softmax probability assigned to expert $i$ by the gate.

The product $f_i \cdot P_i$ is minimized when both quantities are uniform ($1/E$), so summing
penalizes deviations from uniform utilization. The $\alpha$ scaling is small (typically 0.01)
because the gating signal is itself noisy.

This is the same formula used by every subsequent top-K MoE — Mixtral, GShard variants,
DeepSeekMoE (until V3 ditched it for the aux-loss-free scheme). Switch's lasting contribution
is at least as much this loss as the top-1 routing itself.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 4 · Capacity factor and dropping</p>
## What "expert capacity" means

Top-1 routing has a problem GShard didn't: a popular expert can be selected by more tokens than
it has compute capacity to handle in one step. The Switch paper formalizes this with an
**expert capacity** parameter $C$: each expert handles at most $C \cdot T / E$ tokens per batch.
Excess tokens are *dropped* — their output is the identity (residual passes through unchanged).

In practice $C \approx 1.25$ trades a small amount of dropping for a much more parallelizable
dispatch step. The dropping policy is what makes Switch tractable on hardware that requires
fixed-size compute per expert; otherwise the slowest expert gates the wall-clock.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 5 · Results and legacy</p>
## What Switch proved

The headline (Table 9 in the paper): **Switch-C with 1.6T parameters and 64B active** matches
or exceeds dense T5-XXL on multilingual translation and span corruption. The trillion-parameter
threshold was crossed by a model with 7× fewer FLOPs per token than the dense equivalent —
this was the moment "sparse MoE at frontier scale" went from research curiosity to credible.

Production MoE has since moved toward top-2 (Mixtral) and top-K with fine-grained experts
(DeepSeekMoE), partly because the dropping policy makes Switch's top-1 hard to tune and partly
because subsequent work showed top-2 quality justifies the doubled compute. But Switch's
auxiliary loss formula, capacity-factor framing, and "trillion parameters is tractable" demo
are all load-bearing in the modern MoE recipe.

**Switch in one breath.** Top-1 expert routing with a load-balancing aux loss and a capacity

math.
