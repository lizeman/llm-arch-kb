<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 1 · Premise</p>
## Long context without retraining the base

At LLaMA-2-7B's 4K pretraining length, the per-layer KV cache is $2 \cdot 32 \text{ heads}
\cdot 128 \text{ dim} \cdot 4096 \text{ tokens} = 33.5$ MB in fp16, summed to $\sim 1$ GB
across the 32-layer stack. Extending the same model to 128K context multiplies that footprint
by $32\times$ before any sequence-length-dependent compute is even started. The mainstream
2023 responses came in two flavours:

- *Positional rescaling.* YaRN ([Peng et al. 2023,
  arXiv 2309.00071](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.00071)) reinterprets RoPE frequencies for the
  longer range and continues pretraining the whole model on long-document data — typically
  several hundred million tokens of fine-tuning, every parameter updated.
- *Sliding eviction.* StreamingLLM ([Xiao et al. 2023,
  arXiv 2309.17453](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.17453)) keeps a fixed-size window plus a few
  attention-sink prefix tokens and discards the rest. Cheap, but information older than the
  window is irretrievably lost.

Activation Beacon ([Zhang et al. 2024](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.03462)) takes a third
route, closer in spirit to Compressive Transformer ([Rae et al.
2019](https://arxiv.org/abs/1911.05507)) but redesigned for the modern "freeze the base,
fine-tune a small adapter" regime: insert learnable *beacon* tokens at regular intervals that
compress the activations of the preceding window into a small handful of summary states; the
base model attends to those summaries instead of the literal past keys. The base model's
weights stay frozen; only the beacon-specific projections are trained. The paper's headline
claim is a $100\times$ context extension (4K $\to$ 400K) at the cost of $\sim 1$B tokens of
fine-tuning on a single A800 GPU ([§4.1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.03462)).

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 2 · Derivation</p>
## From sliding-window to learned beacon compression

**Starting point — interval segmentation.** Partition the input stream into intervals of
length $L$ (Zhang et al. use $L \in \{1024, 2048, 4096\}$,
[§3.1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.03462)). For each interval $i$, choose a *condensing ratio*
$\alpha \in \{2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64, 128\}$ and append $k_i = \lceil L / \alpha \rceil$ learnable
*beacon tokens* after the interval's real tokens:

$$\underbrace{[x_1, \ldots, x_L]}_{\text{interval 1}} \, [\beta_1, \ldots, \beta_{k_1}],\;
\underbrace{[x_{L+1}, \ldots, x_{2L}]}_{\text{interval 2}} \, [\beta_1, \ldots, \beta_{k_2}],\; \ldots$$

With $L = 1024, \alpha = 32$ we get $k = 32$ beacons per interval — a $32\times$ compression
ratio of activations.

**Step 1 — beacon-only projections.** A standard Transformer layer applies the same projection
matrices $W_Q, W_K, W_V \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$ to every position. Activation Beacon
duplicates these matrices into a *parallel beacon set*
$W_Q^{(\beta)}, W_K^{(\beta)}, W_V^{(\beta)}$ that are used *only* at beacon positions
([§3.2, Eq. 2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.03462)):

$$\mathbf{q}_t, \mathbf{k}_t, \mathbf{v}_t = \begin{cases} W_Q h_t,\; W_K h_t,\; W_V h_t & t \text{ is a real token,} \\ W_Q^{(\beta)} h_t,\; W_K^{(\beta)} h_t,\; W_V^{(\beta)} h_t & t \text{ is a beacon.} \end{cases}$$

These beacon-specific matrices are the *only* new trainable parameters. Real-token projections
remain frozen at their pretrained values. Crucially, since $W^{(\beta)}$ shares the input
hidden dimension with $W$, the model can express any beacon projection that the base attention
mechanism is geometrically capable of consuming — no architectural surgery beyond the parallel
weight set.

**Step 2 — "stepwise expansion" attention mask.** Beacons need a non-standard mask to make
compression behaviour explicit. Within interval $i$, all $L$ real tokens can attend to each
other causally and to *all earlier* intervals' beacons; no real token attends to any other
interval's real tokens directly. Beacons in interval $i$ attend to (a) the $L$ real tokens
of interval $i$, and (b) all beacons from intervals $< i$. Formally, with $\mathcal{R}_i$ the
real-token positions of interval $i$ and $\mathcal{B}_i$ its beacon positions
([Figure 2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.03462)):

$$\text{visible}(t) = \begin{cases} \{j \le t : j \in \mathcal{R}_i\} \cup \bigcup_{i' < i} \mathcal{B}_{i'} & t \in \mathcal{R}_i, \\ \mathcal{R}_i \cup \bigcup_{i' < i} \mathcal{B}_{i'} & t \in \mathcal{B}_i. \end{cases}$$

Two consequences:

1. Real tokens never see other intervals' raw activations — only beacons. The "long context"
   the base model perceives is a sequence of dense local windows interleaved with compressed
   summaries.
2. Beacons compose hierarchically across intervals: $\mathcal{B}_3$ attends to
   $\mathcal{B}_1 \cup \mathcal{B}_2$, so a deep enough stack can carry information across
   arbitrary numbers of intervals at constant per-interval cost.

**Step 3 — the condensing ratio menu.** A single fixed ratio $\alpha$ overfits: short contexts
don't need aggressive compression, very long contexts need more than $32\times$. Zhang et al.
*sample* $\alpha$ uniformly from $\{2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64, 128\}$ at each training step
([§3.3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.03462)), training a single beacon parameter set that
generalizes across the menu. At inference time, $\alpha$ is chosen per deployment based on
target context length — small $\alpha$ for 16K, large $\alpha$ for 400K. This is what gives
the technique its extreme range without separate beacon parameters per regime.

**Step 4 — autoregressive training loss.** Training maximizes the standard next-token
log-likelihood on the *real tokens only* — beacons emit no prediction. Because beacons appear
in `visible(t)` for later real tokens, the beacon projections receive gradient from those
future-token losses, which is how the model learns *what to compress*. The base model's
parameters never receive a gradient: they are frozen via `requires_grad=False` and the optimizer
state is only allocated for $W^{(\beta)}$ ([§3.3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.03462)).

**Step 5 — cost accounting.** Let $T$ be total context length, $L$ interval size, $\alpha$
condensing ratio, $H$ heads, $d_h$ head dimension. The KV cache after processing all of $T$:

$$\text{KV-cache}(T) = \underbrace{2 H d_h \cdot L}_{\text{current interval, real tokens}} + \underbrace{2 H d_h \cdot \tfrac{T}{\alpha}}_{\text{all past beacons}}.$$

At $T = 400$K, $L = 1024$, $\alpha = 128$ the second term is $2 H d_h \cdot 3125 \approx
3{,}200$ tokens-equivalent of KV cache — well *below* the $L = 1024$ local window. The model
streams through arbitrarily long contexts at near-constant memory beyond the local window,
which is the practical claim behind "400K on a single A800". Compute per-token attention cost
is $O(L + T/\alpha)$, linear in $T$ with a small constant.

**Trainable parameter count.** $W_Q^{(\beta)}, W_K^{(\beta)}, W_V^{(\beta)}$ per layer, each
$d \times d$, plus a per-layer beacon output projection — a total of $\sim 4 d^2$ per layer.
For LLaMA-2-7B's 32 layers at $d = 4096$, that is $\sim 2.1$B added parameters. Zhang et al.
report training all of these in $\sim 1$ billion tokens of fine-tuning data
([§4.1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.03462)); the base 7B model is untouched.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 3 · Reference implementation</p>
## Beacon-parallel projections and stepwise mask

```python
# Real-token attention weights are frozen; beacon weights are new and trainable.
# h: [B, T, d]  hidden state at layer input
# is_beacon[t]: 1 if position t is a beacon token, else 0
# interval_id[t]: which interval position t belongs to (real or beacon)

def beacon_attention(h, is_beacon, interval_id, W_Q, W_K, W_V, W_Qb, W_Kb, W_Vb):
    # Per-position projection: select between frozen and beacon weights
    q = where(is_beacon[..., None], h @ W_Qb, h @ W_Q)   # [B, T, H, d_h]
    k = where(is_beacon[..., None], h @ W_Kb, h @ W_K)
    v = where(is_beacon[..., None], h @ W_Vb, h @ W_V)

    # Stepwise mask: real tokens see own-interval real + all earlier beacons;
    # beacons see own-interval real + all earlier beacons.
    same_interval = interval_id[None, :] == interval_id[:, None]
    earlier_beacon = is_beacon[None, :] & (interval_id[None, :] < interval_id[:, None])
    own_real_causal = same_interval & ~is_beacon[None, :] & (arange(T)[None, :] <= arange(T)[:, None])
    mask = earlier_beacon | own_real_causal
    return scaled_dot_product_attention(q, k, v, attn_mask=mask)

def sample_alpha():
    return choice([2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64, 128])           # per training step

def insert_beacons(tokens, L, alpha):
    # Append ceil(L / alpha) beacon slots after each L-token interval
    out = []
    for chunk in batched(tokens, L):
        out.extend(chunk)
        out.extend([BEACON_TOKEN] * ceil(len(chunk) / alpha))
    return out
```

The sketch elides the streaming inference loop, in which each interval's beacons are written
into the KV cache and the interval's real tokens are then evicted — only the $\sim T/\alpha$
beacons survive across the long context. See the reference repo
[`FlagOpen/FlagEmbedding/Long_LLM/activation_beacon`](https://github.com/FlagOpen/FlagEmbedding/tree/master/Long_LLM/activation_beacon)
for the full training-time mask construction and rotary-position handling.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 4 · Empirical evidence</p>
## What is and isn't known

**Introducing paper (Zhang et al. 2024).**

1. *Language modeling at extended context.* On PG-19, Proof-Pile, and CodeParrot, a
   LLaMA-2-7B-Chat base equipped with Activation Beacon at $\alpha$-mix training keeps
   perplexity within $0.05$–$0.15$ of the 4K base out to $32$K, and continues to lower
   perplexity (i.e., gains from longer context) out to $100$K
   ([Table 2, Figure 4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.03462)). The 4K LLaMA-2 baseline diverges
   to $> 100$ PPL by 8K.
2. *Long-document QA.* On the LongBench suite ([Bai et al. 2023,
   arXiv 2308.14508](https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.14508)) Activation Beacon scores 31.8 average
   across 9 English tasks, beating LLaMA-2-7B-Chat-4K (24.2), the YaRN-extended LLaMA-2-7B
   variant in the paper's reproduction (28.7), and StreamingLLM-extended LLaMA-2-7B (25.9)
   ([Table 3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.03462)).
3. *Compression-ratio sweep.* The $\alpha \in \{2, ..., 128\}$ sweep
   ([Table 4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.03462)) shows monotone PPL degradation as
   $\alpha$ grows, but the degradation is modest ($\sim 0.3$ PPL between $\alpha = 8$ and
   $\alpha = 64$) — most of the long-context value survives heavy compression.
4. *Training cost.* $\sim 9{,}000$ GPU-hours of A800 time (single-node training)
   ([§4.1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.03462)). For comparison, YaRN's continued-pretraining
   recipe for the same context regime reports an order of magnitude more
   ([Peng et al. 2023, §5.2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.00071)).

**Independent follow-up.**

- *RULER benchmark* ([Hsieh et al. 2024,
  arXiv 2404.06654](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.06654)) evaluates Activation Beacon among 13
  long-context techniques. RULER finds Activation Beacon-LLaMA-2-7B retains $\sim 90\%$ of
  base performance at 4K but drops off sharply on multi-hop tasks beyond 32K — the broad
  pattern is "exact-recall tasks degrade with $\alpha$, semantic tasks tolerate it"
  (Hsieh et al. §5).
- *LongBench v2* ([Bai et al. 2024,
  arXiv 2412.15204](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.15204)) inherited Activation Beacon's
  evaluation tasks but the technique itself is not in its leaderboard; the authors note in
  §6 that "compression-based methods like Activation Beacon plateau on tasks requiring
  multi-document aggregation."
- *Survey coverage.* The technique is consistently cited as one of the canonical
  "fixed-base compressed-summary" approaches in long-context surveys (e.g.,
  [Pawar et al. 2024, arXiv 2402.02244](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.02244), §4.2).

**Sensitivity studies — what is *not* publicly known.** The introducing paper does not report
ablations on (a) interval size $L$ holding $\alpha$ fixed — only the joint sweep over both;
(b) how beacon parameters interact with subsequent RoPE rescaling like YaRN or NTK-aware
extension; (c) compositional behaviour when stacking Activation Beacon on top of GQA or MLA
KV-compression. I don't know of an independent reproduction at scales above LLaMA-2-7B; the
published checkpoints and follow-up evaluations all sit at the 7B regime.

**Production adoption.** None recorded in this knowledge base. Activation Beacon sits in the
research lineage that includes Compressive Transformer and Landmark Attention; its design
influences appear in subsequent work on KV-cache compression (e.g.,
[SnapKV, Li et al. 2024](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.14469)) but no frontier dense or MoE
production model ships beacon-style learned compression as its long-context primitive.

<Summary>
**Activation Beacon in one breath.** Add a parallel set of beacon-only $W_Q, W_K, W_V$
projections; insert $L / \alpha$ beacon slots after every $L$-token interval; train only the
beacon weights with a stepwise mask that forces real tokens to attend to beacons (not raw past
tokens) for everything beyond the local window. Sample $\alpha$ from
$\{2, \ldots, 128\}$ per step so one model handles many compression ratios. Extends LLaMA-2-7B
from 4K to 400K context at a single-node fine-tune; degrades on multi-document aggregation; no
production adoption to date.
</Summary>
