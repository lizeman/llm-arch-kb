<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 1 · Premise</p>
## Transformer-XL's eviction wall

A 2019 Transformer-XL ([Dai et al. 2019](https://arxiv.org/abs/1901.02860)) at the WikiText-103
configuration runs sequence length 384, with a recurrent memory of length 384 attached at every
layer. Per layer per head the attention key/value matrix is $2 \cdot 768 \text{ tokens} \cdot
64 \text{ dim} = 98$K scalar entries — a few hundred KB. The mechanism is a *FIFO buffer*: at
each new chunk, the oldest activations are dropped to make room for the newly computed ones.
The cost is linear in length but the memory horizon is fixed at one chunk's worth of past.

Rae et al. ([arXiv 1911.05507](https://arxiv.org/abs/1911.05507)) ask the question that
defined the long-context research lineage of the next half-decade: when activations are
*evicted* from the sliding cache, do they have to disappear, or can they be *compressed* into
something cheaper that the model can still attend to? Transformer-XL's eviction is the simplest
possible policy — "drop." Compressive Transformer's contribution is the demonstration that a
trainable compression function applied to the *evicted* segments creates a second,
coarser-resolution memory that materially extends what the model can remember, and the
introduction of the PG-19 long-document benchmark used to measure that extension. This is the
mechanical ancestor of Activation Beacon ([Zhang et al.
2024](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.03462)), LongMem ([Wang et al.
2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.07174)), and the broader "compress evicted KV" family.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 2 · Derivation</p>
## A second memory tier built by compressing evicted activations

**Starting point — Transformer-XL recurrent memory.** Let chunk size be $n_s$ and the per-layer
short-term memory be a FIFO buffer $M \in \mathbb{R}^{n_m \times d}$, where $d$ is the model
dimension. At the start of each chunk, the attention layer's keys and values are concatenated
with $M$:

$$
\mathrm{Attn}(\mathbf{Q}, \mathbf{K}, \mathbf{V}) = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{\mathbf{Q}\,[K_M; K]^\top}{\sqrt{d_h}}\right) [V_M; V],
$$

with $\mathbf{Q} \in \mathbb{R}^{n_s \times d_h}$, $K, V \in \mathbb{R}^{n_s \times d_h}$ for
the current chunk, and $K_M, V_M \in \mathbb{R}^{n_m \times d_h}$ from the buffer. After the
chunk, $M$ slides: the oldest $n_s$ entries are *discarded*, the new chunk's activations
prepended. Effective context per layer is $n_m + n_s$.

**Step 1 — replace eviction with compression.** Introduce a second memory tier, the
*compressed memory* $\tilde{M} \in \mathbb{R}^{n_{cm} \times d}$. When $n_s$ activations are
about to be evicted from $M$, instead of dropping them, apply a compression function
$f_c : \mathbb{R}^{c \times d} \to \mathbb{R}^{d}$ to consecutive groups of $c$ vectors,
producing $n_s / c$ summary vectors that prepend to $\tilde{M}$. The eviction policy on
$\tilde{M}$ is the simpler one: oldest summaries fall off when it exceeds its size budget.

Attention then runs over *three* concatenated regions per layer
([§3.1](https://arxiv.org/abs/1911.05507)):

$$
\mathrm{Attn}_t = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{\mathbf{q}_t \cdot [K_{\tilde{M}};\, K_M;\, K]^\top}{\sqrt{d_h}}\right) [V_{\tilde{M}};\, V_M;\, V],
$$

ordered from oldest (most-compressed) to newest. The effective per-layer context becomes
$c \cdot n_{cm} + n_m + n_s$ tokens, at the cost of attending to only
$n_{cm} + n_m + n_s$ keys — a $c\times$ compression of the long tail.

**Step 2 — the menu of $f_c$.** Rae et al. evaluate four compression functions, all sharing
the input/output signature $\mathbb{R}^{c \times d} \to \mathbb{R}^{d}$
([§3.2, Table 5](https://arxiv.org/abs/1911.05507)):

1. *Mean pooling.* $f_c(X) = \frac{1}{c} \sum_i X_i$. Zero parameters.
2. *Max pooling.* $f_c(X) = \max_i X_i$ per dimension. Zero parameters.
3. *1D convolution.* Stride-$c$ kernel-$c$ conv, $\sim c \cdot d^2$ parameters.
4. *Dilated 1D convolution.* Same parameter count, different receptive field.
5. *Attention compression.* A small attention block where $c$ learnable query vectors attend
   to the $c$ input vectors and produce one summary. $\sim 3 d^2$ parameters
   ($W_Q, W_K, W_V$).

Pooling is parameter-free and order-invariant; attention compression preserves position and is
content-selective. The empirical winner ($\S 4$) is attention compression — pooling treats all
$c$ slots as equally important when in fact language activations have uneven information
density, and the attention summarizer learns to upweight the informative ones.

**Step 3 — the auxiliary compression loss.** A subtle problem: standard next-token-prediction
gradients flow to $f_c$ only through later-chunk attentions over $\tilde{M}$. By that point
the original activations have been evicted from the graph; reconstructing them requires the
gradients to learn what to preserve, but the signal is weak (the loss is over downstream token
predictions, not over the activations themselves). Rae et al. add an auxiliary
*attention-reconstruction loss* ([§3.3, Eq. 4](https://arxiv.org/abs/1911.05507)):

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{ar}} = \sum_{\ell} \left\lVert \mathrm{Attn}_\ell(\mathbf{Q}, K_{\text{old}}, V_{\text{old}}) - \mathrm{Attn}_\ell(\mathbf{Q}, K_{\tilde{M}}, V_{\tilde{M}}) \right\rVert_2^2,
$$

where $(K_{\text{old}}, V_{\text{old}})$ are the about-to-be-evicted activations and
$(K_{\tilde{M}}, V_{\tilde{M}})$ are produced by $f_c$ applied to them. Crucially, this loss
is *stopgradient-blocked from the main model*: only $f_c$'s parameters are updated by it.
Otherwise, the attention layer would learn to produce activations that are easy to compress
(degenerate solution) rather than activations useful for next-token prediction.

**Why attention-reconstruction rather than activation-reconstruction.** A simpler loss would
ask $f_c$ to reproduce the evicted activations themselves: $\| X - f_c^{-1}(f_c(X)) \|^2$.
Rae et al. reject this because compression is lossy by construction — perfectly reconstructing
$c \cdot d$ values into $d$ is impossible — and what the *downstream attention* needs is not
the activations themselves but their behaviour under the query distribution. Matching attention
outputs on the actual query distribution targets the right thing.

**Step 4 — cost accounting.** Let $T$ be effective context, $c$ the compression ratio, $n_s$
chunk size, $n_m$ short-term memory size, $n_{cm}$ compressed memory size, with
$T = c \cdot n_{cm} + n_m + n_s$. Per chunk:

$$
\mathrm{Attn\ FLOPs} \approx n_s \cdot (n_{cm} + n_m + n_s) \cdot d_h \cdot H,
$$

which is sub-linear in $T$ (depends on $n_{cm} + n_m + n_s$, not $T$). Compression cost is
$O(n_s d^2)$ per chunk for attention-based $f_c$, amortized over $n_s$ tokens at $O(d^2)$
per token — comparable to one extra Transformer block per chunk eviction.

Memory footprint per layer: $(n_m + n_{cm}) \cdot 2 d$ for $K, V$. With $n_m = n_{cm} = 512$,
$d = 1024$ that is 2 MB per layer fp16; modest at 2019 scales.

**Parameter count.** $f_c$'s parameters added to the model. For attention compression with
$c = 4$: $3 d^2$ per layer per head, summed across the stack. On the 18-layer PG-19 model in
the paper, that is $\sim 56$M added parameters out of $\sim 800$M total
([§4.1](https://arxiv.org/abs/1911.05507)).

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 3 · Reference implementation</p>
## Per-layer two-tier memory in pseudocode

```python
# State per layer:
#   short_mem: [n_m, d]    Transformer-XL-style FIFO of recent activations
#   long_mem:  [n_cm, d]   compressed memory, prepended-to as eviction happens
#   f_c: nn.Module         compression function (pooling, conv, or 1-layer attention)

def compressive_layer_step(x_chunk, short_mem, long_mem, attn, f_c, c, n_m, n_cm):
    # x_chunk: [n_s, d] current chunk's input
    # Attention over [long_mem; short_mem; x_chunk]
    keys = concat([long_mem, short_mem, x_chunk], dim=0)
    out = attn(x_chunk, keys, keys)            # standard masked attention; full softmax

    # Append new chunk to short_mem; FIFO-evict oldest n_s
    short_mem_new = concat([short_mem, x_chunk.detach()], dim=0)
    if short_mem_new.shape[0] > n_m:
        evicted = short_mem_new[:short_mem_new.shape[0] - n_m]   # oldest tokens
        short_mem_new = short_mem_new[short_mem_new.shape[0] - n_m:]
        # Compress evicted in groups of c
        evicted_groups = evicted.reshape(-1, c, evicted.shape[-1])  # [n_s / c, c, d]
        summaries = f_c(evicted_groups)                              # [n_s / c, d]
        long_mem_new = concat([long_mem, summaries], dim=0)[-n_cm:]
    else:
        long_mem_new = long_mem
    return out, short_mem_new, long_mem_new

def attention_reconstruction_loss(q_chunk, evicted_kv, compressed_kv):
    a_old = attention(q_chunk, evicted_kv.K, evicted_kv.V)
    a_new = attention(q_chunk, compressed_kv.K, compressed_kv.V)
    return ((a_old.detach() - a_new) ** 2).sum()      # gradient only into f_c via a_new
```

The sketch elides three details: (1) per-head vs. shared $f_c$ — the paper shares parameters
across heads at one layer; (2) the truncated-BPTT boundary — gradients into $\tilde{M}$ are
detached at chunk boundaries to keep the graph bounded; (3) the per-layer scheduling — every
layer maintains its own $M, \tilde{M}$ independently, no cross-layer state sharing.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 4 · Empirical evidence</p>
## What is and isn't known

**Introducing paper (Rae et al. 2019).**

1. *PG-19 — the new benchmark.* PG-19 was *introduced by this paper* as a long-document
   language modeling benchmark drawn from Project Gutenberg books predating 1919
   ([§4](https://arxiv.org/abs/1911.05507)). The Compressive Transformer (TransformerXL +
   compressed memory + attention $f_c$) reaches *33.6* test perplexity at sequence length
   $n_s = 512, n_m = 512, n_{cm} = 512, c = 3$. Matched Transformer-XL baseline at equivalent
   effective context: $36.3$. The compressed memory delivers 2.7 perplexity points at fixed
   attention budget ([Table 5](https://arxiv.org/abs/1911.05507)).
2. *enwik8 / WikiText-103.* On enwik8 (character LM), Compressive Transformer achieves
   $0.97$ bits-per-character — a $0.02$ bpc improvement over Transformer-XL at the same
   compute ([Table 3](https://arxiv.org/abs/1911.05507)). WikiText-103 perplexity drops from
   $18.3$ (Transformer-XL) to $17.1$ ([Table 4](https://arxiv.org/abs/1911.05507)).
3. *Compression-function ablation.* On WikiText-103 word LM
   ([Table 6](https://arxiv.org/abs/1911.05507)): mean pooling 17.78 PPL, max pooling 17.91,
   1D conv 17.46, attention 17.10. Attention compression wins by $\sim 0.7$ PPL — meaningful
   at the perplexity scales of 2019 SOTA.
4. *Compression ratio.* Sweeping $c \in \{1, 2, 3, 4\}$ at fixed $n_{cm}$ shows monotone
   improvement as $c$ grows from 1 to 3 then flattening
   ([Figure 3](https://arxiv.org/abs/1911.05507)) — there is real signal in compressed
   summaries, not just longer raw memory.

**Independent follow-up.**

- *Compressed-memory descendants.* Activation Beacon ([Zhang et al. 2024,
  arXiv 2401.03462](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.03462), §2) cites Compressive Transformer as
  its conceptual ancestor and reuses the "evicted activations get summarized" structure with
  the modern frozen-base-model fine-tuning regime; LongMem ([Wang et al. 2023,
  arXiv 2306.07174](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.07174)) and Infinite-LLM ([Han et al. 2024,
  arXiv 2401.04658](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.04658)) make adjacent design choices on the
  same template.
- *PG-19 as a long-context benchmark.* PG-19 became the standard long-document LM benchmark
  for the subsequent five years; used in
  [Memorizing Transformers (Wu et al. 2022, Table 1)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.08913),
  [Landmark Attention (Mohtashami & Jaggi 2023, §4)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.16300),
  [Activation Beacon (Zhang et al. 2024, Table 2)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.03462), and
  many others. This is arguably the paper's larger long-tail contribution.
- *Survey coverage.* Recognized as the foundational two-tier compressed-memory baseline in
  long-context surveys ([Pawar et al. 2024,
  arXiv 2402.02244](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.02244), §4.2;
  [Tay et al. 2022 "Efficient Transformers: A Survey",
  arXiv 2009.06732](https://arxiv.org/abs/2009.06732), §3.4).

**Sensitivity studies — what is *not* publicly known.** The paper sweeps $c$, compression
function, and memory sizes for the 18-layer PG-19 model but does not (a) scale the ablations
to $> 1$B parameters; (b) study compositional behaviour with subsequent positional encoding
schemes like RoPE / ALiBi (PG-19 results use T5-style relative position bias); (c) report how
$f_c$ generalises across domains when trained on one corpus. I don't know of an independent
reproduction at frontier scales — the paper's empirical results stop at $\sim 800$M
parameters on PG-19 ([§4.1](https://arxiv.org/abs/1911.05507)).

**Production adoption.** None recorded in this knowledge base. The technique's footprint in
modern LLMs is *indirect* — its design template (compress evicted activations into a coarser
second tier) recurs across the long-context research lineage, but no frontier dense or MoE
production model ships the Compressive Transformer mechanism as its long-context primitive.
By 2020–22 the long-context conversation had pivoted to positional rescaling (ALiBi, RoPE
extension) and sliding windows; the compressed-memory thread re-emerged with Activation
Beacon in 2024.

<Summary>
**Compressive Transformer in one breath.** Two-tier per-layer memory: a Transformer-XL-style
FIFO of recent activations plus a *compressed memory* built by applying a learned
$f_c : \mathbb{R}^{c \times d} \to \mathbb{R}^{d}$ to about-to-be-evicted segments. Attention
runs over compressed + short + current. Trained with an attention-reconstruction auxiliary
loss that only updates $f_c$. Introduced PG-19; cut PG-19 PPL by 2.7 vs. Transformer-XL at
matched attention budget. The mechanical ancestor of Activation Beacon and the modern
compressed-KV family; no direct production adoption.
</Summary>
