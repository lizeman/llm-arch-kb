<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 1 · Premise</p>
## Where length and resolution collide

At training length $T = 2{,}048$, the LLaMA-7B used as Mohtashami & Jaggi's testbed already
materializes a full attention matrix of $T^2 = 4.2 \times 10^6$ entries per head per layer
([Mohtashami & Jaggi 2023, §1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.16300)). Doubling context to 4K
quadruples the FLOPs and KV-cache footprint; pushing to 32K — the inference regime the paper
actually targets — would cost $256\times$ the attention compute at fixed depth. By 2023 several
families of workaround existed, and each gave up something specific:

- **Sliding-window attention** (Beltagy et al. 2020,
  [arXiv 2004.05150](https://arxiv.org/abs/2004.05150); StreamingLLM keeps an attention-sink
  prefix on top, [Xiao et al. 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.17453)) keeps cost linear in
  $T$ but discards keys older than the window. A token at distance $> W$ is unreachable in one
  layer; deep stacks recover it slowly and lossily.
- **Compressive memory** (Transformer-XL,
  [Dai et al. 2019](https://arxiv.org/abs/1901.02860); Compressive Transformer,
  [Rae et al. 2019](https://arxiv.org/abs/1911.05507)) keeps everything but at coarsened
  resolution: the old tokens survive as pooled or attention-summarized vectors. Resolution
  degrades with distance.
- **kNN memory** (Memorizing Transformers,
  [Wu et al. 2022](https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.08913)) retrieves a small slice of old K/V at
  full resolution, but the retrieval index is non-differentiable; the model cannot learn
  to *index* better.

Landmark Attention's wager is that the right primitive is *random access* rather than uniform
decay or sliding eviction: most queries need only a few specific chunks of the distant past at
full resolution, and the model itself can learn which chunks those are. The contribution is a
single special token — the *landmark* — that summarizes a chunk well enough to act as its
addressable handle inside the attention softmax.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 2 · Derivation</p>
## From dense attention to landmark-gated retrieval

**Starting point.** Standard causal attention at position $t$ over a key/value cache of
length $T$ computes

$$
\mathrm{Attn}(\mathbf{q}_t, K_{1:t}, V_{1:t}) = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{\mathbf{q}_t K_{1:t}^\top}{\sqrt{d_h}}\right) V_{1:t},
$$

with $\mathbf{q}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{d_h}$, $K_{1:t}, V_{1:t} \in \mathbb{R}^{t \times d_h}$.
The $O(T)$ keys all participate in the same softmax; both memory and FLOPs scale as
$\Theta(T \cdot d_h)$ per query position.

**Step 1 — chunking with landmark slots.** Partition the sequence into contiguous *blocks* of
$W$ tokens (Mohtashami & Jaggi use $W = 50$ in their LLaMA-7B fine-tune,
[§4.1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.16300)). For each block $b$, append a single learnable
*landmark token* $\boldsymbol{\ell}_b$ that participates in self-attention exactly like a
real token:

$$
\underbrace{[x_1, \ldots, x_W, \boldsymbol{\ell}_1]}_{\text{block 1}},\;
\underbrace{[x_{W+1}, \ldots, x_{2W}, \boldsymbol{\ell}_2]}_{\text{block 2}},\;\ldots
$$

Each $\boldsymbol{\ell}_b$ is a sequence position; it has its own query, key, value via the
shared $W_Q, W_K, W_V$, and through normal causal attention it absorbs information from the
$W$ tokens of its block. There is no auxiliary loss; the landmark's representation is shaped
entirely by next-token prediction on the chunked sequence.

**Step 2 — *grouped softmax* at training time.** Define $\mathcal{B}(t)$ as the index of the
block containing token $t$. Partition the keys before $t$ into blocks $b = 1, \ldots, \mathcal{B}(t)$.
A landmark $\boldsymbol{\ell}_b$ stands for its whole block. Mohtashami & Jaggi modify the
softmax so that, for a query $\mathbf{q}_t$ and a non-landmark key $\mathbf{k}_j$ in block $b$,
the *normalized* attention weight is the product of two factors —
([§3.2, Eq. 1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.16300)):

$$
\alpha_{t,j} \;=\; \underbrace{\frac{\exp(\mathbf{q}_t \cdot \boldsymbol{\ell}_b / \sqrt{d_h})}{\sum_{b'=1}^{\mathcal{B}(t)} \exp(\mathbf{q}_t \cdot \boldsymbol{\ell}_{b'} / \sqrt{d_h})}}_{\text{block-level (over landmarks)}} \;\cdot\; \underbrace{\frac{\exp(\mathbf{q}_t \cdot \mathbf{k}_j / \sqrt{d_h})}{\sum_{j' \in \text{block } b} \exp(\mathbf{q}_t \cdot \mathbf{k}_{j'} / \sqrt{d_h})}}_{\text{within-block (excluding landmark)}}.
$$

The first factor is a softmax over *landmarks only* — exactly $\mathcal{B}(t)$ logits, one per
block. The second factor is a per-block softmax over the $W$ ordinary keys, with the landmark
removed. Reading this equation backwards is what makes the rest of the design work: the
landmark $\boldsymbol{\ell}_b$ is *forced* to carry whatever signal the model needs to compute
its block's outer-softmax weight, because the gradient of $\alpha_{t,j}$ with respect to
$\boldsymbol{\ell}_b$ flows only through the outer factor.

**Why two softmaxes rather than one.** A single softmax over $\{\mathbf{k}_j\} \cup
\{\boldsymbol{\ell}_b\}$ would let landmarks and tokens compete on the same scale; the landmark
would have to win each token's mass directly. The grouped form decouples the *selection*
problem (which block?) from the *content* problem (which token within the block?). The model
can learn to make landmark logits very different in scale from token logits without that
choice corrupting within-block attention.

**Step 3 — sparse inference via landmark gating.** At inference, materializing the grouped
softmax above costs the same as dense attention. The point of the construction is that, with
the landmarks already trained, we can replace the inner softmax over *every* block with a
sparse approximation:

$$
\widehat{\mathrm{Attn}}(\mathbf{q}_t) = \sum_{b \in \mathrm{TopK}(\mathbf{q}_t \cdot L)} \;\;\;\; \sum_{j \in \text{block } b} \alpha_{t,j} \, \mathbf{v}_j,
$$

where $L = [\boldsymbol{\ell}_1, \ldots, \boldsymbol{\ell}_C] \in \mathbb{R}^{C \times d_h}$
stacks the landmarks for the $C$ past blocks and $\mathrm{TopK}$ picks the highest-scoring
landmark indices. Mohtashami & Jaggi report $K = 2$ retrieved blocks works for their LLaMA-7B
fine-tune ([§4.3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.16300)). For blocks outside the top-$K$ set the
weight is treated as zero — the inner softmax never runs.

**Step 4 — cost accounting.** Let $C = T / W$ be the number of past blocks at position $t$,
$W$ the block size, $K$ the retrieval budget, $d_h$ the head dimension. Per query, per head:

$$
\mathrm{FLOPs} \approx \underbrace{C \cdot d_h}_{\text{landmark scoring}} + \underbrace{K \cdot W \cdot d_h}_{\text{in-block attention}} \;=\; d_h \cdot \left( \tfrac{T}{W} + K W \right).
$$

This is minimized at $W \approx \sqrt{T/K}$ — the classic two-level lookup balance. For
$T = 32{,}768$, $W = 50$, $K = 2$ that gives $\approx 755 d_h$ FLOPs per query vs.
$T d_h = 32{,}768 d_h$ for dense attention, a $\sim 43\times$ reduction at the FLOP level.
Memory follows the same shape: only the landmark matrix $L \in \mathbb{R}^{C \times d_h}$ and
the top-$K$ blocks need to live in fast memory at any given step.

**Step 5 — boundary handling.** Two details Mohtashami & Jaggi treat carefully:

- The *current block* (containing position $t$) and a small *recent window* of $r$ prior tokens
  are exempted from the gating — they are always attended to in full. This keeps short-range
  attention exact and avoids the failure mode where the top-$K$ blocks all sit deep in the past.
- The training-time grouped softmax uses *teacher-forced* chunk boundaries that match what the
  inference loop will see; the model never learns under a different partition than it deploys
  under ([§3.4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.16300)).

**Parameter count.** The only added parameters are the embedding of the landmark token —
$d_h \cdot H$ scalars in total, where $H$ is the number of heads, since the same landmark
embedding is used in every position. Across a typical 32-layer 4096-dim model that is on the
order of $\sim 4{,}000$ extra parameters, *not per layer*: the landmark is a special input
token, not a per-layer projection.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 3 · Reference implementation</p>
## Grouped softmax in PyTorch-style pseudocode

```python
# x: [B, T, d_model]  block-chunked with one landmark token appended per W real tokens
# block_id[t] in [0, C) tells which block position t belongs to
# is_landmark[t] is True iff position t is a landmark slot
def landmark_attn_train(q, k, v, block_id, is_landmark):
    # q, k, v: [B, T, H, d_h]
    logits = einsum("bthd,bshd->bhts", q, k) / sqrt(d_h)
    causal_mask(logits)

    # Outer softmax: only landmark keys participate, normalized over past blocks
    land_logits = logits.masked_fill(~is_landmark[None, None, None, :], -inf)
    outer = softmax(land_logits, dim=-1)            # [B, H, T, T], nonzero only at landmark cols

    # Inner softmax: only non-landmark keys, normalized within each block
    tok_logits = logits.masked_fill(is_landmark[None, None, None, :], -inf)
    # group_softmax normalizes within each block_id group along the key axis
    inner = group_softmax(tok_logits, group=block_id, dim=-1)

    # Combine: weight = outer-block-prob * inner-within-block-prob
    block_prob = scatter_to_token(outer, block_id)  # broadcast block weight to its tokens
    weights = block_prob * inner                    # [B, H, T, T]
    return einsum("bhts,bshd->bthd", weights, v)

def landmark_attn_infer(q_t, k_landmarks, v_landmarks, blocks_kv, K, recent_window):
    # k_landmarks: [C, H, d_h]   one per past block
    # blocks_kv: list of (K_b, V_b), each [W, H, d_h]
    scores = einsum("hd,chd->hc", q_t, k_landmarks) / sqrt(d_h)
    topk = scores.topk(K, dim=-1).indices            # [H, K]
    out = recent_attn(q_t, recent_window)            # always-on local window
    for h in range(H):
        for b in topk[h]:
            out[h] += attend(q_t[h], blocks_kv[b])   # full softmax inside selected block
    return out
```

The sketch elides three production concerns: (1) fused-kernel realization of the grouped
softmax — Mohtashami & Jaggi rely on a custom Triton kernel
([repo `epfml/landmark-attention`](https://github.com/epfml/landmark-attention)); (2)
position encoding for landmarks, which they handle by giving the landmark a position equal to
the *last* token of its block; (3) head-merged top-$K$ — heads can either vote individually
or share a single retrieved block set.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 4 · Empirical evidence</p>
## What is and isn't known

**Introducing paper (Mohtashami & Jaggi 2023).** Two main results:

1. *Perplexity at training length.* On English Wikipedia, a Transformer trained from scratch
   with landmark attention at block size $W = 50$ matches a dense-attention baseline within
   $\sim 0.05$ perplexity at sequence length 512, the training length
   ([Table 1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.16300)). The grouped softmax is *not* a quality
   regression at the trained length.
2. *Extrapolation to longer contexts.* Fine-tuning a LLaMA-7B checkpoint with landmark
   attention on 15B tokens of RedPajama at chunk size $W = 50$ produces a model that
   correctly retrieves a hidden passphrase from contexts up to $32{,}768$ tokens — well beyond
   LLaMA's 2K pretraining length — at $> 90\%$ accuracy on the "Passkey Retrieval" task they
   introduce ([Figure 3, §4.3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.16300)). A dense LLaMA-7B baseline
   drops to chance at $\sim 4{,}000$ tokens.

**Independent follow-up.** Passkey retrieval became the canonical lightweight long-context
probe after this paper; later work directly compares against landmark attention:

- *Activation Beacon* ([Zhang et al. 2024,
  arXiv 2401.03462](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.03462), Table 5) reproduces landmark attention
  on LLaMA-2-7B and reports comparable passkey accuracy at 32K context but a $\sim 2$ point
  PG-19 perplexity gap versus the beacon-based summarizer at equal context length — i.e.,
  landmark attention preserves *retrieval* well but loses *fluency* on broad-context tasks.
- *LongLoRA* ([Chen et al. 2023,
  arXiv 2309.12307](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.12307), §5) uses passkey as one of its
  evaluation tasks and confirms landmark attention's near-perfect recall at 32K, while
  reporting that LongLoRA's S²-attention reaches comparable recall with $\sim 30\%$ less
  fine-tuning compute.
- *Lost in the Middle* ([Liu et al. 2023,
  arXiv 2307.03172](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.03172)) and the broader "needle-in-a-haystack"
  literature inherited the chunked-passkey evaluation protocol that landmark attention's
  paper popularized.

**Sensitivity studies — what is *not* publicly known.** The introducing paper sweeps block size
$W \in \{25, 50, 100\}$ and retrieval budget $K \in \{2, 4\}$
([Table 4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.16300)), reporting that $W = 50, K = 2$ is the sweet spot
for their LLaMA-7B setup. There is *no* public study of: how landmark attention behaves on
tasks that require *aggregating* information across many distant blocks (e.g., document-level
summarization), how the design interacts with RoPE position scaling at extrapolation lengths
beyond 32K, or how it composes with GQA / MLA-style KV compression. I don't know of an
independent reproduction at $> 13$B parameters; the landmark-attention codebase pinned at
LLaMA-7B is what the field has compared against.

**Production adoption.** None recorded in this knowledge base. The technique sits in the
research lineage that fed into Activation Beacon, LongLoRA, and the broader 2024 long-context
retrieval-via-attention thread, but no frontier dense or MoE model ships landmark attention as
its long-context primitive.

<Summary>
**Landmark Attention in one breath.** Train a single learnable token per block under a grouped
softmax that forces it to summarize its block's content. At inference, queries score
landmarks, retrieve top-$K$ blocks, and run full attention only inside those blocks —
$O(T/W + K W)$ per query. Demonstrated to extend LLaMA-7B to 32K context for passkey-style
retrieval; weaker on broad-context tasks; no frontier production adoption.
</Summary>
