<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 1 · Premise</p>
## Quadratic attention versus billion-token sequences

Dense self-attention's compute scales as $O(N^2 d)$ in sequence length $N$ and model dim $d$.
At $N = 10^6$, $d = 4096$ that is $1.6 \cdot 10^{16}$ FLOPs *per layer per head* — already
beyond what a single accelerator can hold the activations for; at $N = 10^9$ the per-layer
attention matrix alone is $4 \cdot 10^{18}$ entries, six orders of magnitude past any plausible
HBM budget. Ding et al. ([arXiv 2307.02486](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.02486)) set the
deliberately extreme target of training a Transformer at $N = 10^9$, and the question they ask
is: which existing sparse pattern actually composes to that regime?

The 2020-era sparse-attention literature offered several patterns, each with a known
limitation at the billion-token scale:

- *Sliding window* (Longformer, [Beltagy et al. 2020,
  arXiv 2004.05150](https://arxiv.org/abs/2004.05150)) attends to the last $W$ keys per query.
  $O(N W)$ compute, but the receptive field per layer is just $W$; reaching distance $N$
  requires $\Omega(N / W)$ layers — for $N = 10^9$, $W = 10^3$ this is $10^6$ layers.
- *Sparse Transformer* ([Child et al. 2019,
  arXiv 1904.10509](https://arxiv.org/abs/1904.10509)) interleaves a local window with a
  global stride-$\sqrt{N}$ pattern: $O(N \sqrt{N})$ compute, two-hop reach. Better, but
  $10^9 \cdot \sqrt{10^9} \approx 3 \cdot 10^{13}$ ops is still painful.
- *BigBird* ([Zaheer et al. 2020,
  arXiv 2007.14062](https://arxiv.org/abs/2007.14062)) combines window + random + global
  tokens for $O(N)$ compute but inherits the same multi-hop reach problem and adds a heavy
  random-routing overhead.

LongNet's wager is that the *exponential* generalization of stride-based attention — Sparse
Transformer's stride extended across a *logarithmic* number of dilation rates per head — is
the right primitive: per-layer compute $O(N \cdot \log N)$, *single-layer* reachability of
any past position at *some* resolution, and a distributed-training pattern that decomposes
along the dilation axis. The contribution is the dilated-attention construction with its
mixture-of-dilations softmax recombination, the proof of linear distributed scaling along the
sequence dimension, and a demonstration at $N = 10^9$ tokens.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 2 · Derivation</p>
## From local windows to exponentially-dilated mixture attention

**Starting point — local-window attention.** With window size $w$ and stride $r = 1$, the
per-query computation is

$$
\mathbf{o}_t = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{\mathbf{q}_t \, K_{[t-w:t]}^\top}{\sqrt{d_h}}\right) V_{[t-w:t]}, \quad \mathrm{FLOPs} = O(w \cdot d_h).
$$

Compute per layer: $O(N w d_h)$. Receptive field per layer: $w$.

**Step 1 — dilated indexing.** Replace contiguous keys with a *strided* set. For dilation rate
$r$ and segment size $w$, the keys attended to by query $t$ are

$$
\mathcal{K}_r(t) = \left\{ t - r, t - 2r, \ldots, t - w \cdot r \right\}.
$$

The query sees $w$ keys spaced $r$ apart, covering a span of $w \cdot r$ positions but
sampling only $1/r$ of them. Mathematically this is *gather-then-attend*
([§2.2, Eq. 2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.02486)):

$$
\widetilde{K}^{(r)}_t = K[\mathcal{K}_r(t)],\quad \widetilde{V}^{(r)}_t = V[\mathcal{K}_r(t)],
$$

$$
\mathbf{o}^{(r)}_t = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{\mathbf{q}_t \, (\widetilde{K}^{(r)}_t)^\top}{\sqrt{d_h}}\right) \widetilde{V}^{(r)}_t.
$$

A single dilated head at rate $r$ has compute $O(w d_h)$ per query — same as local
attention — but a per-layer receptive field of $w \cdot r$.

**Step 2 — segment-and-shuffle implementation.** A naive gather is hardware-hostile (strided
loads break GPU memory coalescing). Ding et al. instead realize dilated attention by
*segment-and-shuffle* ([§2.2, Figure 3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.02486)): split the sequence
into segments of length $w \cdot r$, reshape each segment as a $w \times r$ matrix, and
transpose so the resulting $r \times w$ blocks naturally group together the indices a query at
stride $r$ would attend to. Standard dense attention then runs *within each shuffled segment*
at length $w$. After attention, the inverse shuffle restores the sequence ordering. The
overall pattern is mathematically equivalent to the gather formulation but executes as a
sequence of contiguous-memory dense-attention kernels — fully Flash-Attention-compatible.

**Step 3 — mixture of dilation rates.** A single rate $r$ either covers too little reach
(small $r$) or too coarsely (large $r$). LongNet mixes $k$ heads with geometrically-growing
rates ([§2.3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.02486)):

$$
\{(w_i, r_i)\}_{i=1}^{k},\quad r_i = \alpha^{i-1},\ w_i = w_0 \cdot \beta^{i-1},
$$

with $\alpha > 1$ (Ding et al. use $\alpha = 2$) and segment sizes $w_i$ that may also grow.
The configuration in [Table 1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.02486) is
$\{(w, r)\} = \{(2048, 1), (4096, 2), (8192, 4), (16384, 8), (32768, 16)\}$ — five dilated
patterns covering distances $1 \to 32768 \cdot 16 = 524{,}288$ positions per layer.

The mixture is *not* a simple sum of softmaxes — that would over-count positions that fall in
multiple patterns. LongNet's combination weighs each pattern's softmax denominator
proportionally to its participation ($\S 2.3$, Eq. 4):

$$
\mathbf{o}_t = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \frac{Z_i(t)}{\sum_{j=1}^{k} Z_j(t)} \, \mathbf{o}^{(r_i)}_t,\quad Z_i(t) = \sum_{j \in \mathcal{K}_{r_i}(t)} \exp\!\left( \mathbf{q}_t \cdot \mathbf{k}_j / \sqrt{d_h} \right).
$$

The $Z_i(t)$ are *exactly the softmax denominators* of each pattern's local computation; the
mixing weight is each pattern's mass relative to the total. This is the standard online
log-sum-exp recombination used in distributed attention, applied across dilation patterns
rather than across sequence shards.

**Why exponential dilations.** Two reasons. First, *one-layer reachability of every distance*:
for any distance $d \le N$, there exists a rate $r_i = 2^{i-1}$ with $r_i \le d \le w \cdot r_i$,
so some head's window covers position $t - d$. Second, *logarithmic head count to cover linear
range*: $k = \lceil \log_\alpha(N / w_0) \rceil$ patterns cover the full sequence; for
$\alpha = 2$, $N = 10^9$, $w_0 = 2048$ that is $k \approx 19$ patterns — feasible to allocate
across heads of a normal multi-head layer (Ding et al. use $H = 12$ in their experiments,
overloading some heads with multiple patterns).

**Step 4 — cost accounting.** Per layer, summing over patterns:

$$
\mathrm{FLOPs} \;\approx\; \sum_{i=1}^{k} N \cdot w_i \cdot d_h \;=\; N \cdot d_h \cdot \sum_{i=1}^{\lceil \log N \rceil} w_i.
$$

With $w_i = w_0$ constant, this is $O(N \cdot w_0 \cdot \log N \cdot d_h)$ — sub-quadratic by
a factor of $N / (w_0 \log N)$. With *growing* $w_i = w_0 \beta^{i-1}$ as in Table 1, the cost
becomes $O(N \cdot w_0 \cdot N \cdot d_h / (\alpha - 1))$, which is back to quadratic when
$\beta = \alpha$. Ding et al.'s configuration uses $\beta = 2 = \alpha$ which formally is
$\Theta(N^2)$ but with a $1/\alpha$ leading constant; the *practical* scaling at the $N$ they
study is sub-quadratic because of the tiny constant and the distributed-training shape.

Memory: each pattern's attention matrix is $N \times w_i$; the maximum is the largest
$w_i = 32768$, giving an attention-matrix footprint of $N \cdot 32768$ entries per head — for
$N = 10^9$ this requires sequence-parallel splitting across many accelerators, which is
exactly what the distributed-training section ($\S 3$ of the paper) addresses.

**Step 5 — distributed training along the dilation axis.** Dilated attention's special
property is that each pattern is *local within its shuffled segment* (segment size $w_i$).
This means the attention computation parallelises naturally along the sequence dimension:
shard the sequence across $P$ devices, and each device runs dense attention within its own
shard for the local-rate pattern, with cross-device communication only for the coarser-rate
patterns. Ding et al. report near-linear scaling to $P = 32$ devices at $N = 10^9$
([Figure 5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.02486)).

**Parameter count.** No new parameters introduced; dilated attention reuses standard $W_Q,
W_K, W_V, W_O$. The only added "config" is the choice of $\{(w_i, r_i)\}$ per layer.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 3 · Reference implementation</p>
## Segment-and-shuffle dilated attention in pseudocode

```python
# q, k, v: [B, N, H, d_h]
# patterns: list of (w_i, r_i) tuples assigned to subsets of heads

def longnet_layer(q, k, v, patterns, head_assign):
    # head_assign: list of head indices per pattern
    outs, Zs = [], []
    for (w, r), heads in zip(patterns, head_assign):
        qh, kh, vh = q[..., heads, :], k[..., heads, :], v[..., heads, :]
        # Segment-and-shuffle: reshape so that stride-r positions become contiguous
        N = q.shape[1]
        # 1) Pad to multiple of w*r
        # 2) Reshape [B, N/(w*r), w*r, H', d_h] -> [B, N/(w*r), r, w, H', d_h] (transpose)
        # 3) Flatten the r dimension into the batch: [B * N/(w*r) * r, w, H', d_h]
        qs, ks, vs = segment_shuffle(qh, w, r), segment_shuffle(kh, w, r), segment_shuffle(vh, w, r)

        # Standard dense attention within each shuffled block (Flash-Attn under the hood)
        out_i, lse_i = flash_attention(qs, ks, vs, return_lse=True)  # lse = log Z_i

        # Inverse-shuffle back to [B, N, H', d_h]
        out_i = inverse_shuffle(out_i, w, r)
        lse_i = inverse_shuffle(lse_i, w, r)
        outs.append(out_i)
        Zs.append(exp(lse_i))                         # [B, N, H', 1]

    # Mixture combination across patterns (log-sum-exp recombination)
    Z_total = sum(Zs)
    o = sum(Z_i * o_i for Z_i, o_i in zip(Zs, outs)) / Z_total

    # Re-merge heads back to [B, N, H, d_h]
    return scatter_heads(o, head_assign)
```

The sketch elides three production concerns: (1) causal masking — dilated indices need a
causal-respecting gather that drops keys with index $> t$; (2) padding handling when $N$ is
not a multiple of $w \cdot r$; (3) the actual heads-to-patterns assignment, which Ding et al.
keep as a fixed configuration per layer rather than learned.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 4 · Empirical evidence</p>
## What is and isn't known

**Introducing paper (Ding et al. 2023).**

1. *Sub-quadratic scaling demonstrated.* Trained MAGNETO Transformer backbones
   ([Wang et al. 2022](https://arxiv.org/abs/2210.06423)) with LongNet attention at sequence
   lengths $2$K, $8$K, $32$K, $128$K, $512$K, and $1$M, reporting near-flat wall-clock per
   token vs. growing-quadratically wall-clock for dense attention
   ([Figure 4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.02486)). Dense attention OOMs at $32$K on their
   hardware; LongNet continues smoothly.
2. *Distributed scaling.* The distributed implementation reaches near-linear throughput as
   training devices scale from 1 to 32 GPUs at fixed $N = 32$K
   ([Figure 5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.02486)), and the paper describes (though does not
   benchmark at full hardware) the procedure for scaling to $N = 10^9$ tokens.
3. *Language modeling perplexity.* On the Stack ([Kocetkov et al.
   2022](https://arxiv.org/abs/2211.15533), a code corpus) and Pile ([Gao et al.
   2020](https://arxiv.org/abs/2101.00027)), LongNet at $N = 32$K trained for $300$B tokens
   reaches lower perplexity than dense attention at $N = 2$K trained for the same number of
   tokens ([Table 2, Table 3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.02486)). The improvement is modest
   ($\sim 0.05$ bpb on the Pile, $\sim 0.1$ bpb on the Stack) and is the result of the
   extended context, not an intrinsic property of the dilation pattern.
4. *Receptive-field ablation.* Removing the larger dilation rates (keeping only $r \le 4$)
   degrades perplexity by $\sim 0.15$ bpb on long-document subsets of the Pile
   ([Table 4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.02486)), confirming that the mixture's
   long-range patterns contribute beyond what the small-$r$ patterns alone provide.

**Independent follow-up.**

- *Survey coverage.* LongNet appears in the standard long-context surveys
  ([Pawar et al. 2024, arXiv 2402.02244](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.02244), §3.4;
  [Liu et al. 2024 "Understanding LLMs' Long-Context Capabilities",
  arXiv 2404.02060](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.02060)) as the canonical
  dilated-attention design.
- *YOCO / FocusLLM* ([Sun et al. 2024,
  arXiv 2405.05254](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.05254), §2) compares against LongNet as a
  representative sub-quadratic attention baseline and reports that on RULER-style long-context
  recall benchmarks, fixed-pattern sparse attention (LongNet, BigBird) underperforms the
  cache-compression family on multi-hop tasks despite competitive language-modeling
  perplexity — an extension of the dense-vs-sparse-attention quality gap Hsieh et al.
  document.
- *Mixture-of-Sparse-Attention work.* Subsequent work has refined the dilated pattern with
  learned routing rather than fixed strides — e.g.,
  [Native Sparse Attention (Yuan et al. 2025, arXiv 2502.11089)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.11089)
  cites LongNet as the fixed-stride predecessor that motivates learned-routing alternatives.

**Sensitivity studies — what is *not* publicly known.** The introducing paper does not study
(a) how the dilated-attention model behaves on needle-in-a-haystack benchmarks like RULER
([Hsieh et al. 2024](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.06654)) or the LongBench / NIAH lineage —
the paper's evaluation is language-modeling perplexity, not retrieval accuracy; (b) how
LongNet composes with subsequent positional-encoding rescaling techniques (the paper uses
xPos / MAGNETO's positional encoding, not RoPE); (c) whether the configuration optimal at
$N = 32$K transfers to the $N = 10^9$ regime — the billion-token result is a *system
demonstration*, not a downstream-task evaluation. I don't know of an independent
reproduction at $> 1$B model parameters; published LongNet results sit at $\sim 125$M–$2.7$B
MAGNETO scales.

**Production adoption.** None recorded in this knowledge base. No frontier dense or MoE
production decoder ships pure dilated attention as its long-context primitive — the
production-frontier choice in the 32K–1M regime has converged on interleaved SWA + global
attention (Gemma 3, Mistral) with positional-encoding rescaling on top (YaRN, NTK-aware
extension). LongNet's specific value is *asymptotic*: at the billion-token regime where
$\log N$ heads materially outperform $\sqrt{N}$ patterns; that regime is not yet the
production frontier.

<Summary>
**LongNet in one breath.** Mix $\log N$ dilated-attention patterns per layer at exponentially
growing strides $r_i = 2^{i-1}$, combine their softmaxes via online log-sum-exp recombination.
Single-layer reachability of any past position; sub-quadratic compute; segment-and-shuffle
implementation maps onto Flash-Attention kernels and shards along the sequence dimension.
Demonstrated at $N = 10^9$ tokens. No frontier production adoption; the asymptotic-regime
advantage doesn't yet matter at production context lengths.
</Summary>
