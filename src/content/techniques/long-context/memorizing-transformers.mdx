<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 1 · Premise</p>
## Two ways out of the context cap

By 2022 there were two strategies for letting a transformer access information older than its
attention window:

1. **Compress the past.** Pack old activations into a smaller representation that current
   attention can still see — Compressive Transformer's approach. Lossy but fully differentiable.
2. **Retrieve from the past.** Maintain a non-differentiable database of past activations, and
   at inference time look up the most relevant entries by similarity. Lossless on retrieved
   items, but the retrieval mechanism doesn't get gradient signal.

Memorizing Transformers explored route 2 within a single transformer layer.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 2 · The kNN memory</p>
## Retrieve, then attend

Pick one mid-stack attention layer to augment. As text streams through the model, store every
K, V pair produced by that layer in an external memory bank — a vector database keyed by K.
At each new query position $t$:

1. Use $\mathbf{q}_t$ to look up the $k$ nearest keys in the external bank (k-Nearest-Neighbors,
   exact or approximate).
2. Add the retrieved $k$ (key, value) pairs to the normal in-window keys/values.
3. Run standard attention over the combined set.

```python
def memorizing_attn(q, k, v, external_memory, knn_k=32):
    # q, k, v: [B, T, H, d_h]
    # external_memory: KV database from past chunks
    retrieved_k, retrieved_v = external_memory.knn_lookup(q, knn_k)
    full_k = concat([k, retrieved_k], dim=2)
    full_v = concat([v, retrieved_v], dim=2)
    return scaled_dot_product_attention(q, full_k, full_v)
```

The external memory's vectors are *not* part of the computational graph: the kNN lookup is a
discrete operation, retrieved values are treated as constants. Gradients flow only through the
in-window K, V. This makes the memory cheap to store (no autograd state needed) and easy to
scale to millions of entries.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 3 · Where it sits in the layer stack</p>
## A single augmented layer is enough

Wu et al.'s ablation (Table 5) checked augmenting different layers. Mid-stack layers (e.g.,
the 9th of 12 in their setup) give the largest perplexity reduction. Augmenting early layers
helps less because the queries are too lexical; augmenting the final layer helps less because
its representations are already specialized for next-token prediction. Augmenting *every* layer
helps somewhat but at much greater memory cost.

The mid-stack-only design is part of what makes the technique tractable: a single layer's K, V
to index, not the whole stack.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 4 · Results</p>
## Empirical evidence

On PG-19 and arXiv math, Memorizing Transformers cut perplexity by 1.5–2 nats compared to a
matched-FLOP baseline transformer, with effective context extending to ~64K tokens at training
length 2K — a 32× extension via retrieval (Wu et al. Table 1). The memory bank during training
was the rolling buffer of past activations from the current document.

The technique never made the leap to frontier production decoders, for two reasons:

1. **The non-differentiable retrieval is awkward.** The model can learn to *use* retrieved
   K, V but cannot learn to retrieve better. Retrieval quality is upstream of attention quality.
2. **Retrieval-augmented generation** (RAG) accomplishes similar goals with a different
   architecture (retrieve documents into the prompt, run standard attention over the prompt)
   and is easier to deploy.

Memorizing Transformers remain a clean reference point: they showed that *attention itself* can
be wired to an external memory, separately from the question of whether to use one. The
mechanism survives in research on landmark attention, infinite-context memorization, and certain
retrieval-from-activations work.

<Summary>
**Memorizing Transformers in one breath.** Add a non-differentiable kNN memory of past K, V
pairs at one mid-stack layer. Retrieved entries merge into the layer's attention; effective
context extends 32× at fixed training length. Lost to RAG and compression-based long-context
in production, but remains the cleanest demonstration that attention can natively use external
memory.
