<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 1 · Premise</p>
## What BatchNorm could not do

BatchNorm (Ioffe & Szegedy 2015) normalized each channel across the batch dimension. It worked
well for convolutional vision models with large fixed-size batches, but had two structural
problems for sequence models:

1. **Batch-of-one inference.** During autoregressive generation the running statistics no longer
   reflect any meaningful population. BatchNorm requires storing moving averages and switching
   modes at eval time — fragile.
2. **Variable sequence length.** Padding tokens contaminate the per-batch statistics in ways
   that depend on which sentences happen to share a batch.

Ba, Kiros, and Hinton's fix: normalize each token across its own feature dimension instead of
across the batch.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 2 · The math</p>
## Center, scale, gain, shift

For an input vector $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^d$ at a single token position:

$$\mu = \frac{1}{d}\sum_{i=1}^{d} x_i,\quad \sigma = \sqrt{\frac{1}{d}\sum_{i=1}^{d}(x_i - \mu)^2 + \varepsilon},$$

$$\mathrm{LN}(\mathbf{x}) = \boldsymbol{\gamma} \odot \frac{\mathbf{x} - \mu}{\sigma} + \boldsymbol{\beta}.$$

Two reductions per call (the mean $\mu$, then the variance $\sigma^2$), an elementwise scale
$\boldsymbol{\gamma} \in \mathbb{R}^d$, an additive bias $\boldsymbol{\beta} \in \mathbb{R}^d$.
The output has zero mean and unit variance along the feature axis, then the affine $\boldsymbol{\gamma},
\boldsymbol{\beta}$ recovers per-feature degrees of freedom.

```python
def layer_norm(x, gamma, beta, eps=1e-5):
    # x: [..., d]    gamma, beta: [d]
    mean = x.mean(-1, keepdim=True)
    var = x.var(-1, keepdim=True, unbiased=False)
    return gamma * (x - mean) / (var + eps).sqrt() + beta
```

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 3 · Why it works for transformers</p>
## Independence from the batch

The original transformer (Vaswani et al. 2017) used LayerNorm twice per block — once after the
attention sub-layer's residual, once after the FFN's. This had a few consequences worth naming:

- **Inference is straightforward.** No running averages, no train/eval mode toggle.
- **Variable sequence lengths are trivially handled.** Each token computes its own statistics.
- **The forward signal is bounded.** After LN the output has unit variance per token, which
  constrains how much a single sub-layer's update can drift the residual stream.

The Post-Norm placement was the only thing the original paper got "wrong" in retrospect — see
the [norm-placement entry](/llm-arch-kb/normalization/norm-placement/) for the Pre-Norm vs
Post-Norm story.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 4 · Why it was eventually replaced</p>
## RMSNorm drops half of it

Zhang and Sennrich's 2019 RMSNorm paper showed that almost all of LayerNorm's stabilizing
effect comes from the *re-scaling*, not the *re-centering*. Dropping $\mu$ and $\boldsymbol{\beta}$
(keeping only the RMS-normalization and the gain $\boldsymbol{\gamma}$) gives essentially
identical quality at meaningfully less compute. The original LayerNorm paper's mean-subtraction
machinery has been carried by every transformer for nearly a decade for very little benefit;
RMSNorm is what is actually load-bearing.

See the [RMSNorm entry](/llm-arch-kb/normalization/rmsnorm/) for the analysis. Every open
frontier dense LLM since Llama 1 uses RMSNorm; plain LayerNorm survives only in legacy code,
encoder-decoder models, and vision transformers that haven't migrated.

<Summary>
**LayerNorm in one breath.** Per-token feature-axis normalization with learnable scale and
shift. Independent of batch size, trivial at inference, the workhorse of the 2017 transformer.
Modern decoders drop the mean and bias (see RMSNorm) at no quality cost; LayerNorm itself is
historical.
</Summary>
