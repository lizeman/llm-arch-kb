<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 1 · Premise</p>
## Why attention logits blow up

Attention computes softmax over scaled dot products:

$$\mathrm{Attn}(\mathbf{Q}, \mathbf{K}, \mathbf{V}) = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{K}^\top}{\sqrt{d_h}}\right)\mathbf{V}.$$

The $\sqrt{d_h}$ scaling is a *statistical* assumption: if Q and K entries are zero-mean unit-variance,
the dot product has variance $d_h$, so dividing by $\sqrt{d_h}$ keeps it $O(1)$. But the assumption
breaks the moment a single component of $\mathbf{q}$ or $\mathbf{k}$ is large. Then $\mathbf{q}^\top\mathbf{k}$
becomes dominated by that one term, the softmax sharpens to a single key, and gradients vanish on
every other key.

This shows up at long context, in mixed-precision (fp16/bf16) training, and during the first few
hundred steps before LayerNorm has stabilized the residual stream.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 2 · The fix</p>
## Normalize Q and K first

Apply a per-head RMSNorm (or LayerNorm) to Q and K *before* taking the dot product:

$$\tilde{\mathbf{q}} = \mathrm{RMSNorm}(\mathbf{q}),\quad \tilde{\mathbf{k}} = \mathrm{RMSNorm}(\mathbf{k}),$$
$$\mathrm{logits} = s\cdot\tilde{\mathbf{Q}}\tilde{\mathbf{K}}^\top.$$

Each vector now has unit RMS, so the dot product is bounded by the head dim regardless of input
outliers. A small learnable scale $s$ (often a single scalar per head) replaces the $1/\sqrt{d_h}$
factor since the statistics are now fixed.

```python
def qk_norm_attn(q, k, v, q_norm, k_norm, scale, mask=None):
    # q, k, v: [B, H, T, d_h]
    q = q_norm(q)
    k = k_norm(k)
    logits = (q @ k.transpose(-2, -1)) * scale
    if mask is not None:
        logits = logits.masked_fill(mask, float("-inf"))
    return logits.softmax(-1) @ v
```

The per-head norm parameters are tiny (a single gain vector of size $d_h$ per head) and add
negligible compute.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 3 · Why it matters at scale</p>
## Empirical evidence

Dehghani et al. (2023, "Scaling Vision Transformers to 22B Parameters", arXiv 2302.05442) report
that without QK-Norm, attention logits diverged catastrophically during ViT-22B training,
preventing the model from training at all in bf16. Adding QK-Norm let the same recipe complete
training. The paper presents this as a hard requirement past a certain scale.

Gemma 3 (2025) adopted QK-Norm as standard, citing similar stability concerns on text-only
training at 27B and below. The technique has also been picked up by several closed-frontier
recipes (per public blog disclosures), though those models do not enter this knowledge base per
the [closed-model policy](/llm-arch-kb/about/).

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 4 · The cost</p>
## Almost nothing

QK-Norm adds two RMSNorm calls per attention layer — one on Q, one on K. Both reduce over the
head-dim axis, which is small. On a typical 70B model with 64 heads at 128 head-dim, the extra
compute is well under 1% of attention's total flop count.

The only conceptual cost is that the softmax sharpness is now controlled entirely by the
learnable scale $s$ rather than by the magnitude of Q and K. In practice this is easier to tune,
not harder.

<Summary>
**QK-Norm in one breath.** A small RMSNorm on Q and K before the dot product caps attention logit
magnitude regardless of activation outliers. Mandatory for stable bf16 training past a few
billion parameters; nearly free in compute; adopted in Gemma 3 and frontier-scale ViTs.
</Summary>
