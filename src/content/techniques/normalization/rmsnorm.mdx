<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 1 · Premise</p>
## What LayerNorm actually does

For an input vector $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^d$ at a single token position, LayerNorm computes:

$$\mathrm{LN}(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{\mathbf{x} - \mu}{\sigma}\,\boldsymbol{\gamma} + \boldsymbol{\beta},
\quad \mu = \tfrac{1}{d}\sum_i x_i,\quad \sigma = \sqrt{\tfrac{1}{d}\sum_i (x_i - \mu)^2 + \varepsilon}.$$

Two statistics are estimated per token: the mean $\mu$ (re-centering) and the standard deviation
$\sigma$ (re-scaling). The learnable gain $\boldsymbol{\gamma}$ and bias $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ then
re-introduce per-feature degrees of freedom.

The hypothesis Zhang and Sennrich tested in 2019: the re-centering step does very little. Most
of LayerNorm's stabilizing effect comes from the re-scaling.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 2 · The simplification</p>
## Just normalize by RMS

RMSNorm drops $\mu$ and $\boldsymbol{\beta}$, normalizing instead by the root mean square:

$$\mathrm{RMSNorm}(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{\mathbf{x}}{\mathrm{RMS}(\mathbf{x})}\,\boldsymbol{\gamma},
\quad \mathrm{RMS}(\mathbf{x}) = \sqrt{\tfrac{1}{d}\sum_i x_i^{\,2} + \varepsilon}.$$

The activation is rescaled to unit RMS, then a per-feature gain $\boldsymbol{\gamma}$ is applied.
No mean subtraction, no additive bias.

```python
def rms_norm(x, g, eps=1e-6):
    # x: [..., d]  g: [d] learnable gain
    rms = torch.rsqrt(x.pow(2).mean(-1, keepdim=True) + eps)
    return x * rms * g
```

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 3 · Why it works</p>
## Re-scaling is what matters

The original paper's analysis: the gradient of LayerNorm with respect to $\mathbf{x}$ decomposes
into a re-scaling term and a re-centering term. Empirically the re-centering term contributes
little to gradient flow at the scales relevant to large transformers. Dropping it does not
destabilize training.

Concretely: removing the mean estimation eliminates one reduction along the feature dimension
per call, plus the subtraction, plus the bias parameters. On GPUs, the reduction itself is the
bottleneck — fewer reductions means lower memory traffic.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 4 · Results</p>
## Empirical evidence

The original paper (Table 4) reports RMSNorm reducing per-step time by 7–64% across translation
and language-modeling benchmarks vs LayerNorm, with no statistically significant quality
difference on the tested tasks.

In modern transformer training, the reported speedup is smaller in absolute terms because
attention dominates wall-clock time. But RMSNorm is invoked twice per block (Pre-Norm) or more
(sandwich placements), so the cumulative savings are non-trivial — and there is no quality
reason to prefer LayerNorm.

Every open frontier dense LLM family from Llama 1 onward uses RMSNorm. It is the consensus
choice and a load-bearing piece of the 2024–26 stack.
