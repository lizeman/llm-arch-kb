<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 1 · Premise</p>
## Position without embeddings

Sinusoidal and learned position embeddings inject position into the input. RoPE injects it into
Q and K via rotation. ALiBi takes the third option: inject it into the attention *scores*.

The intuition: distant tokens should be attended to less, all else equal. A static linear
distance penalty on the attention logits encodes that intuition directly, with no learnable
parameters and no per-position computation.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 2 · The bias</p>
## A linear penalty per head

For head $h$ with slope $m_h$, ALiBi modifies the attention logits as:

$$\text{logit}^{(h)}_{i,j} = \frac{\mathbf{q}^{(h)}_i \cdot \mathbf{k}^{(h)}_j}{\sqrt{d_h}} - m_h \cdot (i - j),\quad j \leq i.$$

The slopes $m_h$ are fixed, not learned: a geometric sequence over heads. For $H = 8$ heads,
the slopes are $1/2^1, 1/2^2, \ldots, 1/2^8$. Different heads see different effective windows —
the head with the steepest slope attends very locally, the shallowest-slope head attends almost
uniformly.

```python
def alibi_bias(seq_len, num_heads, device):
    # Geometric slope sequence: 1/2^(8/H), 1/2^(16/H), ... down to 1/2^8
    slopes = torch.tensor([2 ** (-8.0 * h / num_heads) for h in range(1, num_heads + 1)],
                          device=device)
    # Distance matrix (i, j) -> i - j  (clamped to ≥0 since attention is causal)
    pos = torch.arange(seq_len, device=device)
    dist = pos[:, None] - pos[None, :]                 # [T, T]
    bias = -slopes[:, None, None] * dist.clamp(min=0)  # [H, T, T]
    return bias
```

The bias matrix is computed once per layer, not per token, so the overhead is negligible.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 3 · Length extrapolation</p>
## Why it generalizes

The headline result from Press et al. (Figure 1): a model trained at sequence length 1024 with
ALiBi maintains its perplexity essentially unchanged when evaluated at sequence length 16384 —
no fine-tune, no scale adjustment, nothing. Sinusoidal and learned positional embeddings, by
contrast, diverge sharply past their training context.

The mechanism: every additional token simply gets a slightly bigger penalty than the previous
ones. The model never has to extrapolate any "new" positional value; it only has to handle a
larger negative bias, which the softmax handles gracefully by routing more attention toward
recent tokens.

For research and prototyping use cases that need "trained at 1K, run at 100K", ALiBi remains
the simplest answer.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 4 · Why RoPE won anyway</p>
## What the linear bias gives up

Three things tilted the production landscape toward RoPE rather than ALiBi:

1. **Relative position is the *only* signal in ALiBi.** All knowledge of position lives in a
   linear penalty. A model cannot easily learn that "position 0" or "position 5" is structurally
   special. RoPE's frequency ladder gives the model a richer relative-position substrate.
2. **No long-range fine control.** ALiBi forces every head to commit to one linear slope —
   you cannot have a head that pays attention to position $t-1024$ specifically and ignores
   $t-512$ and $t-2048$. RoPE's rotations let the model learn arbitrary relative-distance
   patterns.
3. **Empirically, RoPE-based models tend to do better on long-range reasoning benchmarks**
   (Press et al. report this too, Section 5) once the context is filled with genuinely relevant
   content rather than mostly-irrelevant fillers.

BLOOM (Scao et al. 2022) and MPT (MosaicML 2023) used ALiBi at scale; both lineages later moved
toward RoPE for their successors. ALiBi survives as a research baseline and in resource-constrained
settings where its parameter-free simplicity is valuable.

<Summary>
**ALiBi in one breath.** Fixed per-head linear bias on attention scores. Zero learnable
positional parameters, free length extrapolation (1K → 100K with no degradation), and one big
limitation: every head commits to one linear penalty. RoPE outperforms on dense LLM long-range
tasks; ALiBi stays useful as a clean baseline.
</Summary>
