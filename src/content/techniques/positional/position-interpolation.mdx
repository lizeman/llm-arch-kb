<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 1 · Premise</p>
## RoPE extrapolation is the real problem

A model trained at context length $L$ has only ever seen RoPE rotation angles $t \cdot \theta_i$
for positions $0 \leq t \leq L - 1$. When asked to process position $t = 10L$ at inference, the
high-index (slow) dimensions rotate to angles well outside the training distribution. Attention
degrades quickly past $L$.

The naive extension idea — extending the position range and hoping for the best — fails badly.
PI's question: what if we just *never let position values exceed L*, by squishing the new
position range into the old one?

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 2 · The interpolation</p>
## Divide by the extension factor

For extension factor $s = L_{\text{new}} / L_{\text{train}}$ (e.g., $s = 4$ to go from 4K to
16K), replace position $t$ with $t/s$ when computing RoPE angles:

$$\text{angle}(t, i) = \frac{t}{s} \cdot \theta_i = t \cdot \frac{\theta_i}{s}.$$

Equivalently: divide each rotation rate $\theta_i$ by $s$, leaving positions unchanged. The two
formulations produce identical attention outputs.

Critically, every rotation angle now lies in the originally-trained range — nothing extrapolates.
The model sees angles it has experience with, just spread out over more tokens.

```python
def position_interpolation_rope(x, position, theta, scale):
    # Same as standard RoPE, but with scaled position.
    angles = (position / scale) * theta
    cos = angles.cos()
    sin = angles.sin()
    x1, x2 = x[..., 0::2], x[..., 1::2]
    return torch.stack([x1 * cos - x2 * sin, x1 * sin + x2 * cos], dim=-1).flatten(-2)
```

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 3 · The cost</p>
## All bands lose resolution equally

PI's drawback: every dimension's rotation rate is divided by $s$. This is benign for the slow
high-index dimensions (which barely rotated anyway), but it discards resolution on the fast
low-index dimensions that the model had previously used to distinguish positions $t$ and $t+1$.

After PI with $s = 4$, the fast dimension's rotation between adjacent tokens is $1/4$ what it
was. Two adjacent positions are now harder to distinguish — the model's short-range
positional acuity degrades.

[YaRN](/llm-arch-kb/positional/yarn/) directly addresses this: keep the fast dimensions
untouched (they already saw their full domain), only interpolate the slow ones. The three-band
recipe in YaRN is precisely the per-dimension fix for what PI does too aggressively.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 4 · Results</p>
## Empirical evidence

Chen et al. (Table 5) report extending Llama 7B / 13B / 33B from 2K to 32K context via PI plus
1000 fine-tuning steps. Perplexity at the new length matches the 2K baseline within 0.3 nats —
the technique works, and works cheaply.

Where it loses: needle-in-a-haystack retrieval at very long context shows degradation past
about $4\times$ extension. The short-range positional acuity loss compounds when the task needs
precise position resolution. YaRN's report (arXiv 2309.00071, Table 1) shows ~0.5 perplexity
points lower than PI at $32\times$ extensions, attributable to the band-aware rescaling.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 5 · Where it sits</p>
## The technique that opened the line

PI was the cheapest RoPE-extension recipe to demonstrate that *yes, context-extension via
position rescaling is viable*. The descendants — NTK-aware rescaling, YaRN, LongRoPE — refine
which dimensions to rescale and by how much, but they all inherit PI's basic move: change
$\theta_i$, don't change anything else, fine-tune briefly, ship.

Production decoders almost universally use YaRN or scaled-base RoPE rather than pure PI, but
PI is the prototype that the rest are descendants of.

<Summary>
**PI in one breath.** Squish position values by the extension factor before applying RoPE.
Every dimension's rotation rate gets divided uniformly. Works (cheap fine-tune extends Llama-7B
to 32K), loses short-range positional acuity, opened the RoPE-extension research line that
YaRN and LongRoPE later refined.
</Summary>
