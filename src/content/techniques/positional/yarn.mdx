<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 1 · Premise</p>
## RoPE's training-length boundary

Rotary position embeddings ([Su et al., 2021](https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.09864)) rotate the $i$-th
coordinate pair of every query and key by an angle proportional to the token's absolute position:

$$
\theta_i = b^{-2i/d_h}, \qquad \text{angle}(t, i) = t \cdot \theta_i,
$$

with base $b = 10000$ in the original RoPE recipe. The head dimension $d_h$ is partitioned into
$d_h/2$ rotation rates that form a geometric ladder from $\theta_0 = 1$ (one radian per position)
down to $\theta_{d_h/2-1} \approx b^{-(d_h-2)/d_h} \approx 10^{-4}$. During pretraining the model
observes positions $0 \leq t < L$ for some fixed $L$ — 2048 for Llama 1, 4096 for Llama 2 and
DeepSeek V1, 8192 for OLMo 3. The fast dimensions ($i$ near $0$) cycle through the unit circle
many times across that window; the slow dimensions ($i$ near $d_h/2$) traverse only a small arc.

The naive way to extend the model to context $L_\text{new} = s \cdot L$ is to keep RoPE unchanged
and let positions $t \geq L$ feed through directly. This fails for a frequency-specific reason
that matters for the rest of the entry. The fast bands have already toured the full circle;
rotating them further produces angles drawn from the same training distribution and they remain
in-domain. The *slow* bands have only ever been rotated by small angles in $[0, L \theta_i]$;
rotating to positions $t \approx 10 L$ feeds the model angles it has never seen, and the
attention dot product between far-apart tokens collapses
([Chen et al., 2023, §2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.15595);
[Peng et al., 2023, §3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.00071)). The Position Interpolation paper
attributes the failure to "high-frequency catastrophe" only in passing; the more careful YaRN
analysis distinguishes it as a slow-band extrapolation problem with a fast-band *aliasing*
problem layered on top once interpolation is applied.

[Position Interpolation](/llm-arch-kb/positional/position-interpolation/) (Chen et al., June 2023)
takes the simplest route: rescale every position by $1/s$ so that all rotation angles stay in the
trained range. It works, but uniformly degrades the fast bands' positional acuity — adjacent
tokens that previously differed by one full radian of rotation now differ by only $1/s$. The
[NTK-aware](/llm-arch-kb/positional/ntk-aware-rope/) recipe (bloc97, July 2023; later formalized
in the YaRN paper) corrects this by rescaling the *base* $b$ instead, which by the geometric
ladder hits slow bands much harder than fast ones. NTK-aware works zero-shot up to $s \approx 8$
but its band treatment is still tied to a single closed-form. YaRN's contribution is to make the
band treatment explicit: classify each dimension by its *wavelength relative to the training
window* and apply a different rescaling per class, then correct the softmax entropy drift that
any positional rescaling at long context induces.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 2 · Derivation</p>
## NTK-by-parts and the temperature ramp

**RoPE's frequency-band structure.** Each rotation rate $\theta_i$ has an associated *wavelength*
$\lambda_i = 2\pi / \theta_i = 2\pi \, b^{2i/d_h}$ — the number of positions required for the
$i$-th coordinate pair to complete one full rotation. For the canonical $b = 10000$, $d_h = 128$
geometry, $\lambda_0 = 2\pi \approx 6.28$ positions at the fastest band and $\lambda_{63} = 2\pi
\cdot 10000^{126/128} \approx 5.85 \times 10^4$ at the slowest. The *rotation count* over training
window $L$ is

$$
r_i = \frac{L \, \theta_i}{2\pi} = \frac{L}{\lambda_i}.
$$

A dimension with $r_i \gg 1$ has been "saturated" — it cycled through the unit circle many times
and the model saw its full rotational support. A dimension with $r_i \ll 1$ has only ever swept a
fraction of the circle. This distinction drives every later decision
([Peng et al., 2023, §3.3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.00071)).

**Step 1: Position Interpolation as uniform stretch.** PI rescales every angle by $1/s$:

$$
\theta_i^{\text{PI}} = \frac{\theta_i}{s}, \qquad s = \frac{L_\text{new}}{L}.
$$

Every band's wavelength stretches by $s$. The slow bands benefit (their new angles still fall in
the trained range), but the fast bands lose precision: two adjacent positions that previously had
phase difference $\theta_i \approx 1$ now differ by $\theta_i / s \approx 1/s$, well below the
phase noise the model learned to handle. The model's short-range positional acuity drops, which
is why PI needs fine-tuning even at modest $s$
([Chen et al., 2023, Table 5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.15595)).

**Step 2: NTK-aware as band-dependent stretch.** Replacing the base $b$ with $b' = b \cdot s^{d_h
/ (d_h - 2)}$ produces

$$
\theta_i^{\text{NTK}} = (b')^{-2i/d_h} = \theta_i \cdot s^{-2i/(d_h - 2)},
$$

which equals $\theta_i$ at $i = 0$ (fastest dimension untouched) and $\theta_i / s$ at $i = d_h/2
- 1$ (slowest dimension matches PI). The rescaling factor is a smooth geometric ramp in $i$. This
is better than PI, but it conflates the *index* $i$ with the *rotation count* $r_i$ — geometry
says nothing about whether band $i$ has actually saturated over the training window. A
32-dimensional head with $b = 10000$ has very different saturation behavior than a 128-dimensional
head with the same base; the NTK-aware rescaling treats them the same.

**Step 3: NTK-by-parts.** Peng et al. propose classifying dimensions by their saturation $r_i$
rather than their index $i$. Define two cutoffs $r_{\min}$ and $r_{\max}$ on the rotation count:

- **Slow band** ($r_i \leq r_{\min}$): dimension has not saturated. Interpolate it (PI's recipe)
  because extrapolating untrained angles is the failure mode.
- **Fast band** ($r_i \geq r_{\max}$): dimension has saturated. Leave it untouched. Both
  extrapolated and interpolated angles fall inside the trained support.
- **Middle band** ($r_{\min} < r_i < r_{\max}$): smoothly blend. Define the ramp

$$
\gamma(r_i) = \begin{cases} 0 & r_i \geq r_{\max} \\ 1 & r_i \leq r_{\min} \\ \dfrac{r_{\max} - r_i}{r_{\max} - r_{\min}} & \text{otherwise}\end{cases}
$$

and apply

$$
\theta_i^{\text{YaRN}} = (1 - \gamma(r_i)) \, \theta_i + \gamma(r_i) \, \frac{\theta_i}{s}.
$$

At $\gamma = 0$ the original $\theta_i$ survives; at $\gamma = 1$ the band gets full PI rescaling;
in between the band's effective rotation rate is a linear interpolation in $\theta_i$ between the
two. The paper defaults to $r_{\min} = 1, r_{\max} = 32$ for Llama-2 7B/13B at $L = 4096$, which puts
roughly the fastest dimension pairs in the untouched fast band, the slowest in the interpolated
slow band, and the remainder in the smooth middle
([Peng et al., 2023, §3.3 and Figure 2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.00071)).

The "by-parts" naming makes the contrast with NTK-aware explicit: instead of a global geometric
ramp tied to head index, YaRN applies a *piecewise* ramp tied to per-band saturation. The two
schedules agree only by coincidence on specific architectures.

**Step 4: Attention temperature.** A second effect surfaces independently of the rescaling
choice. At inference with sequence length $T$, the attention softmax distributes mass over $T$
keys per query; the entropy of the distribution grows roughly with $\log T$. The pretraining
model was calibrated for entropy near $\log L$. Stretching the context to $T = sL$ without
compensation leaves attention "flatter" than training — a phenomenon Peng et al. report as a
measurable perplexity penalty independent of which rescaling scheme is chosen
([Peng et al., 2023, §3.4 and Figure 3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.00071)).

Their fix: divide attention logits by a temperature $t$ before the softmax, with $t$ chosen so
the average per-query entropy matches the pretraining length. The empirically fit form is

$$
\frac{1}{\sqrt{t}} = 0.1 \, \ln s + 1, \qquad \text{equivalently} \qquad t = \frac{1}{(0.1 \ln s + 1)^2}.
$$

For $s = 16$ this gives $1/\sqrt{t} \approx 1.28$; for $s = 32$, $1/\sqrt{t} \approx 1.35$. The
constants $0.1$ and $1$ were fit to Llama-1 7B and reused for Llama-2 7B/13B without per-model
tuning. Because attention logits are computed as $\mathbf{q}^\top \mathbf{k} / \sqrt{d_h}$, the
temperature can be folded into the existing $\sqrt{d_h}$ denominator: divide by $\sqrt{d_h \cdot
t}$ at inference, equivalent to scaling the query (or key) by $1/\sqrt{t}$ before the dot product.
This makes the temperature correction free at inference time, in contrast to the rescaling step
which is also free but requires recomputing the cosine/sine tables.

**Parameter count and compute.** YaRN adds zero parameters — it changes the precomputed
$(\theta_i)_{i < d_h/2}$ table and a single scalar temperature. The cost at inference is the same
as standard RoPE plus one multiplication per logit by $1/\sqrt{t}$. The training cost is the
fine-tune itself: 400 steps at $L_\text{new}$ on $\sim 64$M tokens for Llama-2 7B/13B
([Peng et al., 2023, §4.1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.00071)), roughly 0.1% of pretraining tokens.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 3 · Reference implementation</p>
## Sketch

```python
# Shapes: d_h head dim, T_train training context, s extension factor.
# Frequencies and ramp computed once per layer at config time; applied per token.

def yarn_frequencies(d_h, base=10000.0, T_train=4096,
                     s=16.0, r_min=1.0, r_max=32.0):
    i = arange(d_h // 2)                          # [d_h/2]
    theta = base ** (-2 * i / d_h)                # [d_h/2]   original RoPE
    wavelength = 2 * pi / theta                   # [d_h/2]   λ_i
    rotations = T_train / wavelength              # [d_h/2]   r_i

    # piecewise ramp γ(r_i): 0 = preserve, 1 = interpolate
    gamma = clip((r_max - rotations) / (r_max - r_min), 0.0, 1.0)

    theta_yarn = (1 - gamma) * theta + gamma * (theta / s)   # [d_h/2]
    return theta_yarn

def yarn_temperature(s):
    # 1/sqrt(t) = 0.1 ln s + 1; fold into the sqrt(d_h) denominator at inference
    return (0.1 * log(s) + 1.0) ** 2

def yarn_attention(q, k, v, theta_yarn, pos, d_h, s):
    # q, k, v: [B, T, H, d_h]; pos: [T]
    angles = pos[:, None] * theta_yarn[None, :]   # [T, d_h/2]
    cos, sin = angles.cos(), angles.sin()
    q = apply_rotary(q, cos, sin)                 # standard RoPE rotation
    k = apply_rotary(k, cos, sin)

    t = yarn_temperature(s)                       # scalar
    logits = einsum("bthd,bshd->bhts", q, k) / sqrt(d_h * t)   # [B, H, T, T]
    return softmax(logits, dim=-1) @ v
```

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 4 · Empirical evidence</p>
## Ablations and adoption

**Perplexity on PG19 and Proof-Pile.** Peng et al. fine-tune Llama-2 7B and 13B for 400 steps at
$L_\text{new} \in \{64\text{K}, 128\text{K}\}$ from a 4K base. On PG19 at the 64K evaluation
point, YaRN at $s = 16$ reaches lower perplexity than PI and the NTK-by-parts ablation (no
temperature) at matched fine-tune budget; the temperature correction is the load-bearing
difference between rows 4 and 5 of the paper's
[Table 1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.00071). On Proof-Pile the gap widens — Peng et al. report
YaRN beating PI and dynamic-NTK by roughly 0.1 perplexity points at 64K and more at 128K
([Peng et al., 2023, Table 2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.00071)). The temperature term
contributes a consistent improvement over NTK-by-parts alone: small per token, free at inference.

**Passkey retrieval.** Table 3 of the paper reports near-perfect retrieval at depths through
80-100K on the Llama-2 7B 64K and 128K models, with PI dropping substantially at the deepest
probe and zero-shot NTK-aware dropping further still. The discrepancy between perplexity and
passkey results is non-trivial — perplexity gaps are tenths of points, retrieval gaps are tens of
percentage points. The paper does not resolve this: a model can score well on average next-token
prediction while still missing the specific long-range copy operation that needle-in-a-haystack
measures. Later analyses ([Liu et al., 2023, "Lost in the Middle"](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.03172))
confirm that retrieval-style probes are more sensitive to positional acuity than averaged
perplexity, but the dissociation remains an open question for any RoPE extension scheme.

**Adoption in production decoders.** DeepSeek-V2 reports YaRN as the chosen recipe for extending
its 4K-trained base to 128K, citing sample efficiency over uniformly scaled base RoPE
([DeepSeek-V2, §3.3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.04434)). DeepSeek-V3 keeps the same 4K → 128K
YaRN extension across its 671B-parameter geometry
([DeepSeek-V3, §3.4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.19437)). OLMo 3 32B applies YaRN with scaling
factor 8 to extend from 8K to 64K during a dedicated long-context fine-tuning stage
([OLMo 3 model card](https://huggingface.co/allenai/Olmo-3-1125-32B)). The Qwen 3 family uses
YaRN combined with Dual Chunk Attention to extend beyond a 32K ABF-trained window
([Qwen 3 technical report](https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.09388)); Qwen 3-Next reportedly pushes the
262K native context to ~1M via YaRN
([Qwen 3-Next model card](https://huggingface.co/Qwen/Qwen3-Next-80B-A3B-Instruct)). The
[gpt-oss-120b release](https://huggingface.co/openai/gpt-oss-120b) documents YaRN scaling from a
4K base to 131K with RoPE base 150K.

**Where YaRN is not used.** Llama 3.1 reaches 128K context without YaRN, using long continued
pretraining over a much larger token budget with a uniformly scaled RoPE base
([Llama 3.1 paper, §5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.21783)). The Meta team's note is that with
sufficient training tokens, simpler base scaling matches YaRN's quality and removes the recipe
complexity. This is consistent with the YaRN paper's own framing of the method as a
*sample-efficient* extension, not a quality ceiling — at 0.1% of pretraining tokens YaRN beats the
alternatives, but the gap narrows when continued pretraining is generous.

**Unresolved questions.** Three are worth flagging. First, no public study isolates the
temperature term's contribution at very large $s$ (256+); the constants $0.1$ and $1$ were fit at
moderate scales. Second, the perplexity-vs-retrieval divergence noted above means low PG19
perplexity is not a sufficient proxy for long-context recall, but no replacement metric has
become standard. Third, the choice of $r_{\min}, r_{\max}$ defaults is justified by visual inspection
in the paper's Figure 2; whether other architectures need different cutoffs is not systematically
studied. [LongRoPE](/llm-arch-kb/positional/longrope/) sidesteps the cutoff question by treating
the per-dimension factors as a search variable, which empirically recovers non-monotone schedules
YaRN cannot express — evidence that the closed-form ramp is good but not optimal at extreme
extensions.

<Summary>
**YaRN in one breath.** NTK-by-parts: classify each RoPE dimension by its training-window
rotation count, preserve fast bands, interpolate slow bands, ramp smoothly in between, and
rescale softmax temperature by $1/\sqrt{t} = 0.1 \ln s + 1$ to keep attention entropy calibrated.
32× context extension on Llama-2 in 400 steps; adopted by DeepSeek V2/V3, OLMo 3, Qwen 3, and
gpt-oss for context windows from 64K to ~1M.
</Summary>
