<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 1 · Premise</p>
## The Post-Norm penalty

Standard Post-Norm transformers have a clean theoretical property: every block's output is
renormalized to unit variance before being passed on. Each layer sees a controlled distribution.
This is desirable for expressivity reasons — it prevents the residual stream from drifting.

The problem (per Xiong et al. 2020, see the [norm-placement entry](/llm-arch-kb/normalization/norm-placement/))
is that Post-Norm gradients shrink multiplicatively with depth and the architecture diverges
past ~12 layers without careful warmup. Pre-Norm fixed the gradient issue but at the price of
unbounded residual-stream growth.

DeepNet's question: can you keep Post-Norm's stable distribution and *fix* the gradient
problem by rescaling the residual?

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 2 · The scaling</p>
## β-scaled residuals

DeepNet uses Post-Norm placement but adds a scalar $\beta$ to the residual addition:

$$\mathbf{x}_{\ell+1} = \mathrm{Norm}\bigl(\beta \cdot \mathbf{x}_\ell + f_\ell(\mathbf{x}_\ell)\bigr).$$

The trick: $\beta$ is not learned. It's set to a constant derived from the network's depth $N$
such that the forward-pass variance and gradient magnitudes are both bounded.

For an $N$-layer encoder, Wang et al. derive

$$\beta_{\text{enc}} = (8N)^{-1/4},\quad \alpha_{\text{enc}} = (2N)^{1/4},$$

where $\alpha$ scales the initialization of the sublayer weights. The decoder formulas are
analogous with constants that account for both encoder and decoder depth.

The result is an *initialization recipe* rather than a training-time modification: you compute
$\alpha, \beta$ once based on the architecture's depth, apply them at init and freeze. No
learnable scalar to track during training.

```python
def deepnet_residual(x, sublayer, beta):
    # beta is depth-derived constant, e.g., (8N)^(-1/4) for an N-layer encoder
    return layer_norm(beta * x + sublayer(x))
```

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 3 · Results</p>
## Empirical evidence

Wang et al.'s headline (Figure 1): **1000-layer DeepNet trains stably from scratch on machine
translation**, where comparable Pre-Norm or vanilla Post-Norm transformers diverge well below
that depth. At more pedestrian scales (Transformer-Big, T5-XXL), DeepNet matches or slightly
outperforms Pre-Norm baselines on BLEU, with the same training-step count.

The mechanism the paper formalizes: at initialization, the per-layer gradient magnitude is
*independent* of depth. Stacking another 100 layers does not change the early-step gradient
seen by layer 1 — there is no signal attenuation to compensate for.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 4 · Why production decoders didn't adopt it</p>
## The "merely deep" regime

Like ReZero ([entry](/llm-arch-kb/residual/rezero/)), DeepNet is theoretically clean but
practically lives in academia. Two reasons:

1. **Depth 32–80 is the production regime.** At those depths, Pre-Norm + RMSNorm + warmup is
   well-understood and known to work. DeepNet's edge appears past 100 layers, where production
   models don't go.
2. **The $\beta$ constants are architecture-specific.** Changing the model's depth requires
   re-deriving $\beta$ from scratch. Pre-Norm is depth-agnostic, which is operationally simpler.

DeepNet's framework — depth-derived initialization constants that bound forward/backward
variance — is also the conceptual ancestor of the careful-init story in OLMo 2's Post-Norm
revival. The lineage is alive in current open-frontier research, just not under the same name.

<Summary>
**DeepNet in one breath.** Post-Norm residual rescaled by a depth-derived constant β = (8N)^(-1/4).
Trains 1000-layer transformers from scratch. Lives in research because production caps out at
~80 layers where Pre-Norm + RMSNorm has incumbent advantages.
</Summary>
