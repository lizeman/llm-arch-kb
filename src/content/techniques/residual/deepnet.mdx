<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 1 · Premise</p>
## Post-Norm has the geometry; Pre-Norm has the stability

Post-Norm transformers — Vaswani et al.'s original placement, normalization *after* the
residual addition — have a clean theoretical property: every block's output is renormalized to
controlled variance before the next block reads it
([Vaswani et al. 2017, §3.1](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.03762)). The residual stream never
drifts because the normalization step caps its magnitude after every write. Empirically, when
Post-Norm transformers *do* train, they often reach lower final loss than equivalently sized
Pre-Norm baselines ([Liu et al. 2020, Table 2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2004.08249)).

The catch: Post-Norm trains badly past $\sim 12$ layers without a careful warmup schedule. The
diagnosis (Xiong et al. 2020, [§4.1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2002.04745)) is that the gradient
norm at the first block shrinks like $\mathcal{O}(1/\sqrt{L})$ under standard initialization,
so deep stacks see gradients orders of magnitude smaller at the input than at the output. Early
training steps then bias the optimizer toward updating only late layers, often catastrophically.

Pre-Norm placement — normalization *inside* the residual branch — was the response. It trains
deep stacks reliably (the [norm-placement entry](/llm-arch-kb/normalization/norm-placement/)
covers the zoo) but loses the stable-distribution-across-depth property: the residual stream's
norm grows monotonically with depth, and layers deep in the network see a different
distribution than shallow ones.

Wang et al. (2022) ask: can the Post-Norm geometry be kept, while the depth-induced gradient
shrinkage is *initialized away* rather than fought through warmup? Their answer — DeepNet, the
paper's name; *DeepNorm*, the construction's name in the body — scales to a 1000-layer
encoder-decoder.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 2 · Derivation</p>
## α-rescaled residual, β-shrunk init

The construction modifies a Post-Norm transformer in exactly two places. First, the residual
addition is rescaled before normalization:

$$
\mathbf{x}_{\ell+1} = \mathrm{LayerNorm}\bigl(\alpha\, \mathbf{x}_\ell + f_\ell(\mathbf{x}_\ell)\bigr).
$$

The constant $\alpha > 1$ enlarges the contribution of the residual relative to the sublayer
output before the normalization renormalizes both. Second, the sublayer's internal weights —
specifically the FFN's $W_1, W_2$, the attention's value projection $W_V$, and the output
projection $W_O$ — are initialized by scaling Xavier's variance by $\beta$:

$$
W \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(0,\; \beta^2 \cdot \mathrm{Var}_{\text{Xavier}}(W)\right).
$$

With $\beta < 1$, the sublayer's output at initialization has reduced variance, so the
$f_\ell(\mathbf{x}_\ell)$ term contributes less than the $\alpha\, \mathbf{x}_\ell$ term at
step zero. The post-block normalization then makes the output close to a renormalization of
$\mathbf{x}_\ell$ itself — i.e., close to identity, which is the regime the
[ReZero](/llm-arch-kb/residual/rezero/) and [residual](/llm-arch-kb/residual/residual-overview/)
entries identified as the gradient-friendly one.

**The depth-derived constants.** For an $N$-layer encoder-only stack, Wang et al. derive

$$
\alpha_{\text{enc}} = (2N)^{1/4}, \qquad \beta_{\text{enc}} = (8N)^{-1/4}.
$$

For an encoder-decoder with $N$ encoder layers and $M$ decoder layers, the decoder uses
$\alpha_{\text{dec}} = (3M)^{1/4}$ and $\beta_{\text{dec}} = (12M)^{-1/4}$
([Wang et al. 2022, §3.2, Theorem 1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.00555)). These are
*constants*, derived once from architecture choices, frozen for training — no learnable
parameter, no schedule.

**Where the constants come from.** The derivation (§3, Theorem 1) bounds the per-step model
update $\|\mathbf{x}^{(t+1)}_L - \mathbf{x}^{(t)}_L\|$ in terms of depth and weight-update
magnitude. Without DeepNorm, the bound grows like $\mathcal{O}(L)$ in depth, which is what
causes Post-Norm divergence. With $\alpha = (2N)^{1/4}$ and $\beta = (8N)^{-1/4}$, the bound
becomes a *constant* in $L$: the same SGD step size produces the same expected model-output
change regardless of depth. Wang et al. argue (§4) that this depth-invariant update budget is
what unlocks 1000-layer training.

**Forward-pass variance preservation.** A complementary calculation (§3.3) tracks the variance
of $\mathbf{x}_\ell$ across depth. The combined choice of $\alpha$ and $\beta$ keeps the
expected pre-norm variance roughly constant: the $\alpha$ scaling boosts the residual term, the
$\beta$ scaling shrinks the sublayer term, and after LayerNorm both effects are absorbed into a
unit-variance output. The first block sees the same activation statistics as the thousandth.

**Why the quarter-root.** The Theorem 1 bound on the model update at depth $L$ involves a sum
of $L$ terms each bounded by $\beta^2 / \alpha^2$. Setting the bound to be $\mathcal{O}(1)$
requires $\beta^2 / \alpha^2 \sim 1/L$, i.e. $\beta / \alpha \sim L^{-1/2}$. The factor-of-two
distribution between $\alpha$ and $\beta$ — $\alpha \propto L^{1/4}$, $\beta \propto L^{-1/4}$
— is the symmetric choice that minimizes the constant in front of the bound. The encoder's $8$
in the denominator and the decoder's $3$ in the numerator come out of carefully bounding the
self-attention and cross-attention contributions separately.

**Relationship to ReZero.** Both ReZero and DeepNet damp the sublayer contribution at init —
ReZero with a learnable scalar at zero, DeepNet with a constant $\beta < 1$ multiplied into the
initialization variance. The difference: ReZero's $\alpha_\ell$ multiplies the sublayer
*output* and is learned, allowing per-layer adaptation; DeepNet's $\beta$ shrinks the sublayer
weights and stays fixed, but adds the $\alpha > 1$ boost on the residual side to keep Post-Norm
geometry. DeepNet trades adaptivity for the Post-Norm distribution-control property.

**Parameter and compute cost.** Zero extra parameters; two extra scalar multiplies per block
($\mathcal{O}(B \cdot T \cdot d)$ FLOPs), negligible against the sublayer.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 3 · Reference implementation</p>
## DeepNorm block, sketch

```python
def deepnorm_block(x, sublayer, alpha, layer_norm):
    # x: (B, T, d) — residual stream
    # alpha: depth-derived constant, e.g., (2N)^(1/4) for an N-layer encoder
    # sublayer weights have been initialized with variance shrunk by beta = (8N)^(-1/4)
    return layer_norm(alpha * x + sublayer(x))            # Post-Norm placement

def deepnorm_init(model, alpha_beta_pairs):
    # Re-initialize FFN W_1/W_2, attention V_proj, and output_proj weights with std *= beta
    for layer, (_, beta) in zip(model.layers, alpha_beta_pairs):
        for w in [layer.ffn.w1, layer.ffn.w2, layer.attn.v_proj, layer.attn.out_proj]:
            nn.init.xavier_normal_(w.weight, gain=beta)
```

The $\alpha$ scalar appears in the forward pass; the $\beta$ shrinkage appears only at
initialization. Other architectural details (number of heads, $d$, FFN ratio) are unchanged
from a standard Post-Norm transformer.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 4 · Empirical evidence</p>
## What DeepNet trains and what others reproduced

**Headline experiment** ([Wang et al. 2022, §4.1, Figure 1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.00555)):
a 1000-layer (500 encoder + 500 decoder) DeepNet trains stably from scratch on machine
translation. The matched 1000-layer vanilla Post-Norm baseline diverges within the first
hundred steps; the Pre-Norm baseline trains but the 1000-layer configuration does not
outperform a 200-layer model, suggesting wasted capacity. DeepNet improves monotonically with
depth up to $L = 1000$.

**Multilingual translation at depth-200** (§4.2, Table 2): a 200-layer DeepNet trained on a
102-language multilingual translation benchmark beats the 48-layer M2M-100-12B
([Fan et al. 2020](https://arxiv.org/abs/2010.11125)) by +5 BLEU on average. The benchmarks
include high-resource language pairs (En–De, En–Fr) and low-resource directions (En–Zu,
En–Mr); DeepNet's gain is largest on low-resource pairs, consistent with the hypothesis that
deeper stacks help when data is scarce. The depth comparison is direct evidence that DeepNet's
stability translates to a usable quality lift at scales no Post-Norm transformer could reach
before.

**Standard-depth comparisons** (§4.3, Tables 3–4): at Transformer-Big (6+6 layers) and T5-large
(24+24 layers), DeepNet matches or marginally beats Pre-Norm baselines on WMT'14 EN-DE BLEU and
T5-style downstream tasks, with the same training-step budget. No regression in the regime
where Pre-Norm already works.

**Independent corroboration via OLMo 2's Post-Norm revival.** Olmo et al. (2025) describe a
"reordered-norm" variant where normalization is applied after the residual branch's compute
but before the addition; their stability analysis credits DeepNorm-style depth-derived weight
init for the recipe's behavior on the 13B model
([OLMo Team 2025, §3.1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.00656)). The lineage is alive in current
open-frontier work, even when not under the "DeepNet" name.

**B2T-Connections (Takase et al. 2023).** A follow-up evaluates DeepNorm against an
"Bypass-to-Transformer" residual variant and finds DeepNorm provides the strongest stability
benefits at 18+ encoder layers on machine translation, but B2T narrows the gap at moderate
depths
([§4, arXiv 2206.00330](https://arxiv.org/abs/2206.00330)). The agreement on the basic
mechanism — depth-aware residual rescaling — strengthens the DeepNorm analysis.

**No frontier-scale ablation of DeepNet against Pre-Norm + RMSNorm at the production depth
tier.** Open frontier labs ship 60–130-layer dense decoders, well below DeepNet's claimed
sweet spot. Llama 3.1 405B is 126 layers
([Grattafiori et al. 2024, §3.1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.21783)), DeepSeek-V3 is 61 layers
([DeepSeek-AI 2024, §2.1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.19437)), all on Pre-Norm + RMSNorm. The
public depth where DeepNet's gradient-bound argument matters — past 100 layers — is roughly
where production caps out, and no released frontier training run has reported a DeepNet head-
to-head at that depth. The strongest available evidence remains the Wang et al. translation
benchmarks.

**Why DeepNet isn't standard.** Two factors. First, $\alpha, \beta$ depend on depth, so the
recipe re-derives constants whenever architecture width or depth changes — Pre-Norm + RMSNorm is
depth-agnostic, which is operationally simpler. Second, at $L \leq 80$ the gradient bound
DeepNet improves is already tame under warmup + Pre-Norm, so the lift over the consensus is
small to nonexistent in the depth tier production actually ships. The recipe is theoretically
clean and empirically validated at 200–1000 layers; its impact remains downstream of whether
labs ever ship that deep.

<Summary>
**DeepNet in one breath.** Post-Norm residual with two depth-derived constants: scale the
residual addition by $\alpha = (2N)^{1/4}$, shrink sublayer-weight init by $\beta = (8N)^{-1/4}$.
Bounds the per-step model update independently of depth, unlocking stable training of
1000-layer encoder-decoders where vanilla Post-Norm and Pre-Norm both fail. Reportedly improves
low-resource translation at 200 layers and gives matched quality at standard depths. Not in
production decoders because frontier dense models cap below the depth tier where the
mechanism's benefit becomes load-bearing.
</Summary>
