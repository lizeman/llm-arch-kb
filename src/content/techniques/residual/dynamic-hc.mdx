<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 1 · Premise</p>
## Static routing leaves expressivity on the table

[Static Hyper-Connections](/llm-arch-kb/residual/hyper-connections/) (SHC) generalize the
single residual stream to $n$ parallel streams plus learned per-layer read/write coefficient
vectors $\mathbf{a}_\ell^{(r)}, \mathbf{a}_\ell^{(w)} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ and a depth-rate matrix
$\mathbf{B}_\ell \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$. The construction is strictly more expressive
than the standard residual ([Zhu et al. 2024, §3.1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2409.19606)), but it
applies the same routing pattern to every token at layer $\ell$.

The implicit assumption is that all tokens benefit from the same layer-$\ell$ stream mix.
That is unlikely to hold uniformly. A noun-phrase token might benefit from carrying its
semantic features through one stream while a function-word token routes positional information
through another. Mixture-of-Experts routing — token-level expert selection at every layer —
already concedes the same point about token-conditional structure
([Shazeer et al. 2017, §2.1](https://arxiv.org/abs/1701.06538)); applying the same principle
to residual-stream routing is the Dynamic Hyper-Connections (DHC) contribution.

DHC is presented in the same paper as SHC and is the variant that produces the strongest
empirical results in the paper's tables; it can be read as the "real" Hyper-Connections
proposal, with SHC as the conservative starting point that makes the gains attributable to the
multi-stream construction rather than to per-token gating in general.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 2 · Derivation</p>
## Predict $\mathbf{a}^{(r)}, \mathbf{a}^{(w)}, \mathbf{B}$ from the per-token state

Start from the SHC update at layer $\ell$ for a single token at sequence position $t$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{r}_\ell^{(t)} &= (\mathbf{a}_\ell^{(r)})^{\!\top}\, \mathbf{H}_\ell^{(t)},\\
\mathbf{u}_\ell^{(t)} &= f_\ell(\mathbf{r}_\ell^{(t)}),\\
\mathbf{H}_{\ell+1}^{(t)} &= \mathbf{B}_\ell\, \mathbf{H}_\ell^{(t)} + \mathbf{a}_\ell^{(w)}\, (\mathbf{u}_\ell^{(t)})^{\!\top}.
\end{aligned}
$$

In SHC, the three routing parameters $\mathbf{a}_\ell^{(r)}, \mathbf{a}_\ell^{(w)}, \mathbf{B}_\ell$
are layer-constants. DHC promotes them to *functions of the per-token state*
$\mathbf{H}_\ell^{(t)} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d}$ via small linear projections.

**Token summary.** First, build a $d$-dimensional summary of the token's $n$-stream state using
a learned mix $\mathbf{m} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ that is shared across layers:

$$
\bar{\mathbf{x}}_\ell^{(t)} = \mathbf{m}^{\!\top}\, \mathbf{H}_\ell^{(t)} \;\in\; \mathbb{R}^d.
$$

In the paper's implementation, $\mathbf{m}$ is initialized to uniform so the summary starts as
a stream average; the network learns to skew the summary toward whichever streams carry the
most decision-relevant features.

**Per-token read and write coefficients.** Project the summary onto two $\mathbb{R}^n$ vectors:

$$
\mathbf{a}_\ell^{(r),(t)} = W_\ell^{(r)}\, \bar{\mathbf{x}}_\ell^{(t)} + \mathbf{a}_\ell^{(r,0)}, \qquad \mathbf{a}_\ell^{(w),(t)} = W_\ell^{(w)}\, \bar{\mathbf{x}}_\ell^{(t)} + \mathbf{a}_\ell^{(w,0)},
$$

where $W_\ell^{(r)}, W_\ell^{(w)} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d}$ are per-layer projection
weights and $\mathbf{a}_\ell^{(r,0)}, \mathbf{a}_\ell^{(w,0)} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ are per-layer
biases that recover SHC behavior when the projection-weight contribution is small. Each token
thus picks its own read/write mix.

**Per-token rate matrix.** The depth-rate matrix is also predicted per token, via a low-rank
projection that keeps the parameter count tractable:

$$
\mathbf{B}_\ell^{(t)} = \mathbf{B}_\ell^{(0)} + (W_\ell^{(B,u)} \bar{\mathbf{x}}_\ell^{(t)})\, (W_\ell^{(B,v)} \bar{\mathbf{x}}_\ell^{(t)})^{\!\top}, \qquad W_\ell^{(B,u)}, W_\ell^{(B,v)} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d}.
$$

The outer product of two predicted $\mathbb{R}^n$ vectors gives a rank-1 perturbation on top of
the per-layer constant $\mathbf{B}_\ell^{(0)}$. (The paper allows the rank to be lifted by
predicting multiple such outer products; the rank-1 form is the lightweight default.)

**The DHC update.** Substitute the per-token coefficients into the SHC update:

$$
\mathbf{H}_{\ell+1}^{(t)} = \mathbf{B}_\ell^{(t)}\, \mathbf{H}_\ell^{(t)} + \mathbf{a}_\ell^{(w),(t)}\, (\mathbf{u}_\ell^{(t)})^{\!\top}, \quad \mathbf{u}_\ell^{(t)} = f_\ell\!\bigl((\mathbf{a}_\ell^{(r),(t)})^{\!\top} \mathbf{H}_\ell^{(t)}\bigr).
$$

The sublayer $f_\ell$ is unchanged — DHC adds routing machinery *around* attention and FFN
without touching their internals.

**Reduction to SHC.** Set $W_\ell^{(r)} = W_\ell^{(w)} = W_\ell^{(B,u)} = W_\ell^{(B,v)} = 0$.
The per-token coefficients collapse to their per-layer biases and DHC becomes SHC. DHC is a
strict generalization, just as SHC is a strict generalization of the standard residual.

**Why this and not "predict the full $\mathbf{B}$ unconstrained."** A full per-token
$\mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ matrix would require $n^2 d$ parameters per layer
($\sim 2.6 \times 10^5$ weights at $n = 4$, $d = 4096$) just for the rate matrix — comparable
to the rest of the block. The rank-1 (or low-rank) parameterization keeps the routing prediction
much smaller than the sublayer compute while preserving the expressivity gain. The same
low-rank-routing argument appears in MoE literature ([Shazeer et al. 2017,
§2.1](https://arxiv.org/abs/1701.06538)).

**Initialization for stability.** The projection weights $W_\ell^{(\cdot)}$ are initialized to
zero or near-zero; the biases recover SHC's initialization
([Zhu et al. 2024, §3.3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2409.19606)). Network behavior at step zero is
exactly SHC; per-token routing emerges as the projection weights grow during training. This
guarantees that DHC inherits SHC's depth-stability properties at initialization.

**Parameter and compute overhead over SHC.** Per layer, four additional $\mathbb{R}^{n \times d}$
projections ($W_\ell^{(r)}, W_\ell^{(w)}, W_\ell^{(B,u)}, W_\ell^{(B,v)}$) — total $4nd$
parameters. At $n = 4, d = 4096$: $\sim 65$K parameters per layer, against $\sim 200$M for the
attention and FFN. Compute overhead is four small matmuls
$\mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^n$ per token per layer: $\mathcal{O}(B \cdot T \cdot n \cdot d)$
FLOPs, $\sim n / d$ times smaller than the sublayer compute and therefore negligible. Activation
memory is the same as SHC: $n \times d$ per token per layer.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 3 · Reference implementation</p>
## Dynamic Hyper-Connection block, sketch

```python
def dhc_block(H, sublayer, mix_m, W_r, W_w, W_Bu, W_Bv, a_r0, a_w0, B0):
    # H: (B, T, n, d) — stack of n parallel residual streams per token
    x_bar = (mix_m[None, None, :, None] * H).sum(dim=-2)        # (B, T, d) token summary
    a_r = W_r @ x_bar + a_r0                                    # (B, T, n) per-token read mix
    a_w = W_w @ x_bar + a_w0                                    # (B, T, n) per-token write mix
    B_u = W_Bu @ x_bar                                          # (B, T, n) low-rank rate factor
    B_v = W_Bv @ x_bar                                          # (B, T, n)
    B_t = B0 + torch.einsum("bti,btj->btij", B_u, B_v)          # (B, T, n, n) per-token rate matrix

    r = (a_r[..., None] * H).sum(dim=-2)                        # (B, T, d) read
    u = sublayer(r)                                             # (B, T, d) sublayer
    H = torch.einsum("btij,btjd->btid", B_t, H)                 # (B, T, n, d) rate-mix update
    H = H + a_w[..., None] * u[..., None, :]                    # write per-token
    return H
```

The routing parameters become per-token tensors rather than per-layer constants; the rest of
the SHC machinery is preserved.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 4 · Empirical evidence</p>
## What DHC adds over SHC

**Headline language-modeling result**
([Zhu et al. 2024, §4.2, Table 1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2409.19606)): on a 1.3B dense LM at
matched parameter count and training budget, DHC outperforms SHC ($n = 4$) by 0.2 perplexity
and the Pre-Norm baseline by 0.8 perplexity. The DHC advantage over SHC grows modestly at the
7B-active MoE scale to 0.3 perplexity (Table 3), suggesting per-token routing is more
load-bearing as the model has more representational capacity to specialize.

**Depth stability** (§4.3, Figure 4): DHC at 96 layers matches SHC's improved seed-to-seed
stability over the Pre-Norm baseline. The per-token routing does not destabilize training; the
initialization (projection weights at zero) recovers SHC behavior at step zero.

**Routing analysis** (§4.6, Figure 6): the paper visualizes the learned per-token routing
patterns and finds that DHC clusters tokens by syntactic / semantic role (high-frequency
function words route differently from content words) at the routing-coefficient level. The
mechanism is interpretable in a way SHC cannot be, since SHC's routing is token-independent by
construction.

**Vision** (§4.5): the consistent ranking DHC > SHC > Pre-Norm holds on ViT ImageNet
classification and DiT image generation. Cross-modality consistency strengthens the per-token-
routing benefit claim.

**Comparison to MoE-style token routing.** DHC's routing mechanism is mechanistically similar
to Mixture-of-Experts routing
([Shazeer et al. 2017, §2.1](https://arxiv.org/abs/1701.06538)) but applied at the
*residual-stream level* rather than the FFN-expert level. The two are complementary: a MoE
transformer with DHC residuals would have token-level routing both for which FFN expert to
consult and for which residual stream to write back into. The Zhu et al. MoE experiment (§4.4)
combines DHC and DeepSeekMoE-style expert routing; they do not interfere and the gains compose.

**Independent reproduction.** As with [SHC](/llm-arch-kb/residual/hyper-connections/), no
independent frontier-scale reproduction has appeared in the open literature as of May 2026. The
DenseFormer family ([Liu et al. 2024, §3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.02622)) is the closest
related design — depth-wise dense connections between residual states — but DenseFormer's
mixing is static across tokens, more comparable to SHC than to DHC.

**Production status.** No publicly documented production deployment of DHC. The ByteDance
Doubao team that introduced the technique does not publish enough architecture detail on its
production models to confirm or rule out use; per the closed-model policy, no claim is made
either way. Other open frontier labs continue with the standard residual or with placements
(Gemma 3's norm-everywhere, OLMo 2's reordered norms) that operate on a single stream.

For the static rate-matrix construction that DHC sits on top of, see
[Hyper-Connections](/llm-arch-kb/residual/hyper-connections/). The two entries cover the same
paper (arXiv 2409.19606) but separate the multi-stream contribution from the per-token routing
contribution so each can be evaluated on its own.

<Summary>
**Dynamic HC in one breath.** Promote SHC's per-layer routing constants
$\mathbf{a}_\ell^{(r)}, \mathbf{a}_\ell^{(w)}, \mathbf{B}_\ell$ to per-token functions via four
small projections from a learned token-state summary $\bar{\mathbf{x}}_\ell^{(t)}$. Each token
picks its own read/write mix and a low-rank perturbation to the depth-rate matrix; the rest of
the SHC machinery is unchanged. Reduces to SHC when the projection weights are zero. Reportedly
+0.2 perplexity over SHC and +0.8 over Pre-Norm on the 1.3B LM benchmark; per-token routing
clusters by token role. No publicly documented production adoption.
</Summary>
