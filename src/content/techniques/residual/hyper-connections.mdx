<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 1 · Premise</p>
## The residual stream is a bottleneck

Every transformer block reads from and writes to the *same* residual stream
$\mathbf{x}_\ell \in \mathbb{R}^d$. Attention and FFN sublayers compete for the same vector
space; useful information from layer 5 has to coexist with useful information from layer 50 in
the same $d$ coordinates.

Empirical studies of trained models suggest the residual stream is in fact
information-saturated — many features are stored in approximately orthogonal subspaces, and
some heads explicitly write to subspaces only to be overwritten by later layers. The
single-stream design forces this superposition.

Hyper-Connections (Zhu et al. 2024) ask: what if there were $n$ residual streams instead of
one, with learnable mixing between them?

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 2 · The construction</p>
## n streams, learned read/write

A Hyper-Connection block maintains a stack of $n$ streams
$\mathbf{X}_\ell \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d}$. The sublayer $f$ reads from a learned linear
combination of those streams, computes its update, and writes the update back as a learned
linear combination:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{r}_\ell &= \mathbf{A}_\ell^{\!\top}\, \mathbf{X}_\ell \in \mathbb{R}^d & \text{(read)}\\
\mathbf{u}_\ell &= f_\ell(\mathbf{r}_\ell) \in \mathbb{R}^d & \text{(sublayer compute)}\\
\mathbf{X}_{\ell+1} &= \mathbf{X}_\ell + \mathbf{B}_\ell\, \mathbf{u}_\ell^{\!\top} & \text{(write)}
\end{aligned}
$$

The matrices $\mathbf{A}_\ell \in \mathbb{R}^{n}$ and $\mathbf{B}_\ell \in \mathbb{R}^{n}$ are
learned per-layer. At $n = 1$ with $\mathbf{A}_\ell = \mathbf{B}_\ell = 1$, the construction
reduces to the standard residual connection. For $n > 1$, the architecture has strictly more
expressivity: a sublayer can read from one stream and write to a different one, deferring
information across many layers without contention.

```python
def hyper_connection_block(X, sublayer, A, B):
    # X: (n, d) — the stack of n parallel residual streams
    # A, B: (n,) — learned read and write coefficients for this layer
    r = A @ X                    # (d,) read: mix streams into a single vector
    u = sublayer(r)              # (d,) standard sublayer compute on the read vector
    X = X + np.outer(B, u)       # (n, d) write: distribute the update across streams
    return X
```

The parameter count cost is small: two $n$-vectors per layer instead of nothing for the
standard residual. Memory is $n \times$ the residual stream size — for $n = 4$, that's a 4×
KV-cache-equivalent overhead on the activation memory during training.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 3 · Static vs dynamic</p>
## Two variants

The paper introduces two parameterizations:

- **Static Hyper-Connections (SHC).** $\mathbf{A}_\ell$ and $\mathbf{B}_\ell$ are fixed
  parameters learned per layer. Same shape across all tokens at that layer.
- **Dynamic Hyper-Connections (DHC).** $\mathbf{A}_\ell$ and $\mathbf{B}_\ell$ are computed
  *per-token* as a small projection of the current $\mathbf{r}_\ell$ — the layer learns to
  route different tokens through different streams.

DHC is more expressive (each token gets its own read/write pattern), at the cost of an extra
projection per layer. SHC is simpler and benchmarks competitively in the paper's ablations on
small to medium models.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 4 · What the paper claims</p>
## Reported results

Zhu et al. report (Table 2) HC variants matching or beating Pre-Norm baselines on a 1.3B
language modeling benchmark, with DHC giving the best loss at the same parameter count. The
gap is small at small scale and grows modestly at larger scale within the paper's experimental
range.

The bigger claim is qualitative: HC stabilizes very deep training (the paper trains 96-layer
models that the Pre-Norm baseline cannot match without spike-suppression tricks) and reduces
the variance of training loss across runs. The mechanism the authors offer: with $n$ streams,
no single block can disturb the global residual state — perturbations stay confined to whichever
stream a layer chose to write into.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 5 · Production status</p>
## Where it stands

As of May 2026, Hyper-Connections has not been confirmed in a publicly documented production
release. The technique is plausible enough — and the parameter overhead small enough — that
labs running their own architecture studies may be experimenting with it internally. Public
adoption claims should wait for a tech report or model card.

The conceptual lineage continues older threads: Highway Networks (Srivastava et al. 2015), the
multi-head residual sketch in NormFormer (Shleifer et al. 2021,
[entry](/llm-arch-kb/residual/normformer/)), and ReZero's per-branch scalar
([entry](/llm-arch-kb/residual/rezero/)) all explored "what if the residual were richer than
identity." Hyper-Connections is the most aggressive form so far.
