<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 1 · Premise</p>
## Pre-Norm leaves residual-stream norm uncontrolled

Pre-Norm Pre-RMSNorm transformers train stably but the residual stream's norm grows
monotonically with depth: each block adds a perturbation without renormalizing the stream
itself (see the [residual-stream entry](/llm-arch-kb/residual/residual-overview/)). Shleifer
et al. observed that this unbounded growth is correlated with slower convergence — the model
spends compute learning to compensate for distributional drift across depth that does not
carry useful signal.

NormFormer's approach: add three small normalization terms that selectively curb the growth
without compromising Pre-Norm's gradient-flow properties.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 2 · The three additions</p>
## Where the extra norms go

For a standard Pre-Norm block `x ← x + sublayer(norm(x))`, NormFormer adds:

1. **LayerNorm after self-attention.** A LayerNorm applied to the attention sub-layer's output
   *before* the residual addition: `x ← x + LN_post_attn(attn(LN(x)))`. Caps the magnitude of
   the attention block's per-step contribution to the stream.
2. **Head-wise scaling of attention outputs.** A per-head learnable scalar
   $\gamma_i \in \mathbb{R}$ multiplied into each head's output before the output projection
   $W_O$: $\mathrm{head}_i \leftarrow \gamma_i \cdot \mathrm{head}_i$. Lets the model attenuate
   noisy heads' contributions to the residual.
3. **LayerNorm after the FFN's first linear.** Inside the FFN, after the first linear and
   activation but *before* the second linear: $\mathrm{FFN}(x) = W_2 \cdot \mathrm{LN}(\sigma(W_1 x))$.
   Stabilizes the activations the second projection sees.

```python
def normformer_block(x, attn_qkv, W_O, ffn_w1, ffn_w2,
                     ln_pre_attn, ln_post_attn, ln_pre_ffn, ln_mid_ffn,
                     head_scales):
    # ----- attention sub-block -----
    h = attn_qkv(ln_pre_attn(x))                          # (B, T, num_heads, d_h)
    h = h * head_scales[None, None, :, None]              # (2) per-head scaling
    a = W_O(flatten_heads(h))                             # output projection
    x = x + ln_post_attn(a)                               # (1) extra LN after attn

    # ----- FFN sub-block -----
    h = ffn_w1(ln_pre_ffn(x))
    h = ln_mid_ffn(gelu(h))                               # (3) extra LN inside FFN
    x = x + ffn_w2(h)
    return x
```

The three additions cost only a handful of extra learnable parameters per block — the paper
reports +0.4% parameter overhead, negligible against the FFN and attention.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 3 · Results</p>
## Empirical evidence

Shleifer et al. report NormFormer reaching the baseline Pre-Norm GPT-3-style model's final
perplexity in **24% fewer steps** at the 1.3B scale, with consistent gains across the studied
range of 125M to 2.7B parameters and across both causal and masked LM pretraining. At matched
compute, the 1.3B model reaches 0.27 perplexity lower than the baseline, and downstream
zero-shot performance matches GPT-3-Large 60% faster.

The mechanism the paper attributes: Pre-Norm transformers suffer a *gradient magnitude
mismatch* — early layers see much larger gradients than late ones. The three extra norms
re-balance this, so deeper layers see a stable distribution and can spend gradient signal on
actual learning rather than on adapting to shifting input statistics.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 4 · Why production decoders didn't adopt it</p>
## Three small things vs no things

NormFormer's three additions are individually small but collectively change the parameter
inventory of every block. For a research team building a new model, the question is whether
the ~24% training speedup justifies the architectural divergence from the Pre-Norm + RMSNorm
consensus that the rest of the open ecosystem assumes.

In practice, the answer has been no: Llama, DeepSeek, Gemma, OLMo, Mistral, Qwen all run pure
Pre-Norm. The cost of being different from the consensus (kernel compatibility, tooling
support, hyperparameter knowledge) exceeded the benefit of a single-recipe training speedup.

NormFormer's three additions are nevertheless interesting evidence that Pre-Norm + RMSNorm is
not Pareto-optimal — there exists a strict improvement on training efficiency. The fact that
nobody ships it is a coordination problem more than a technical one.
