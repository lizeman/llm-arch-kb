<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 1 · Premise</p>
## Why deep transformers need help

A standard Pre-Norm residual transformer block is

$$\mathbf{x}_{\ell+1} = \mathbf{x}_\ell + f_\ell(\mathrm{Norm}(\mathbf{x}_\ell)).$$

At initialization, $f_\ell$ outputs noise of nontrivial magnitude. Stacking $L$ such blocks
multiplies the perturbation by $L$; the residual stream norm grows like $\sqrt{L}$ even before
training begins. Past depth 50–100, this manifests as gradient pathologies that require
LayerNorm, careful initialization, and warmup to suppress.

Bachlechner et al.'s observation: if you could make every block start as the *identity* — its
contribution exactly zero at step 0 — none of this would matter.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 2 · The fix</p>
## One scalar, initialized to zero

Add a learnable scalar $\alpha_\ell \in \mathbb{R}$ per residual branch, initialized to 0:

$$\mathbf{x}_{\ell+1} = \mathbf{x}_\ell + \alpha_\ell \cdot f_\ell(\mathbf{x}_\ell).$$

At $\alpha_\ell = 0$ the block is literally the identity function. Gradient through the block
is exactly 1. Training opens by leaving the residual stream alone; as $\alpha_\ell$ becomes
nonzero, each block gradually pulls its sublayer's contribution into the stream.

Note the second change: **no normalization**. ReZero argues that with $\alpha_\ell$ controlling
the contribution magnitude, the LayerNorm becomes redundant.

```python
class ReZeroBlock(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, sublayer):
        super().__init__()
        self.sublayer = sublayer
        self.alpha = nn.Parameter(torch.zeros(1))  # crucial: init to 0
    def forward(self, x):
        return x + self.alpha * self.sublayer(x)
```

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 3 · Results</p>
## Empirical evidence

The headline experiments (Table 2, Bachlechner et al.):

- **128-layer transformer trains stably from scratch with no warmup, no LayerNorm.** Standard
  Pre-Norm at 128 layers requires careful warmup; Post-Norm at this depth diverges.
- **WikiText-103 perplexity matches LayerNorm-based baselines** at 12 to 64 layers, with 56%
  faster wall-clock convergence (Table 4).
- **ResNets, RNNs, and other residual architectures** all benefit from the same recipe.

The mechanism — start at identity, grow contributions slowly — is mathematically clean enough
to give a precise account of why warmup is sometimes necessary for standard initializations: it
is doing the same work as letting $\alpha_\ell$ grow from 0, just less directly.

<p class="section-eyebrow">§ 4 · Why it isn't standard</p>
## The Pre-Norm + RMSNorm consensus won

ReZero is correct. It is also rarely used at production scale. The reasons:

1. **Pre-Norm + RMSNorm is a known quantity.** The training dynamics, hyperparameter sweet
   spots, and failure modes are charted across hundreds of models. Switching is risk for unclear
   reward at the scales people actually care about.
2. **At "merely deep" sizes** (32–80 layers), warmup + Pre-Norm just works. ReZero's headline
   benefit (no warmup at 128+ layers) is academic.
3. **A single learnable scalar per branch is hard to initialize correctly** when combined with
   modern techniques like FSDP and mixed-precision training. Standard norms are battle-tested
   here; new per-layer scalars are not.

DeepNet (Wang et al. 2022) revisits the same problem with a different solution — scaling each
residual addition by an initialization-derived constant rather than a learnable scalar — and
reportedly does scale to 1000+ layer transformers. The lineage is alive in research.

<Summary>
**ReZero in one breath.** Multiply each residual branch by a learnable scalar α initialized to
zero; drop the LayerNorm. The network starts at the identity function, gradients flow
perfectly, and depth becomes nearly free. Works; not standard at production scale because
Pre-Norm + RMSNorm has decisive incumbent advantages.
</Summary>
